Vintage Story · v1.22.3 · Standard · Inventory & carrying

The Knapsack

On Day One you carry everything in your bare hands — ten hotbar squares and not a stitch more. Every bag you weave, sew or tan after that is space you don't have to walk back for. This is the climb from empty-handed to a fully-loaded pack: the four bag slots that change the game, the reed hand basket that comes first, and the linen sack, backpacks and specialist mining bag that follow.

Inventory progression 4 bag slots +3 → +24 slots Hand basket = day-one

00 — The four bag slots

How Carrying Works

You always have your hotbar — the row of ten squares along the bottom of the screen. That's your entire carrying capacity until you make your first bag. Everything beyond it comes from bags, and bags go in their own special slots.

always

The hotbar

Ten slots, there from the start. It's your hands, your tools, your day-one inventory. With no bags equipped, this is all you can carry.

the key

4 bag slots

Open your inventory (E) and you'll see four slots marked with a basket icon. Each one holds one bag, and each bag adds its slots to your inventory grid.

flexible

Mix & match

The four slots are independent — you can run four hand baskets, four leather backpacks, or any mix. Swap a bag and its contents come with it.

Bags stack — that's the whole point

One bag is nice; four bags is transformative. Because all four slots add their capacity together, your effective inventory is 10 hotbar + (sum of the four equipped bags). Four leather backpacks take you from 10 slots to 34 total. The progression below is really a race to fill those four slots with the best bags you can craft.

A bag is an item, not a building

Bags live on your person. You can pull one out of its slot and drop it in a chest (with its contents intact), hand it to a friend, or — for the hand basket — set it down as a placeable container. But to add its slots to your inventory, it has to sit in one of your four bag slots.

01 — From nothing to a full pack

The Progression

Bags don't unlock on a tech tree — they unlock on materials. Each rung below is gated by something you naturally acquire as you settle in: reeds from a pond, flax from your first field, leather from your first hunt. Climb it in the order your world hands you the parts.

  1. Empty hands — 10 slots. Just the hotbar. You're knapping stone tools and hauling one stack at a time. The goal of this whole guide is to never stay here long.
  2. The Hand Basket — +3 each. Woven from cattail / reed tops found at the edge of nearly any water. The earliest expansion in the game and the one you should rush — even one basket eases the day-one squeeze.
  3. The Linen Sack — +5 each. Sewn from linen once you're growing flax and have knapped your way to cloth. A clean upgrade over the basket; four of them give you 20 carried slots.
  4. The Hunter Backpack — +4 each. An early leather-and-cordage pack — a step up from baskets when you've taken your first animals but aren't drowning in hides yet.
  5. The Leather Backpack — +6 each. The carrying king: the most slots per bag of any general pack. Four of them is the practical end-game of personal capacity — 34 total slots.
  6. The Mining Bag — +12, specialist. A huge twelve slots, but it only holds stone, ores, minerals, metals and mining tools. The deep-cave loadout, not an everyday pack.
You won't replace all four at once

Bags trickle in. A realistic early game is basket → basket → sack → sack, then backpacks replacing baskets one at a time as leather comes in. There's no penalty to a mixed loadout — every slot counts the same whatever bag it came from.

02 — The first rung

The Hand Basket

Your reed basket, and almost certainly the first storage you'll ever make. Cattails and other reeds grow in dense stands at the margins of ponds, lakes and rivers in wetter climates — harvest the tops, and you have basket material before you've even smelted a thing.

+3 slots

What it gives

Three inventory slots per basket. Modest, but it's available on Day One when three slots is a real difference, and you can equip up to four for +12.

reeds

What it costs

Woven from cattail / papyrus tops — a renewable, no-tech material gathered by hand from waterside reed beds.

bonus

Also a container

The hand basket can be set down as a placeable container too — handy as a cheap early chest before you've sawn planks.

Rush the reeds

If your spawn is anywhere near water, detour for reeds before you build. A couple of hand baskets on your back early pays for itself in trips not made back to a base you haven't even built yet.

03 — The cloth upgrade

The Linen Sack

Once you're farming flax and processing it into linen, the sack is a straight upgrade over the basket — five slots against the basket's three, for cloth you'll be making anyway for clothes and sails.

+5 slots

What it gives

Five slots per sack. Four equipped is 20 carried slots — 30 with the hotbar, a comfortable working inventory.

linen

What it costs

Sewn from linen, the cloth you rett and process from harvested flax. A mid-early material, gated only by your first field.

found

Other ways to get one

You don't always have to craft it — sacks turn up in trade, and smashing certain cracked storage vessels in ruins can drop one.

04 — Leather on your back

The Backpacks

Hunting and tanning open the two leather packs. The hunter backpack is the early option; the leather backpack is the best general-purpose bag in the game and what you'll ideally fill all four slots with.

Leather takes time

Tanning hides into usable leather runs through a soak in a barrel and a wait — it isn't instant. Don't sit on baskets and sacks waiting for the "perfect" loadout; equip what you have and swap up to leather backpacks as the tannery delivers.

05 — The specialist

The Mining Bag

The biggest single bag — twelve slots — but with a hard restriction: it only accepts stone, ores, minerals, metals and mining tools. It's not a general pack; it's a deep-dive loadout for when you're hauling a cartload of ore out of a cave.

+12 slots

Enormous, but narrow

Twelve slots is four times a hand basket — but you can't put food, planks or tools-that-aren't-mining-tools in it. The capacity is real only for rock and metal.

swap it in

A loadout, not a fixture

The smart play is to slot mining bags before a dig and swap them back out for backpacks topside. One or two mining bags plus a couple of backpacks is a strong cave kit.

The math on a deep dig

Two mining bags (+24, ore-only) and two leather backpacks (+12, anything) gives you 36 carried + 10 hotbar = 46 slots — with two-thirds of it reserved for the rock you came down for. That's the most efficient way to carry a mine on your back.

The two numbers to remember

20 — four linen sacks, a solid mid-game inventory you can reach early from a single flax field. 24 — four leather backpacks, the practical ceiling for general carrying (34 slots total with the hotbar). Everything past that is specialist mining-bag space.

07 — When your back is full

Beyond Your Back

Bags solve carrying. They don't solve keeping — a backpack does nothing to stop your food rotting, and 34 slots fills fast once you're hauling building stone. The moment you're back at base, things should come off your back and into proper storage.

bulk

Chests

Cheap plank storage for tools, blocks and ore. No spoilage benefit — never your food. The workhorse for everything a bag carried home.

food

Storage vessels

Fired clay; holds big stacks and slows spoilage. Where harvested grain and veg belong once they're off your back.

depth

The cellar

A sealed, dark, cool room multiplies a vessel's preservation. The endpoint of the food chain that started with the bag you carried it home in.

For containers, the food cellar and how preservation actually scores, see The Homestead. For the clothing your hide-and-flax materials also feed into, see Warmth & Wear.
