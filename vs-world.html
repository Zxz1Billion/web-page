The World

00 — Orientation

Overview

The world runs along two axes. Learn to read both.

Climate — the horizontal world

Across the surface, temperature and rainfall set the rules. They decide your biome, what plants and animals appear, and whether crops thrive or freeze. To change your climate, travel.

Geology — the vertical world

Straight down, the world is layered rock over depth. The stone around you decides which ores can appear, what building blocks you get, and how stable reality stays as you descend. To change your geology, dig.

Climate tells you where to live and what grows. Geology tells you where the ore hides. This page covers the surface first, then drills down, and ends with the prospecting pick.

01 — The horizontal axis

Climate & Temperature

Temperature follows a north–south gradient. It gets hotter toward the equator and colder toward the poles. Altitude stacks on top: the higher you climb, the colder it gets, so a mountaintop near the equator can still hold snow. Rainfall varies region to region independently of temperature, from parched to soaking.

A landscape is a function of temperature × rainfall. For warmth and crops, travel toward the equator. For cold, ice and snow, travel poleward or climb. Check your reading on the world map (M) and watch it change as you move.

Read the map before you settle

The same world holds every climate. You just haven't walked to them yet. Put your first base in a temperate, moderate-rainfall band: mild winters, good farmland, plenty of wood and game.

02 — Knowing your region

Landscapes & Biomes

Each landscape offers different resources and dangers. Here are the main ones and what each is good for.

temperate

Forest

The all-rounder. Abundant wood, game (deer, hares), wild berries and forage. Mild winters in the temperate band. Recommended first base.

open

Plains / grassland

Open, flat and sunny. Ideal for farming and grazing herds. Little wood or cover, so build with stone and watch for predators.

scrub

Shrubland

Dry, sparse cover between forest and desert. Scattered bushes and small game. Thin resources, but easy to cross and clear.

hot · arid

Desert & savanna

Heat-loving crops thrive. Little water, little wood. Home of hyenas, fast pack predators. Exposed sand and rock make ore easy to spot.

cold

Tundra & taiga

Snow, ice and a brutal growing season. Cold-hardy crops only. Conifer wood and cold-climate game, but you fight winter year-round.

wetland

Swamp / wetland

Boggy ground yields peat (fuel) and surface bog-iron limonite. Watch the disease risk. Sickness clouds make swamps a place to harvest, not to live.

vertical

Mountains

Exposed rock and ore, steep relief and snow up high (altitude = cold). Great for surface prospecting and stone. Harsh and steep to settle on.

The swamp trade-off

Wetlands are an easy early source of surface limonite and peat, but standing water and disease make a permanent swamp base risky. Mine the resources, then carry them somewhere drier to live.

03 — The clock and the calendar

Seasons & the Calendar

The year is 12 months: three each of spring → summer → autumn → winter. The default month length is 9 days (configurable), so a season is 27 days and a full year 108 days. Day length shifts with season and latitude: long summer days near the poles, short dark winter ones.

Survival rule
Larder + warmth

Winter is the main threat. It gets cold, crops stop growing or die, surface water and wells freeze, and there's little to forage. Survive it on stored food and warmth. A hearth and warm clothing keep your body temperature up while the world outside goes still.

Preparing for your first winter

  1. Plant early, harvest before the frost. Sow in spring and early summer so crops mature with time to spare. Cold weather halts growth and kills tender plants.
  2. Fill a larder. Preserve and store everything you can: dried, salted, cellared. A full larder carries you when nothing grows.
  3. Build cold-storage and shelter. A cellar slows spoilage and a greenhouse extends the growing season. See structures for both.
  4. Secure warmth. Keep a hearth or firepit burning and craft warm clothing before the temperature drops, so cold never chips at your health.
  5. Stockpile fuel & water. Lay in firewood and coal, and store water indoors before sources freeze.
Plan to the calendar

Treat your first autumn as a deadline. Gather and store everything you'll eat and burn through winter before the first frost. See crops and seasons in The Larder.

04 — The vertical axis

The Vertical World

Dig straight down and you find a thin skin of soil over many courses of rock, sinking through the deep toward bedrock. Sea level sits roughly midway up the column. What you find, and how safe you are, changes with every band.

  • Surface soilDIRT · GRASS · TOP

    Dirt and grass over the rock world. Plants, forage, farmland and loose surface stones sit here. Stability is highest at and above the surface.

  • Upper rockJUST BELOW SOIL

    The first rock layers under the topsoil. Shallow ores like copper sit here. Surface metal nuggets hint at veins just below.

  • Sea levelMID-COLUMN MARKER

    The waterline the column is measured around. Treat it as your mental zero. Caves, water and many mid-depth deposits cluster near here.

  • Mid rockBELOW SEA LEVEL

    The middle bands. Iron-bearing ores favour these depths. Stability has begun to slip, so bring light and watch your temporal meter.

  • The deepFAR DOWN

    Deep stone hosts the rarer metals, coal seams and gem pockets. Stability is low and drifters press in. Dangerous ground (see temporal stability).

  • BedrockTHE FLOOR

    The impassable bottom of the world. You cannot dig through it. Hit bedrock and you've reached the deepest the column goes.

Stability drops as you descend

The deeper you go, the lower your temporal stability falls and the more drifters appear. Depth holds the best ore and the most hostile world. Full mechanic in The Rust & The Gear.

05 — Why rock type matters

Geology

A rock's type decides which ores can appear in it and what building stone you get. Stone comes in three families:

  • Sedimentary, laid down in layers: limestone, chalk, sandstone, shale, claystone, conglomerate, chert, plus rock salt (halite).
  • Igneous, cooled from melt: granite, basalt, andesite, peridotite (plus volcanic scoria, tuff, suevite, obsidian). The deepest layer, kimberlite, holds diamonds.
  • Metamorphic, reformed under heat and pressure: slate, marble and phyllite.

Stone stacks top-to-bottom in the order basalt → sedimentary → metamorphic → igneous → kimberlite, so the rock around you hints at how deep you are.

Certain ores favour certain host rocks, so identifying the stone is half of prospecting. It tells you what's even possible before you sample. (Bog-iron limonite is the exception: it forms on the surface in wetlands, not inside a host rock.)

06 — Reading the column

Ore by Depth

Metals sort roughly by depth. A surface metal nugget means a vein of that metal sits somewhere in the rock below. Rough rule of thumb:

Stone tools can't mine ore

A flint or stone pickaxe will not break ore blocks. You need a metal pickaxe. So your first copper has to be smelted into a pick before you can mine anything beyond it. That bootstrap is covered in metalworking and the wider Stone→Steel road.

07 — The key how-to

Prospecting With the Propick

The prospecting pick ("propick") turns digging blind into a directed hunt. Craft one as soon as you can spare the metal. It makes finding ore reliable. It has two modes. Toggle the tool's function with the held-item function key (default F).

Density Search

Break up to three rock blocks within about 16 blocks of each other to take a sample. The propick reports the probability of each ore type in that part of the world, in ‰ (parts per thousand). It tells you what a region holds, not exactly where. The relative-density words, lowest to highest:

Miniscule › Very Poor › Poor › Decent › High › Very High › Ultra High

"Decent" or better means a strong chance the ore generates there. Some ores are capped low by design, so even Miniscule means "present".

Node Search

Break a single block and the game scans a radius (default ~6 blocks) around you, reporting nearby ore nodes with a direction and a relative size. The size words, smallest to largest:

Trace › Small › Medium › Large › Very large › Huge amounts

Anything other than "No ore node nearby" means ore within range, in any direction, including up or down. Use this mode to home in.

The workflow

  1. Survey with Density Search. Sample as you travel to find a region whose readings show the ore you want. Higher ‰ for that ore means a more promising area.
  2. Switch to Node Search (F). In a promising region, toggle the mode and break single blocks for direction-and-size hints on nearby nodes.
  3. Home in. Walk toward the reported direction and re-sample. The size class climbs from Trace toward Huge as you close on the deposit.
  4. Dig it out. On top of a node, mine the ore with a metal pickaxe. Flint and stone won't break it.

A density reading looks like this. The region's ore odds, in ‰:

Density search (3 samples taken): Found the following ore deposits: Copper .......... 8.4 ‰ (decent) Cassiterite ..... 1.1 ‰ (poor) Lignite (coal) .. 14.2 ‰ (high) Iron ............ 0.3 ‰ (very poor — likely just generation-limited) Reading reflects the area around the samples, not an exact location.

Switch to Node Search and a hit reads like this. A direction and a size class to walk toward:

Node search: Found the following ore nodes: Medium amounts of Copper to the north-west Trace amounts of Cassiterite to the east Follow the strongest reading and re-sample as you go.
Density to choose, node to find

Think of it as zoom levels. Density Search picks the region ("is the ore even here?"). Node Search finds the deposit ("which way and how big?"). Use them in that order to stop tunneling on hope. Don't dismiss a low ‰ reading. Some ores are capped low by design, and a small number is still a green light.

With a region surveyed and a node located, turn that ore into tools and a better pick. Pick up the road in metalworking and The Long Road.
