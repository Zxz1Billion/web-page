Beams
& Braziers

A short, sharp field guide to Decorative Blocks — a tight, lovingly-made set of structural and cozy pieces that fill the gaps vanilla leaves in medieval, rustic, and cottage builds. Timber beams and support posts for convincing framing, palisades and iron bars for fences and cages, seats you can actually sit on, braziers and chandeliers for warm light, thatch and canopies for roofs, and rope ladders to climb. No tech tree, no grind — just better blocks.

Decorative Blocks MC 1.19.2 · Forge Builder-focused by stohun

00 — Orientation

Start Here

Decorative Blocks doesn't change how you play — it changes what you can build. It's a curated, high-quality set of structural and decorative pieces designed to slot straight into vanilla's palette, with an unmistakable medieval / cozy-cottage flavour. There's no machinery and almost nothing to "unlock": you craft these from wood, stone, iron, and string at a normal crafting table, then place them like any block.

structure

Framing

Beams, Support Beams, Palisades, Lattice, and Iron Bar panels for timber frames and cages.

comfort

Furniture

Sittable Seats, Stools, Bar Stools, and Bar counter blocks for believable interiors.

light

Hearth

The Brazier, Chandelier, and Candle Holders — warm, point-source lighting.

shell

Cover

Canopy/Awning, Thatch, and rod/pole pieces for roofs and overhangs, plus Rope Ladders.

How to read this guide

Block names, exact recipes, and light levels vary a little across mod versions, so treat the specifics here as the shape of things and let JEI/EMI and the in-game tooltips be the ground truth in your pack. Everything in Decorative Blocks is craftable from common materials and works in survival from day one.

Part of the building hub

Decorative Blocks is one of several "more building blocks" mods you'll likely run together. See the Building & Decoration hub for how it pairs with Supplementaries (functional clutter and signage), Chipped (huge texture-variant palettes), and Macaw's furniture/roofs/bridges. Each covers a different gap; together they cover almost everything.

The shape of a build

  1. Frame it. Lay Beams and Support Beams over a plaster/wattle wall to read as exposed timber framing (§01).
  2. Furnish it. Drop in Seats, Stools, and a Bar counter so the space is actually liveable (§02).
  3. Light it. A Brazier or Chandelier gives warm, medieval light instead of a floating torch (§03).
  4. Cover it. Roof with Thatch and shade openings with a Canopy (§04).
  5. Connect it. Run a Rope Ladder up to the loft and you're done (§05).

01 — The bones

Structure & Framing

The structural pieces are the heart of the mod. They turn a flat plaster box into a convincing half-timbered building, and they cage, fence, and divide space in ways vanilla fences and walls never quite manage.

Structural & framing blocks
BlockWhat it isBest used for
BeamA thin wooden timber that orients to the three axes (like a vanilla log/pillar).Exposed framing on walls, ceiling joists, decorative trusses.
Support BeamA standing post with a connecting head that links to beams & adjacent supports.Vertical columns, mine-shaft props, pergola legs, market stalls.
PalisadeA tall, spiked wooden stake fence — taller and pointier than a vanilla fence.Stockades, war camps, dungeon pens, defensive walls.
Lattice / TrellisA flat criss-cross wooden panel.Garden trellis, window grilles, climbing-plant support, screens.
Bar Panel (Iron Bars)Decorative metal bar panels beyond vanilla iron bars.Cell doors, cages, gates, grates, industrial windows.

Building convincing timber frames

Half-timbering is the signature look. The trick is to treat Beams as the dark wood "skeleton" laid over a light "infill" wall (white concrete/terracotta, or a wattle-and-daub style block, §04). Run beams along the top and bottom of each storey, drop a few verticals, and add a diagonal brace or two — your eye fills in the rest.

  • Mind the axis. Beams snap to the face you place them against, so place onto the wall to get a vertical, onto the floor/ceiling to get a horizontal. Sneak-place if you need precision.
  • Support Beams for load. Use them wherever a frame should look like it's actually holding weight — porch posts, loft supports, bridge piers. Their heads connect to neighbouring beams for clean joints.
  • Stack them tall. A column of Support Beams reads as a single continuous post, perfect for tall halls and barns.

Frames & cages

For pens, prisons, and animal cages, Palisades give you a spiky timber stockade with real height, while Bar Panels give you the metal-grate look for cell doors and dungeon windows. Mix the two — a palisade outer ring with barred inner cells — for a layered, lived-in fortress.

Lattice does double duty

Lattice panels are great as garden trellis and window grilles, but they also make convincing rustic railings and balustrades on balconies and stair landings where a full fence would feel chunky.

02 — Sit down a while

Seating & Furniture

One of the mod's most-loved features: seats you can actually sit on. Right-click a Seat and your character plops down onto it — perfect for taverns, dining halls, council chambers, and screenshots.

Furniture you can use
BlockSittable?Role
Seat / ChairYes — right-clickThe headline piece. Dining chairs, thrones, benches.
StoolYes — right-clickBackless seating; cottages, workshops, fireside.
Bar StoolYes — right-clickTall seat to pair with the Bar counter.
Bar / Counter—A counter block at sitting height — the tavern bar or kitchen island.
Table piece—Surface to gather furniture around (often paired with the Bar/counter pieces).

Sitting, in practice

  • Right-click to sit. Approach a Seat or Stool and right-click the top — you'll mount it like a minecart. Sneak or jump to stand back up.
  • Leave headroom. Sitting needs clear space; don't tuck a seat directly under a slab or low ceiling or you may not be able to mount it (see §07).
  • Dye it. Many seat variants exist per wood type (and some take colour/cushions), so you can match the chair to the table and the room.

Cozy interiors

The furniture set is what turns a Decorative Blocks house into a Decorative Blocks home. A Bar counter lined with Bar Stools is an instant tavern; a ring of Seats around a table is a dining hall; a single Stool by the hearth makes a workshop feel inhabited. Combine with Supplementaries' jars, mugs, and shelves (§06) for clutter that sells the scene.

Benches from seats

Place several Seats in a row with matching orientation to read as a long bench — handy for great halls, churches, and mead benches where individual chairs would look fussy.

03 — Keeping warm

Light & Hearth

Floating torches break immersion. Decorative Blocks gives you proper medieval light sources — a fire bowl, a hanging chandelier, and candle holders — that look the part while still lighting the room and keeping mobs out.

Light sources & the hearth
BlockLightNotes
BrazierBright (full block-level light when lit)An iron fire-bowl on legs. Lights with flint & steel; can ignite/cook things above it — and can set you on fire.
Soul BrazierDimmer blue flameSoul-fire variant for eerie blue lighting; same cautions.
ChandelierWarm, mid-levelHangs under a ceiling/beam; centrepiece for halls and dining rooms.
Candle HolderSoft, low-levelWall- or surface-mounted accent light for tables, sconces, and shelves.

Warm lighting that fits the build

The Brazier is the workhorse: place it in a courtyard, on a wall ledge, or atop a Support Beam post and light it with flint & steel for a strong, flickering fire that doubles as a campfire-style light. The Chandelier wants a ceiling or a beam to hang from and instantly anchors a room. Candle Holders are the finishing touch — line a dining table or punctuate a corridor.

The brazier is also a hazard

A lit Brazier is real fire — walk into it and you'll take burn damage, and flammable blocks placed too close can catch. That's a feature for traps and atmosphere, but mind where you put it indoors. Some braziers can also cook or burn items dropped onto them, campfire-style — handy, but keep your hotbar items clear of an open flame.

Light without the fire risk

If you want medieval ambience without the burn hazard near wooden furniture, lean on Chandeliers and Candle Holders for interior light and save open Braziers for stone courtyards, gateposts, and outdoor scenes.

04 — Over your head

Roofing & Cover

Vanilla has stairs and slabs but no real roofing texture and no cloth awnings. Decorative Blocks fills both gaps: thatch for rustic roofs, canopies for market stalls and shopfronts, and wattle-and-daub-style infill for the walls underneath your timber frame.

Roofing & cover pieces
BlockWhat it isBest used for
ThatchStraw/reed roofing block (often with stair & slab variants).Cottage & farmhouse roofs, hayloft tops, rustic huts.
Canopy / AwningA cloth overhang you mount above a window or door.Market stalls, shopfronts, porches, balcony shade.
Rod / PoleThin support rods/poles for hanging & framing.Awning supports, banners, tent poles, hanging chains.
Wattle & Daub style infillPlaster/woven-wall blocks for between the timbers.The light wall surface behind exposed Beams.

Thatch roofs

Thatch reads beautifully on steep cottage roofs. Build the roof shape with thatch stairs and slabs to get clean eaves and ridges, then cap the top edge with full blocks. The straw texture pairs naturally with timber-framed walls and gives that warm, lived-in farmhouse silhouette.

Canopies & overhangs

The Canopy/Awning is what makes a medieval market come alive — mount a row of them over shop windows in striped colours and you've got a bazaar. They also work as porch covers, balcony shades, and tent flaps. Pair with Rod/Pole pieces for the supports so the cloth looks like it's actually hung.

Build the wall first

For half-timbering, lay your wattle-and-daub infill wall first, then apply the Beams (§01) on top as the visible frame, and finish with a Thatch roof above. That order keeps the timber sitting proud of the plaster the way real framing does.

05 — Up and down

Ladders & Access

Vertical movement is the unglamorous part of building, and vanilla's single ladder gets old fast. Decorative Blocks adds rope ladders and additional ladder variants that look right in rustic and nautical builds.

Climbing & access
BlockWhat it isBest used for
Rope LadderA hanging rope-and-rung ladder, made from string + sticks.Haylofts, ship rigging, treehouses, well shafts, towers.
Ladder variantsAdditional wood-type / styled ladders beyond vanilla.Matching the ladder to the wood palette of a build.

Rope ladders, in practice

  • They hang down. A Rope Ladder reads as suspended from above, so it's ideal where a stiff wooden ladder would look wrong — barn lofts, ship rigging, cliff descents.
  • Climb like vanilla. Treat them like ordinary ladders for movement: stand against them and hold forward to climb, sneak to hold position.
  • Match the wood. The extra ladder variants let you keep a single wood palette across a build instead of breaking it with vanilla oak ladders.
Loft access without the scaffold look

For a cottage with a sleeping loft, a Rope Ladder up to the loft hatch keeps the floor clear and looks far cozier than a boxed-in staircase. Pair it with a Lattice (§01) railing around the loft edge.

06 — Putting it together

Build Ideas

The mod really sings when you combine pieces. Here are three quick recipes for scenes that use Decorative Blocks across all five categories — and where pairing with other building mods earns its keep.

A medieval tavern

  1. Frame the hall. Plaster walls with exposed Beams; Support Beams as porch posts and roof trusses.
  2. Build the bar. A run of Bar counter blocks with Bar Stools down the front; Seats around tables in the room.
  3. Light it. A Chandelier over the main table, Candle Holders on the bar, and a Brazier out in the courtyard.
  4. Dress it. Add Supplementaries mugs, jars, and a hanging sign; Chipped wood variants for worn floorboards.

A cottage roof

  1. Roof in thatch. Build a steep roof with Thatch stairs and slabs, capped with full thatch at the ridge.
  2. Shade the door. A small Canopy over the entrance on Rod/Pole supports.
  3. Climb the loft. A Rope Ladder up to a loft railed with Lattice.

A dungeon cage

  1. Wall the pit. A ring of Palisades for the spiked outer stockade.
  2. Bar the cells. Bar Panels for cell doors and grated windows.
  3. Light it grimly. A Soul Brazier for cold blue light, or a regular Brazier as a hazard guests would rather avoid.
Pairing with other mods

Supplementaries adds the functional clutter Decorative Blocks deliberately leaves out — signs, jars, shelves, lanterns, faucets — so the two slot together cleanly. Chipped multiplies your texture palette, giving worn, mossy, and carved variants of the same wood and stone so your beams, floors, and walls don't all read identical. Run all three and you have a near-complete medieval building kit. See the Building & Decoration hub for the full pairing list.

  • Tavern: Beams + Bar + Stools + Chandelier
  • Cottage: Thatch + Canopy + Rope Ladder
  • Dungeon: Palisades + Bars + Soul Brazier
  • Pairs with: Supplementaries · Chipped

07 — Field notes

Tips & Gotchas

Small habits that make the mod easier to live with. Nothing here is a deep mechanic — just the practical edges you'll bump into.

  • Beams snap to orientation. Like logs, a Beam takes its axis from the face you place it against. Place onto a wall for a vertical, onto floor/ceiling for a horizontal; sneak-place for control near other blocks. orientation
  • Seats need clear space above. Sitting is a mounted animation, so a Seat tucked under a slab or low ceiling may refuse to seat you. Leave headroom over chairs and stools.
  • Braziers are light and hazard. A lit Brazier brightens the room but also burns players and nearby flammables. Use it for atmosphere and traps; keep it away from wooden furniture indoors. fire
  • Light with flint & steel. Braziers generally need lighting (and can be extinguished). If a Brazier isn't glowing, it probably isn't lit yet.
  • Match wood types. Most pieces come per wood species — pick one palette per build so beams, seats, and ladders agree.
  • Keep a decor chest. These blocks are cheap but numerous. A dedicated chest (or shulker) of beams, seats, thatch, and braziers near your build saves endless trips to the crafting table.
  • JEI/EMI is your recipe book. Recipes are plain crafting-table affairs; if you're unsure what makes a given piece, look it up in JEI/EMI rather than guessing.
Don't burn your own build down

The single most common mistake is placing a lit Brazier next to thatch, beams, or wooden furniture. Open flame plus a thatch roof is exactly as flammable as it sounds. For interiors, prefer Chandeliers and Candle Holders; reserve open braziers for stone surroundings.

★ — The path

Progression — dressing a build with Decorative Blocks

Decorative Blocks has no tech tree, so its "progression" is a workflow: a repeatable order of operations for taking a build from a rough box to a finished, lived-in scene. Work outside-in and structure-first, and each pass makes the next one easier to read. The sequence below is the same whether you're raising a medieval tavern, a cottage, or a dungeon cage — only the palette changes.

  1. Rough the structure. Block out the footprint, walls, and roof line in plain blocks first — wattle-and-daub infill (§04) for walls, a stone or wood shell for a dungeon. Get the silhouette right before you decorate anything.
  2. Frame the timber. Lay Beams over the infill as exposed half-timbering and stand Support Beams as porch posts, roof trusses, and load-bearing columns (§01). This is the pass that turns a box into a building.
  3. Furnish the interior. Drop in Seats and Stools around tables, line a Bar counter with Bar Stools (§02). A tavern's bar, a cottage's fireside stool, a dungeon's lone bench all land here.
  4. Light the room. Hang a Chandelier over the main table, dot Candle Holders along the walls, and set a Brazier (or a Soul Brazier for a dungeon's cold blue glow) out where open flame is safe (§03).
  5. Roof and cover. Cap the shell with a Thatch roof and shade doors and windows with a Canopy on rod supports (§04) — the pass that makes a cottage read as a cottage.
  6. Finish the edges. Ring the lot with Palisades, rail balconies and lofts with Lattice, bar dungeon cells with Bar Panels, and run a Rope Ladder up to the loft (§01, §05). These details are what sell the scene.
Where this fits

This dressing pass is one stage of a larger building habit. See the Building hub for how Decorative Blocks slots alongside its neighbours, lean on Supplementaries for the functional clutter that finishes an interior, and follow The Ascent for where building sits in the wider progression.
