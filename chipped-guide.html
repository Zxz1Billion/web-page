The Palette
Engine

Chipped is the quiet superpower of a build-focused modpack: it doesn't add new materials, it multiplies the ones you already have. Feed a single vanilla block into a themed workbench and a grid of hundreds of alternate textures and shapes opens up — bevelled stone, mossy bricks, herringbone planks, frosted glass, tufted wool. This guide covers every bench, the one-click chipping workflow, and how to wield that palette without your build turning into a swatch book.

00 — Orientation

Start Here

Chipped is purely cosmetic, but it is transformative. There is no new ore, no tech tree, no power requirement — you pick a base vanilla block, browse a huge palette of alternate textures and shapes, and walk away with a variant that has the exact same hardness and behaviour. The "progression" is entirely in your eye for detail.

If most decorative mods hand you a fixed set of new blocks, Chipped does the opposite: it takes blocks you already mine — stone, sandstone, planks, wool, glass, terracotta — and explodes each one into a wall of variations. A single block of Stone might offer well over a hundred faces: tiled, cracked, runed, cobbled, panelled, polished into a dozen geometric cuts. You're not unlocking materials; you're unlocking finishes.

no materials

Bring your own blocks

Every variant is made from a vanilla base you already have. No grinding, no new ingredients — just the block and a bench.

1:1

Free conversions

Chipping is a one-for-one swap. You never lose a block, and you can chip a variant straight back to plain.

cosmetic

Same physics

A chipped block keeps the base's hardness, blast resistance, tool, and behaviour. It only looks different.

scale

Hundreds of looks

Across all bases, Chipped adds many hundreds of variants — enough that two builds need never share a texture.

Where it sits in the pack

Chipped is a cornerstone of the Building & Decoration hub — it's the texture layer underneath everything else. Pair it with shape-and-prop mods (Supplementaries, Decorative Blocks, Macaw's sets) for trim and furniture, and with Building Gadgets to place the result fast.

The whole loop in one breath

  1. Craft a bench. Each themed workbench unlocks one family of variants (stone, wood, glass, wool…).
  2. Insert the base block. Drop in plain Stone, Oak Planks, White Wool — whatever the bench accepts.
  3. Browse the grid. The bench shows every variant for that block at once. Hover, compare, decide.
  4. Take the variant. Pull out as many as you fed in — it's a 1:1 trade, fully reversible.
  5. Build, then re-chip if you change your mind. Nothing is permanent.

02 — The mechanic

How It Works

The chipping interaction is deliberately frictionless. There's no fuel, no timer, no recipe to memorise — the bench is a live catalogue of everything a block can become.

  1. Place the matching workbench for the material you want to restyle (Mason for stone, Carpenter for wood, and so on).
  2. Insert the base block. Open the bench and drop the plain vanilla block into the input — the grid populates instantly with every variant available for it.
  3. Browse the variant grid. Scroll the wall of alternates; hover to see names. Many bases have dozens to a hundred-plus options.
  4. Take the variant. Click the look you want and pull out the result. You get back the same count you put in.
1:1 and reversible

Conversions are one-for-one: one base block in, one variant block out, with no waste and no cost. Better still, it's reversible — feed a chipped variant back in and you can chip it to a different variant or all the way back to the plain base. Nothing you do is destructive, so experiment freely.

Because every variant inherits its base, you can also chip in bulk: insert a full stack and convert all 64 at once, then hover the next look and convert again. The bench remembers the input you fed it, so iterating through finishes for a wall is a matter of a few clicks rather than a fresh craft each time.

It's a finish, not a new block

A chipped variant is functionally identical to its base in every way the game cares about — mining level, hardness, blast resistance, redstone behaviour, the lot. That's the point: you can swap finishes mid-build without rebalancing anything. It also means a variant won't suddenly become a different kind of block; "Stone" stays stone.

03 — Designing with it

Palette Strategy

Hundreds of variants is a gift and a trap. The skill in Chipped isn't finding a nice texture — it's choosing a small, cohesive set and using them with discipline. Great Chipped builds usually lean on a family of related finishes plus a couple of accents, not a confetti of everything.

Pick a family, not a favourite

Before you place a single block, browse one base's grid end-to-end and notice the groups: the polished cuts, the cracked/weathered set, the tiled/panelled set, the runed/ornate set. A coherent build draws its main mass from one such group, so the wall reads as one material seen from different angles rather than a patchwork.

mass

One base, one family

Pick a single base block and 2–4 variants from the same visual family for the bulk of a structure. Repetition reads as intent.

rhythm

Mix shapes, not chaos

Vary the shape of a finish — smooth, panelled, bevelled — to add surface rhythm while keeping the colour and material constant.

accent

Reserve loud variants

Save the ornate, runed, or high-contrast variants for trim, frames and focal points. A little goes a long way.

layer

Let other mods do shape

Chipped restyles full cubes. Reach for Supplementaries / Decorative Blocks for slabs, beams, posts and props in matching tones.

Mixing shapes within a family

A common move is to take one Chipped family and rotate between two or three of its members across a surface — a smooth field, a panelled border, a bevelled course at eye level. Because they share colour and material, the eye reads texture and depth without registering "different blocks." This is how you get walls that look hand-detailed instead of flat.

Playing well with vanilla and other mods

  • Vanilla as the anchor. Keep some plain vanilla blocks in the mix — they ground the busier Chipped finishes and stop a build from feeling over-decorated.
  • Supplementaries & Decorative Blocks add the shapes Chipped doesn't: timber frames, brick pillars, hedges, lanterns, sconces. Chip the base to taste, then trim with these.
  • Match tones across mods. When you pick a Chipped terracotta or wood finish, choose other mods' props in the nearest matching hue so the whole scene shares a palette.
The three-tone rule of thumb

For most builds, lock in one dominant material (the mass), one supporting material (floors/roofs), and one accent (trim/detail). Chip each into a small family. Three coherent tones beats thirty clashing ones every time.

05 — Placing it fast

Workflow with Building Gadgets

Chipped solves what a wall looks like; it doesn't speed up placing the wall. That's where the Gadgets come in. The natural pipeline is: design your palette at the bench, stock it, then build at speed with the Building Gadgets — see the dedicated Building Gadgets section of the Building & Decoration hub for the full toolset.

  1. Chip the palette first. At the relevant bench, convert a generous supply of each variant you've chosen — a few stacks per finish so you don't run dry mid-build.
  2. Load the Building Gadget. Set the variant block as its build material and lay down large surfaces, walls and floors in one swing instead of block by block.
  3. Refine with the Exchanging Gadget. Changed your mind on a finish after the fact? The Exchanging Gadget swaps an already-placed block for another in bulk — perfect for trading one chipped variant for a different one across a whole face.
  4. Re-chip leftovers. Anything you over-produced goes straight back to base (or another variant) at the bench. No waste.
Chip before you place, not after

The Building/Exchanging Gadgets copy and place specific blocks. Decide the variant at the bench first, then build — it's far smoother than placing plain blocks and trying to restyle them in the world. If you do want to restyle in place, the Exchanging Gadget is the bulk tool for it.

06 — Field notes

Tips

A handful of habits make Chipped painless across a long build session.

  • Keep a "palette chest." Dedicate one chest (or a labelled row) to the chosen variants for the current build. With the family decided up front, you grab finishes by hand instead of re-chipping every time.
  • Label your benches. If you keep several benches standing, sign or name them by category so you're not opening the Glassblower when you wanted the Mason.
  • Remember it's all cosmetic. Every variant has the same hardness, tool, and behaviour as its base — so you can swap finishes freely without affecting how the block mines, explodes, or interacts.
  • Browse before you build. Open the grid and scroll the whole thing once. The best finish is often three rows down, not the first one that looks nice.
  • Lean on JEI. Search "chipped" to find which bench makes a variant, or to confirm a base is supported, before you go crafting a bench you don't need.
  • Re-chip, don't re-mine. Made too many of one finish? Chip the surplus back to base or sideways into the variant you actually want — never break and recraft.
The one thing to internalise

Chipped costs you nothing but attention. There's no resource sink and no risk — the only way to "waste" it is to over-decorate. Choose a tight palette, stock it in a palette chest, place it with the Gadgets, and re-chip anything you don't love. cosmetic only
