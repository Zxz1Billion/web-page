Vintage Story · v1.22.3 · Decoration & style

The Architect

Vintage Story rewards builders. Every block keeps its real material texture, the chisel carves any block into trim and furniture, and the world hands you dozens of natural stones, clays, woods and metals to combine. This page takes you from a survival cube to a finished build, from a Day-Three cob hut to a stone manor, with full material recipes, six styles, and a step-by-step cottage you can copy.

00 — Principles

What Makes a Build Beautiful

Good builds come from four levers you can pull with materials you already have, not from rare blocks. Use these and even a cob hut looks intentional.

1 · texture

Material variety

Flat single-material walls read as "survival box." Mix two or three materials: stone base, cob or plaster body, timber accents. The eye then reads craft.

2 · depth

Break the flat plane

Push and pull the surface: a stone plinth at the base, beams standing proud, window lintels & sills, roof overhangs. The shadow lines make it look real.

3 · palette

A limited palette

Pick a small set of materials and repeat them. Three woods and two stones beats fifteen random blocks. Tie the whole build together with one accent.

4 · light

Warm, placed light

Lanterns, candles and a hearth at human height beat a ceiling of torches. Warm pools of light sell coziness and double as drifter defence.

Scale rule of thumb

Survival cubes feel cramped because they're too small. Aim for doorways ~1×2, ceilings 3–4 blocks high, and rooms at least 5×5. Extra air makes everything look deliberate.

01 — The key tool

The Chisel

The chisel is the key tool for good-looking builds. Crafted from metal, it carves any solid block into microblocks. Each full block is a 16×16×16 grid of voxels you can remove one chunk at a time, and the carved result keeps the original block's texture. Use it to make trim, beams, arches, railings, slabs at any thickness, and furniture out of ordinary materials.

How chiseling works

  1. Hold the chisel and a hammer. Chiseling needs the chisel in the main hand and a hammer in the off hand — both at once. Look at a placed block and a 16×16×16 grid overlay appears; the block becomes an editable "chiseled block" sculpted in place.
  2. Left-click removes a voxel chunk, right-click adds one back. The carving is fully reversible within the block's material set — over-cut and you can replace it.
  3. Press F for the tool-mode menu. It holds the grid size (the bite per click — rough-shape big, detail small), rotate (left = clockwise, right = counter-clockwise), flip (mirror horizontally relative to your facing), and set name.
  4. Combine materials. A single chiseled block can hold more than one material: pick the block up, drop it onto the "+" slot to register a second material, then place voxels of either. (You can also merge two chiseled blocks in the grid — the lower block's material transfers up, but its carving is lost.)
Copying: creative only

In survival, chiseled blocks cannot be copied — each is carved by hand, so design a shape once and reproduce it deliberately. In creative, middle-click copies a finished chiseled block, which is the fast way to prototype a palette before you commit in survival.

What to chisel first

Start with the highest-value chisels: corner posts & beams (thinner than a full block), window sills & lintels, roof trim, half-thickness walls, and simple furniture (a table is a chiseled plank on legs). Don't micro-detail everything — every chiseled block is its own little entity and is heavier to render than a plain block, so a few sharp accents beat a wall of fiddly carving. A handful of chiseled details lifts an entire build.

Five reliable chisel techniques

  • The slab at any thickness. Shave a full block down to a 1-, 2- or 4-voxel sheet for floors, shelves, table tops and panelling thinner than the game's stock slab.
  • The chamfer. Knock the top voxels off one edge of a beam or post for a soft bevelled corner — instantly reads as worked timber rather than a cube.
  • The recessed panel. Sink the centre voxels of a wall block a step deep to fake a framed panel or a door inset; pair with an inlaid second material for trim.
  • The wedge / slope. A diagonal cut makes a roof slope or brace without a dedicated stair block, and matches any material you like.
  • Round it. Step the corners of four blocks inward in small bites to suggest a column, an arch or a barrel.

Colour your plaster

Lime plaster drives mid-game charm. Whitewash gives a clean render, and adding pigments (from plants, ochre, charcoal) tints it. White plaster between dark timber beams is the classic fachwerk look (see §04).

Where the materials come from

A palette is only useful if you know the cost of each block. The decorative materials trace back to a few simple chains:

Two common myths

Glass is from quartz, not sand — sand goes into plaster and mortar. And marble can't be bricked; you polish it. Knowing this saves you mining the wrong rock for a manor wall.

03 — The make-or-break

Roofs

The roof makes or breaks a build. A flat slab roof reads as unfinished. Give it a pitch, an overhang and texture. The plan below is a hip roof: it steps inward as it rises. Build it with stairs/slabs (or chiseled wedges) on the slopes.

The roof pieces the game gives you

Vintage Story ships dedicated roofing blocks in six materials — sod, thatch, bamboo, clay, slate and copper — each in a set of slope shapes: slanted roofing, roof ridge, roof tip, and inner / outer corners. Clay roofing adds full blocks, slabs and stairs. Between these, stock stairs/slabs, and chiseled wedges, you can build any roofline.

Roofing materials & how they're made

Clay (shingle)Fired shingles; 4 colours (blue, brown, red, light)Tidy, medieval, the mid-game default

Roof shapes are techniques, not blocks

Gable, hip, gambrel and mansard are real-world roof forms you build from the pieces above — the game has no "gable block." Knowing the silhouettes lets you pick one on purpose:

  • Gable. Two slopes meeting at a ridge, leaving a triangular wall (the gable end) at each end. The simplest pitched roof; great for cottages and inns.
  • Hip. All four sides slope inward to the ridge or a point — no gable wall. Calmer, more solid; the schematic above is a hip. Lay it with the inner/outer corner pieces.
  • Gambrel. A two-pitch slope per side (shallow up top, steep below) — the "barn" roof. Buys headroom in a loft.
  • Mansard. A four-sided gambrel; very steep lower slopes, almost-flat top. Reads grand and townhouse-like for a manor.

Roofing rules

  • Always overhang the eaves by at least one block past the wall. The shadow it casts is half of why pitched roofs look good.
  • Use slanted roofing, stairs, or chiseled wedges for the slope, not vertical steps. Cap the top with a ridge line and the corners with the corner pieces.
  • Mix two shingle tones (e.g. dark + rust clay shingles) for a weathered, hand-laid texture.
  • Pitch matters: steeper roofs read as cold-climate/medieval, shallow roofs as warm/rustic. Match it to your biome.
  • Slope tricks: a 1:1 (45°) slope is easy but blunt; mix a 2:1 lower course with a 1:1 upper for a subtle bell-curve, or break a long run with a small dormer to kill the monotony.

04 — Beating the flat wall

Walls With Depth

The biggest single upgrade to any build is breaking up flat walls. The classic technique is timber framing (fachwerk): a grid of dark beams with plaster infill, on a stone base course. Below is a front elevation. Note it's a face-on view, not a top-down plan.

Techniques, cheapest to fanciest

  • Base course / plinth: the bottom 1–2 rows in stone, the rest in cob/plaster/brick. Instant "grounded" look.
  • Corner posts & a top plate: chiseled timber at every corner and along the wall top frames the whole face.
  • Lintels & sills: a timber or stone beam over and under every window and door.
  • Timber framing: vertical studs + a diagonal brace per panel, plaster between. The best mid-game wall.
  • String courses & offsets: a protruding row of brick or a half-block step partway up adds a shadow line on tall walls.
  • Wattle & daub: apply daub (soil + sand + dry grass + clay, ~6–8 pieces per block, dyeable) over wattle fencing for a rustic infill between heavier timbers.

Give the wall real depth

The elevation above is one block thick — fine, but the best walls vary their depth. A few ways to do it without doubling your material bill:

  • Two-block walls where it counts. Thicken the ground floor or a feature wall to two blocks, then chisel the inner face back to reveal a deep window reveal and a sturdy plinth. Thick cob and stone also insulate, so depth doubles as winter warmth (see The Homestead).
  • Proud beams. Set the timber frame standing one or two voxels out from the plaster with the chisel. The shadow line between beam and infill is the whole fachwerk effect.
  • Recessed panels. Sink the plaster infill a step behind the frame instead. Either direction reads as built rather than painted-on.
  • A jettied upper storey. Overhang the first floor a half-block past the ground floor on a row of chiseled corbels — the classic medieval town look.

05 — Openings & light

Windows, Doors & Light

windows

Glazing

Plain glass early, leaded/coloured glass later. Frame each window with a chiseled timber or stone surround and add shutters. Even fake ones add depth.

doors

Doorways

Recess the door a half-block, add a lintel above and a stone threshold below. A small porch roof or two posts make an entrance feel like one.

light

Warm light

Oil lamps & lanterns on walls, candles on tables, a chandelier over a hall, and a hearth as the heart of the room. Lanterns, candles and chandeliers never burn out or get doused by rain.

fire

Hearth & chimney

A built fireplace with a stone chimney breaking the roofline lights, warms, and anchors the silhouette.

Don't ceiling-spam torches. A few warm sources low on the walls and tables light a room better than a grid above. The brightest craftable light is a lined lantern (level 18); the level-24 chandelier only comes from ruins or traders, so treat it as a found centrepiece, not a recipe. A sealed, lit interior also keeps drifters out (see temporal).

06 — The inside

Interiors & Furniture

An empty box never looks finished. Storage doubles as decoration: barrels, crocks, shelves and cabinets (voxel-placed in 1.22) all work as set dressing. Here's a single-room layout, top-down.

  • Anchor the room with a hearth on one wall and a rug in the centre.
  • Furniture from chisels: a table is a chiseled plank on legs; benches, stools and shelves are quick carves.
  • Use your storage as decor: a row of labelled crocks, stacked barrels, a cabinet wall and open shelves of food read as a real pantry.
  • Clutter, tastefully: a few tools on the wall, a planter by the window, a painting, books. The "lived-in" details.

Furniture from the chisel

Most furniture is just chiseled blocks. A few patterns cover a whole room:

  • Table: a thin chiseled plank top on four corner legs carved from a second block — or a single block with the underside hollowed.
  • Bench / stool: a 4-voxel-tall seat with thin legs; line three along a table.
  • Shelving: stacked thin slabs with a back panel; fill with real crocks, bowls and books so it reads as storage.
  • Counter & cabinets: a waist-high run of chiseled blocks with the new cabinets (voxel-placed in 1.22) set into it for a kitchen wall.
  • Four-poster bed: a real bed flanked by four chiseled posts and a thin canopy — instant manor bedroom.

08 — Full build, step by step

Full Build: The Timber-Frame Cottage

A complete, copyable build that uses everything above: a stone base course, white-plaster-and-beam walls, glass windows, a log-cornered frame and an overhanging shingle roof. Step through the layers, then follow the build order.

  1. Foundation (Y0). Lay a solid cobblestone / stone-brick footprint. This is your base course and it lifts the whole build off the dirt.
  2. Frame & door (Y1). Raise cob or plaster walls with a log/chiseled-timber post at each corner, leaving a 1-wide door in the south wall.
  3. Windows (Y2). Continue the walls and cut glass windows, two on each long side, framed by the corner posts. Add sills below them.
  4. Top plate (Y3). Cap the walls with a ring of beam (the timber top plate). It ties the frame together and gives the roof something to sit on.
  5. Eaves (Y4). Lay the first roof course as a full thatch/shingle layer that overhangs the walls by a block all round.
  6. Ridge (Y5). Step the roof inward and run a shingle ridge along the top. Use stairs/chiseled wedges on the slope between the eaves and the ridge.
  7. Dress it. Hang a lantern by the door, add a hearth & chimney, a window planter, and a stone path to the threshold. Done.
Make it yours

The plan is a 7×6 starter. Scale it up, add a second storey on the same footprint, or bolt on a lean-to in the same palette. The proportions and the layering are the point, not the exact size.

09 — The last 10%

Finishing & Landscaping

The details around a build matter as much as the build. Once the shell is up:

  • Ground it: a stone or gravel path to the door, a low fence or hedge, and a few planters or a kitchen garden.
  • Light the exterior: a lantern either side of the door, path lights, a glowing window at dusk.
  • Add age & asymmetry: mossy/weathered variants, a slightly off-centre chimney, a lean-to that breaks the symmetry. Perfection looks fake.
  • Features: a well, a wood store, a drying rack, a bench under a tree. The marks of a lived-in homestead.
  • Terrain: nestle the build into the land, terrace a slope with retaining walls, don't float it on a flat platform.

Paths & landscaping, in detail

  • Paths read by edge, not fill. A path of gravel, cobble or packed dirt looks best when its edge wanders a voxel or two — chisel the border blocks irregular rather than ruler-straight. Sink the path a step below the lawn so it reads as worn-in.
  • Terrace slopes, don't flatten them. Hold a hillside with low retaining walls of stone and step the garden up in beds. A build cut into the land always beats one on a flat platform.
  • Mix ground textures. Grass into gravel into a few flagstones into soil for a planting bed — three or four ground materials make a yard look tended.
  • Frame the approach. A low fence or hedge either side of the path, a gate, two lanterns at the threshold; the eye should be led to the door.
  • Plant for age. Let saplings, reeds and wild bushes creep up to the walls; a perfectly mown perimeter looks unlived-in.
Functional & beautiful aren't separate

A drying rack, a quern, a forge and a cellar door all work as decoration when placed thoughtfully. Pair this page with The Homestead (the working builds) for a base that's productive and a pleasure to come home to.
