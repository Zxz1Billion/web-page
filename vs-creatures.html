The Bestiary

Everything that moves in this world wants something — your meat to eat, your hide to wear, or simply to be left alone. This is the field guide to the creatures you'll meet: what they do, what they drop, and exactly how to handle each one. Learn the damage tiers first, then read your neighbours: the skittish prey, the pack hunters, the things that crawl out of rifts, and the livestock you can raise once you stop running.

00 — Read this first

Combat & Damage Tiers

Fighting in Vintage Story isn't about raw hit-point swings — it's a game of tiers. Almost every attack lands at a damage tier (roughly 1, 2 or 3), and your armour reduces incoming damage by tier. Matching your gear to the threat matters more than swinging harder.

How damage and armour interact

An attack's damage tier describes how penetrating it is. Armour reduces incoming damage by tier: light armour blunts tier-1 hits well but barely slows a tier-3 blow, while heavier, higher-tier plate is what keeps you standing against the worst predators. This is why tiered armour matters — a hare bite is nothing, but a bear or a deep-cave drifter punches through anything flimsy.

The simple rule

Wear the best armour you can carry against tougher creatures. The same blow that a leather jerkin shrugs off from a wild pig will gut you when it comes from a gray boar, a wolf, or a storm-grade drifter — because those land at a higher tier.

Aggression & seek radius

Each animal has its own seek radius — how close you can get before it reacts. For prey that means how soon they bolt; for predators it's how far away they'll notice and charge you. A wolf's aggression radius is large and unforgiving; a hyena's is much smaller (around 10 blocks); a hare's "radius" just means it sprints off the moment you're near.

Your weapons

throw + stab

Spears

Knappable from stone, then later metal. Stab in melee or throw them with G for a ranged opener — the one true ranged option a Commoner has on Day One.

butcher

Knives

Weak as a weapon but essential: a knife is how you butcher a downed animal into meat, hide and fat. Carry one on every hunt.

Hunter

Bows

Crafted by the Hunter class. The premier ranged weapon — kite predators and pick off prey before they ever close the distance.

Malefactor

Slings

The Malefactor's ranged tool. Cheap ammo, useful for softening targets from cover when you can't craft a bow.

Where this fits

New to fighting at all? Start with The First Day for knapping a spear and surviving night one. For the full weapon, armour and metal-tier ladder, see The Long Road — better tiers there directly translate into surviving the creatures below.

01 — The harvest

Prey & Passive Animals

These animals won't hurt you — they're walking food, hide and resources. The challenge is catching them: most are skittish and bolt the moment you're spotted. Approach low, open with a thrown spear or an arrow, then close in and butcher with a knife.

How to hunt skittish prey

  1. Approach downwind and crouched. Skittish animals (hare, deer, gazelle) bolt early. Move slowly and use cover; sprinting straight at them never works.
  2. Open at range. Throw a spear (G) or loose an arrow before they flee. A hit that doesn't kill still slows the chase.
  3. Finish & butcher. Close in for the kill, then right-click the carcass with a knife to harvest meat, hide and fat.

The herbivores & small game

Hares are the classic first kill — small, skittish, common, and they'll happily eat vegetables tossed into or near a trough, which makes them tameable. Deer are larger and very skittish but worth the stalk for meat, hide and fat. Gazelle are fast and live in warm regions — open at range or you'll never catch one. Raccoons and foxes are small wildlife: minor meat and hide, and a fox will scatter your loose poultry.

Poultry & farm stock

Chickens (and the rooster) are the easiest sustainable food — keep a small flock for a steady supply of eggs plus meat. Sheep give wool (shear them), goats give milk, and all of them give meat and hide when butchered.

Wild pigs & boar — read the tier

Pigs are a special case. A wild pig will defend itself but only deals tier-1 damage — manageable in any armour. The exception is the large gray boar, which hits at tier 2 and should be treated with respect. The good news: pigs breed into tameness — by the 3rd generation of bred pigs the piglets lose their fear and become passive farm animals.

Don't waste the kill

Always carry a knife when hunting — without one you can't butcher, and the carcass goes to waste. Raw meat must be cooked, and a varied diet (protein and veg, fruit, grain, dairy) raises your max health. Full system in The Larder.

02 — Things that hunt you

Predators

Three wild predators will actively come for you. Early on — before armour and metal weapons — they are the most likely thing to kill you in daylight. Know which is which, and never let a fight happen on open ground.

tier 2 · packs

🐺 Wolf

Hunts in packs, deals tier-2 damage, and has a large aggression radius — it notices you from far off and commits. The single most dangerous early-game animal. A lone wolf is survivable; three are not.

tier 2 · warm

🐆 Hyena

The warm-region answer to the wolf — appears where it's too hot for wolves. Also tier-2 and also pack-hunting, but with a much smaller aggression radius (~10 blocks), so you can often avoid one by giving it room.

high HP · high dmg

🐻 Bear

High health and high damage — a bear is dangerous to players and to other animals alike. It can soak a lot of punishment and dish out more. Avoid early; only fight one when well-armed and well-armoured.

How to survive each

  1. Use terrain against packs. Wolves and hyenas swarm. Put your back to a wall, a cliff or a tree so they can't surround you — being flanked is what kills you, not any single bite.
  2. Fight from a doorway. A one-block gap funnels a pack into single file so you face one mouth at a time. Your shelter doorway is a fortress against wolves.
  3. Kite with ranged. Throw spears or shoot a bow while backing away. Open hits before the gap closes; for a bear, ranged kiting is often the only safe way to whittle that big health pool down.
  4. Give the hyena space. Its short ~10-block radius means walking a wide arc around one frequently avoids the fight entirely.
  5. Just leave the bear. Early on, a bear is not a fight — it's a hazard to walk around. Come back with metal armour and a metal spear or bow.

Never fight a pack in the open

If you hear a pack and you're caught outside, get to a doorway, a one-wide ledge or up a block they can't reach — anywhere they have to come at you one at a time. Running across flat ground means being surrounded and torn down. Better armour (see The Long Road) turns these tier-2 hits from lethal into survivable.

03 — The unliving

Temporal & Hostile Creatures

Not everything here is an animal. The world's broken time bleeds drifters, cave locusts and rift creatures into reality. These are the monsters of Vintage Story — and dealing with them is bound up with temporal stability.

Drifters — the core hostile

The shambling, rasping drifter is the creature you'll fight most. They spawn at light level 7 or below, cluster near rifts, and during a temporal storm they appear everywhere — even in daylight. Besides a melee swipe they have a thrown-rock attack for ~1 damage when they can't path to you. There are six tiers, and deeper/stronger ones hit far harder — and note tainted & corrupt drifters can spawn on the surface in low light, not only deep underground, so a night-time fight isn't always against the weak kind.

There's an upside: killing drifters restores some temporal stability, and stronger variants restore more. They also drop temporal gears — the stability currency that powers translocators, repairs and trade. Fighting drifters is how you both defend and resupply.

Locusts — metal in the dark

Locusts are corrupt-metal insectoids lurking in dark caves, usually deep where you mine. The common bronze locust hits for only ~1.5, but the deep corrupt locust hits for ~4 and is far nastier in a cramped tunnel. The Clockmaker class is specifically strong against them, so a Clockmaker deep-miner clears infested caverns more easily.

The bell — a deep mini-boss

Rarely, deep down, you'll find a bell: a stationary object with enormous HP and no attack of its own. Get within ~15 blocks and it rings, continuously spawning deep/tainted/corrupt drifters and bronze locusts in a ~12-block radius until you destroy it. Come prepared or leave it be.

Rift creatures — born of the surface tears

Surface rifts spawn their own horrors. Bowtorn carry a ranged turret attack (effective ~14–29 blocks, activating if you're within 30) and flee below 30% HP; shivers climb walls and bite. Both are tougher than basic drifters — where a rift glows on the surface, expect a harder fight than the usual night-time drifter.

The whole system, explained

Temporal stability, rifts, storms, gears and translocators are the spine of the late game. This page is just the bestiary half — the mechanics live in The Rust & The Gear. Keep light around your base: drifters spawn in the dark, so a lit base is a quiet base.

04 — New in 1.22

Fish

Version 1.22 added fishing, and with it a whole new tier of prey: 20+ freshwater fish species living in rivers and lakes, with location-specific populations and their own bait preferences.

You catch them with rods and lures or with traps, matching bait to the species you're after. Fish are a reliable source of protein nutrition that doesn't require chasing skittish land game, and the catch can be dried or salted for long-term storage — invaluable for stocking a larder before winter or a storm.

05 — Stop running, start farming

Animal Husbandry

Once you can survive the wildlife, you can raise it. Husbandry turns the hunt into a renewable supply: tame animals by feeding them at a trough, breed them across generations, and butcher the surplus for meat, hide and fat without ever leaving home.

Taming & breeding

You keep and tame animals by feeding them at a trough — hares and pigs, for example, eat vegetables tossed nearby or placed in a trough. Fed, penned animals settle, and a well-fed pair will breed. Offspring are tamer than their parents: this is why wild pigs become fully passive by the 3rd generation — each generation born in captivity loses more of its fear.

What to keep, and why

wool

Sheep

Shear for wool → cloth and warm clothing. Renewable and they never need killing.

eggs

Chickens

A small flock means steady eggs — the easiest sustainable food in the game — plus meat from the surplus.

milk

Goats

Milk for dairy nutrition (and the dairy products that come from it). Fills a whole nutrition bar most players neglect.

meat · hide · fat

Pigs & others

Bred to passivity, pigs are a reliable meat, hide and fat herd. The workhorse of a survival farmstead.

Butchering — turning an animal into goods

Butchering with a knife yields three things: meat (food), hide (which becomes leather via tanning), and fat (rendered into tallow for candles, soap and recipes). A managed herd gives you all three on demand.

Start a small farmstead

  1. Catch a breeding pair. Tame a couple of the same animal (e.g. two wild pigs, or a few hares) by feeding vegetables near a trough until they settle.
  2. Pen them in. Fence a paddock so they can't wander off or get eaten by wolves. Keep predators out.
  3. Build a trough & keep it stocked. Toss in vegetables (or the right feed) so they stay fed — fed animals breed.
  4. Breed across generations. Let a fed pair produce young; raise those young to breed again. By the 3rd generation pigs are fully passive farm stock.
  5. Harvest sustainably. Shear sheep for wool, collect eggs, milk goats — and butcher only the surplus with a knife for meat, hide and fat. Keep your breeders alive.
Where the goods go next

Leather (from tanning hide), tallow (from fat), wool-cloth and a full larder of dried meat, eggs and dairy all feed into the wider crafting and cooking systems. Pick that thread up in The Larder, and bring it all home with the basics in The First Day.
