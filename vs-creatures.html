The Bestiary

Everything that moves wants something: your meat, your hide, or to be left alone. This is the field guide to the creatures you'll meet. What they do, what they drop, and how to handle each one. Learn the damage tiers first, then the skittish prey, the pack hunters, the things from rifts, and the livestock you can raise once you stop running.

00 — Read this first

Combat & Damage Tiers

Fighting in Vintage Story runs on tiers. Almost every attack lands at a damage tier (roughly 1, 2 or 3), and your armour reduces incoming damage by tier. Match your gear to the threat. It matters more than swinging harder.

How damage and armour interact

An attack's damage tier is how penetrating it is. Armour reduces incoming damage by tier. Light armour blunts tier-1 hits well but barely slows a tier-3 blow. Heavier, higher-tier plate keeps you standing against the worst predators. A hare bite is nothing, but a bear or a deep-cave drifter punches through anything flimsy.

The simple rule

Wear the best armour you can carry against tougher creatures. A blow a leather jerkin shrugs off from a wild pig will gut you from a gray boar, a wolf, or a storm-grade drifter, because those land at a higher tier.

Aggression & seek radius

Each animal has its own seek radius: how close you can get before it reacts. For prey, that's how soon they bolt. For predators, how far away they notice and charge. A wolf's radius is large and unforgiving. A hyena's is much smaller (around 10 blocks). A hare's just means it sprints off the moment you're near.

Your weapons

throw + stab

Spears

Knappable from stone, later metal. Stab in melee or throw them by holding and releasing right-click for a ranged opener. The one ranged option a Commoner has on Day One.

butcher

Knives

Weak as a weapon but essential. A knife is how you butcher a downed animal into meat, hide and fat. Carry one on every hunt.

Hunter

Bows

Crafted by the Hunter class. The best ranged weapon. Kite predators and pick off prey before they close the distance.

Malefactor

Slings

The Malefactor's ranged tool. Cheap ammo. Soften targets from cover when you can't craft a bow.

Where this fits

New to fighting? Start with The First Day for knapping a spear and surviving night one. For the full weapon, armour and metal-tier ladder, see The Long Road. Better tiers there translate directly into surviving the creatures below.

01 — The harvest

Prey & Passive Animals

These animals won't hurt you. They're walking food, hide and resources. The challenge is catching them. Most are skittish and bolt the moment you're spotted. Approach low, open with a thrown spear or an arrow, then close in and butcher with a knife.

How to hunt skittish prey

  1. Approach downwind and crouched. Skittish animals (hare, deer, gazelle) bolt early. Move slowly and use cover. Sprinting straight at them never works.
  2. Open at range. Throw a spear (hold and release right-click) or loose an arrow before they flee. A hit that doesn't kill still slows the chase.
  3. Finish and butcher. Close in for the kill, then right-click the carcass with a knife to harvest meat, hide and fat.

The herbivores & small game

Hares are the classic first kill: small, skittish, common. They flee from anything within 12 blocks, even before being hit, and jump only 1 block high, so a fence or a 2-deep pit holds them. They eat any vegetable at a small trough or dropped nearby, which makes them tameable. Deer are larger and flee at 20 blocks; damaged males turn aggressive and seek you. Deer also fight wolves and hyenas, and males always drop antlers. Gazelle are fast, live in hot dry regions, spot you from a long way off, and cannot be tamed. Open at range or you'll never catch one. Raccoons (can't be tamed) and foxes are small wildlife: minor meat and hide. A fox will scatter your loose poultry.

Flee radius is the number that matters

For prey, the seek radius is the flee radius. A hare bolts at 12 blocks, a deer at 20. Open your shot from outside that ring or close it crouched and behind cover. Sprint inside it and the animal is already gone.

Poultry & farm stock

Chickens (and the rooster) are the easiest sustainable food. Keep a small flock for a steady supply of eggs plus meat. Sheep give wool (shear them), goats give milk, and all of them give meat and hide when butchered.

Wild pigs & boar — read the tier

Pigs are a special case. A wild pig defends itself but only deals tier-1 damage, manageable in any armour. The large gray boar is the exception. It hits at tier 2, so treat it with respect. Both flee an approaching player but attack if you crowd them, and both are fiercely protective of piglets. Adult boars always drop tusks. Wild pigs spawn in groups of 2–3, elder gray boars in 1–2. Pigs breed into tameness. By the 3rd generation of bred pigs, the piglets lose their fear and become passive farm animals.

Don't waste the kill

Always carry a knife when hunting. Without one you can't butcher, and the carcass goes to waste. Cook raw meat, and eat a varied diet (protein and veg, fruit, grain, dairy) to raise your max health. Full system in The Larder.

02 — Things that hunt you

Predators

Three wild predators come for you. Early on, before armour and metal weapons, they are the most likely thing to kill you in daylight. Know which is which, and never fight on open ground.

tier 2 · packs

🐺 Wolf

Hunts in packs of 2–3, deals tier-2 damage, and actively hunts any player within 20 blocks (hit one and the rest aggro within 15). Lives in forested temperate regions. The most dangerous early-game animal. A lone wolf is survivable. Three are not.

tier 2 · warm

🐆 Hyena

The warm-region wolf, appearing where it's too hot for wolves (~25–40 °C, dry). Also tier-2 and pack-hunting (groups up to 5), but adults only hunt within ~10 blocks. Give it room and you can often slip past.

15 dmg · tier 2

🐻 Bear

A heavy 15-point tier-2 slash and a deep health pool, with a 20-block aggression radius. Five variants by climate (brown, black, polar, panda, sun). Brown/black/polar can rampage unprovoked; panda & sun only retaliate. Avoid early.

How to survive each

  1. Use terrain against packs. Wolves and hyenas swarm. Put your back to a wall, cliff or tree so they can't surround you. Being flanked kills you, not any single bite.
  2. Fight from a doorway. A one-block gap funnels a pack into single file, so you face one mouth at a time. A shelter doorway is a fortress against wolves.
  3. Kite with ranged. Throw spears or shoot a bow while backing away. Open hits before the gap closes. For a bear, ranged kiting is often the only safe way to whittle down that health pool.
  4. Give the hyena space. Its short ~10-block radius means a wide arc around one often avoids the fight.
  5. Leave the bear. Early on, a bear is a hazard to walk around, not a fight. Come back with metal armour and a metal spear or bow.

Never fight a pack in the open

Caught outside when you hear a pack? Get to a doorway, a one-wide ledge or up a block they can't reach, anywhere they have to come at you one at a time. Running across flat ground gets you surrounded and torn down. Better armour (see The Long Road) turns these tier-2 hits from lethal into survivable.

03 — The unliving

Temporal & Hostile Creatures

Not everything here is an animal. The world's broken time bleeds drifters, cave locusts and rift creatures into reality. These are the monsters of Vintage Story, and dealing with them ties into temporal stability.

Drifters — the core hostile

The shambling, rasping drifter is the creature you'll fight most. They spawn at light level 7 or below. On the surface, only the basic Surface type spawns, and only within 25 blocks of a temporal rift; the meaner tiers are gated to lower depths. During a temporal storm they appear everywhere, even in daylight. A drifter wanders until it spots you within 20 blocks, then pursues. Besides a melee swipe (cooldown 0.5–1.75s), one within 15 blocks that can't path to you throws a rock for 1 damage every 1–10 seconds. There are six types, and deeper ones hit far harder.

There's an upside. Killing drifters restores some temporal stability, and stronger variants restore more. They also drop temporal gears, the stability currency that powers translocators, repairs and trade. Fighting drifters defends you and resupplies you.

Locusts — metal in the dark

Locusts are corrupt-metal insectoids in dark caves, usually deep where you mine. The common bronze locust hits for only ~1.5. The deep corrupt locust hits for ~4 and is far nastier in a cramped tunnel. The Clockmaker class is strong against them, so a Clockmaker deep-miner clears infested caverns more easily.

The bell — a deep mini-boss

Rarely, deep down, you'll find a bell: a stationary creature with extremely high HP and no attack of its own. When it detects you it rings, continuously spawning drifters and the occasional bronze locust, holding up to 6 enemies at once with a new one every 1–5 seconds, until you destroy it. It's a damage race against an endless add-spawner. Come prepared or leave it be.

Rift creatures — born of the surface tears

Surface rifts spawn their own horrors. Bowtorn carry a ranged turret attack (effective ~14–29 blocks, activating if you're within 30) and flee below 30% HP. Shivers climb walls and bite. Both are tougher than basic drifters. Where a rift glows on the surface, expect a harder fight than the usual night drifter.

04 — New in 1.22

Fish

Version 1.22 added fishing and a new tier of prey: 20+ freshwater fish species in rivers and lakes, with location-specific populations and their own bait preferences.

Catch them with rods and lures or with traps, matching bait to the species. Fish are a reliable source of protein that doesn't require chasing skittish land game. Dry or salt the catch for long-term storage, useful for stocking a larder before winter or a storm.

05 — Stop running, start farming

Animal Husbandry

Once you can survive the wildlife, you can raise it. Husbandry turns the hunt into a renewable supply. Tame animals by feeding them at a trough, breed them across generations, and butcher the surplus for meat, hide and fat without leaving home.

Taming & breeding

Keep and tame animals by feeding them at a trough. Hares and pigs, for example, eat vegetables tossed nearby or placed in a trough. Fed, penned animals settle, and a well-fed pair will breed. Offspring are tamer than their parents. This is why wild pigs become fully passive by the 3rd generation: each generation born in captivity loses more of its fear.

What to keep, and why

wool

Sheep

Shear for wool → cloth and warm clothing. Renewable, and they never need killing.

eggs

Chickens

A small flock gives steady eggs, the easiest sustainable food in the game, plus meat from the surplus.

milk

Goats

Milk for dairy nutrition and the products made from it. Fills a nutrition bar most players neglect.

meat · hide · fat

Pigs & others

Bred to passivity, pigs are a reliable meat, hide and fat herd. The workhorse of a farmstead.

Butchering — turning an animal into goods

Butchering with a knife yields three things: meat (food), hide (which becomes leather via tanning), and fat (rendered into tallow for candles, soap and recipes). A managed herd gives all three on demand.

Start a small farmstead

  1. Catch a breeding pair. Tame two of the same animal (e.g. two wild pigs, or a few hares) by feeding vegetables near a trough until they settle.
  2. Pen them in. Fence a paddock so they can't wander off or get eaten by wolves. Keep predators out.
  3. Build a trough and keep it stocked. Toss in vegetables or the right feed so they stay fed. Fed animals breed.
  4. Breed across generations. Let a fed pair produce young, then raise those young to breed again. By the 3rd generation pigs are fully passive farm stock.
  5. Harvest sustainably. Shear sheep for wool, collect eggs, milk goats, and butcher only the surplus with a knife for meat, hide and fat. Keep your breeders alive.
Where the goods go next

Leather (from tanning hide), tallow (from fat), wool-cloth and a full larder of dried meat, eggs and dairy all feed the wider crafting and cooking systems. Pick that thread up in The Larder, and cover the basics in The First Day.
