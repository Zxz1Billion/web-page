Every modpack has a tech tree people argue about — and a quiet layer of mods that make the whole thing pleasant: the recipe lookup, the look-at tooltip, the minimap, the quest book, the rendering engine, and a stack of libraries nobody sees. This page covers all of it for To the Moon 2.0, so no mod in the pack goes unmentioned. Read it once, bind a few keys, and the rest of the pack reads itself.

00 — Orientation

Start Here

Most of what's on this page is invisible until it's missing. These are the quality-of-life, information, mapping, performance and library mods — the connective tissue that turns a heap of tech and magic mods into a coherent pack you can actually play.

You don't need to "progress" through any of this. Almost all of it is on the moment you load in. The single most useful habit for reading a big modpack is to lean on two mods from minute one:

what is this

JEI — the index

Hover any item and press R (recipe) or U (uses). It's the searchable encyclopedia of the entire pack. If you don't know how to make something, ask JEI first.

what am I looking at

Jade — the tooltip

Point at any block or mob and a tooltip tells you what it is, which mod it's from, its progress, and whether your tool can harvest it. Your in-world eyes.

where to go next

FTB Quests — the path

If the pack ships a quest book, it's your intended route through the mods, with rewards. Open it early to see what the authors want you to build.

How to read this page

The first half (§01–04) is mods you'll use daily — info, maps, inventory, quests. The second half (§05–07) is the machinery under the pack — the performance stack, ore unification, and the library mods. §08 is a checklist for your literal first hour. Skip around; nothing here is sequential.

01 — Know what you're touching

Recipe & Info Mods

This cluster answers the two questions you ask constantly in a modpack: "how do I make this?" and "what is this thing?". Learn the four below and you'll rarely need to alt-tab to a wiki.

Just Enough Items (JEI)

The backbone of pack literacy. JEI's item list sits on the right of every inventory screen; the search bar at the bottom filters it live. It indexes every recipe in the pack, including those added by machines and custom processes, and chains them — click through from an ingot to the ore that makes it to the machine that processes it.

JEI — the keys worth muscle-memory
Key / actionDoes
R (hover item)Show recipes that make this item.
U (hover item)Show uses — every recipe this item goes into.
A (hover item)Toggle a bookmark; the bookmark list pins items you're working toward.
Type in search@modname filters to one mod, #tag by tag, $ for fluids.
Click a tabCycle recipe categories (e.g. crafting → smelting → a machine's own page).
Cheat mode is a setting, not a default

JEI has a "cheat" mode that gives items on click — usually only enabled in creative or by a server. In normal survival, JEI shows recipes; it doesn't hand you items. Don't expect to click-grab on a survival server.

Jade — the "look-at" tooltip

Jade (a maintained fork of the classic HWYLA/WAILA idea) draws a small overlay naming whatever your crosshair is on. Out of the box it shows the block/entity name, the mod it comes from, and — via plugins from other mods — extra state: machine progress bars, energy/fluid contents, crop growth stage, mob health, and crucially harvestability (whether your current tool can mine it, shown by a wrench/pick icon). Toggle individual data lines in its config screen if the overlay gets busy.

AppleSkin

A tiny but beloved mod: it adds saturation and hunger information to the HUD and to food tooltips. Hover a food item and you'll see how much hunger and saturation it restores; the hunger bar gains shading that previews what a food will refill and how much saturation you have left. It makes food choices in a tech pack (where you'd rather not babysit hunger) a non-issue.

Better F3 & Nature's Compass

debug

Better F3

Reorganises the vanilla F3 debug screen into clean, colour-coded, configurable sections. Same data, far more readable — handy when you're chasing coordinates, biome, light level or FPS.

biomes

Nature's Compass

Craft it, right-click to open a searchable list of every biome, pick one, and it points you toward the nearest instance with the distance. The fastest way to find that one biome a recipe or mob needs.

02 — Don't get lost

Maps & Navigation

Two complementary mapping approaches ship here: a live, screen-overlay minimap (JourneyMap) and an in-world, hold-in-hand atlas item (Map Atlases). They coexist happily — use the minimap for moment-to-moment travel and the atlas for a tangible, shareable record.

JourneyMap

The standard real-time mapper. It gives you a corner minimap, a fullscreen map (default J), and a web map you can open in a browser. It auto-explores as you move, and its big draw is waypoints — drop a marker (default B at your location, or click on the fullscreen map) and it shows distance and a beacon you can navigate to across dimensions.

  • Waypoints — name and colour them; deaths auto-drop a waypoint so you can recover gear. Essential for marking your base, the Nether portal, and that ore vein you couldn't carry home.
  • Mob radar — toggleable dots for mobs, players and animals around you.
  • Fullscreen tools — measure, teleport (if permitted), and edit waypoints from the big map.
Servers can clip its wings

On multiplayer, the server controls JourneyMap features through its own config. Mob radar, cave mapping and the player-tracker are frequently disabled server-side to prevent cheating. If a feature is greyed out online, that's intended — not a bug.

Map Atlases

A different, charming take on mapping. Map Atlases gives you a craftable Atlas item that holds vanilla maps and auto-stitches them together into one seamless, Antique-Atlas-style scrollable map as you explore — no need to craft and frame dozens of separate maps. Open it in your hand, pan and zoom, and add markers for points of interest. Because it's built on real in-world map items, it works cleanly in multiplayer and can be handed to another player.

Two maps, two jobs

Let JourneyMap handle live navigation and waypoint beacons; let the Atlas be your physical, in-inventory record you can pull out, annotate and trade. They don't conflict — keeping both is the comfortable setup.

Need to find a place rather than remember one? That's Nature's Compass from §01 — pair it with JourneyMap waypoints: locate the biome, then drop a waypoint when you arrive.

03 — Stop fighting the UI

Inventory & Input QoL

A loose bag of mods that each fix one small, daily annoyance. None of them change progression — they just remove friction.

Small comforts, big difference
ModFixesHow you notice it
Mouse TweaksSlow, click-by-click inventory workDrag to move/split across slots, RMB-drag to distribute, and a shift-scroll to pull whole stacks. Inventory chores get 3× faster.
ClumpsLag from huge XP-orb spraysXP orbs clump into a few merged orbs (after a mob farm or a big mine). Same XP, far fewer entities, smoother frames.
FastWorkbench / FastFurnace / FastBenchSlow recipe lookups in crafting tables & furnacesCaches recipe matching so big modpacks don't hitch when you open a workbench or a furnace ticks. Invisible — that's the point.

RightClickHarvest

RightClickHarvest lets you harvest mature crops with a right-click — it picks the produce and replants the seed in one motion, optionally costing a little hunger. It's most relevant to farming, so it's covered alongside the agriculture mods: see the Farmer's Delight guide for the full treatment. Mentioned here so you know why right-clicking your wheat suddenly just works.

These are "set and forget"

Mouse Tweaks, Clumps and the Fast* trio have almost nothing to configure — they're correct by default. The only knob most players touch is Clumps' merge radius, and only on heavily-modded mob farms.

04 — Structure & company

Progression & Multiplayer

The FTB suite provides the pack's quest framework and its team/party system. Even in single-player these shape how you experience the pack; in multiplayer they're how you share a base and a goal.

FTB Quests

An in-game quest book organised into chapters. Pack authors use it to lay out intended progression — "smelt your first ingot," "build a Mekanism enrichment line," "reach the Moon" — and to hand out rewards (loot, items, sometimes unlocks) for completing tasks. Quests can be detection-based (auto-complete when you have the item) or require you to submit items. Open the book early: it's the clearest map of what the pack actually wants you to do, and a good antidote to the "I have 200 mods, now what?" paralysis.

  • Chapters group related goals; lines between quests show prerequisites.
  • Rewards are claimed from completed quests — don't forget to collect them.
  • The book is read-only for players (authors edit it in a separate mode), so you can't break your own progression.

FTB Teams & FTB Library

parties

FTB Teams

Create or join a team to share quest progress, claimed chunks and (in some packs) a shared inventory of rewards. Invite friends, set a party so a quest completed by one member counts for all.

shared base

FTB Library

The common code FTB Quests and Teams both sit on — sided data, the team system, GUI widgets and config plumbing. You never open it directly; it just has to be present for the other two to work.

Corail Tombstone — death insurance

When you die, Corail Tombstone drops a grave at the death site holding all your items and XP, instead of scattering them on the ground to despawn. Walk back, right-click the grave, and everything's restored. It also layers in a small progression of its own (soul tokens, enchantments, perks) tied to the mod's altar. Because it's most relevant to surviving the pack's mobs and dimensions, the full rundown lives in the Mobs & Combat guide — flagged here so you know your loot is safe behind a tombstone, not a 5-minute despawn timer.

05 — Make it run, make it shine

Performance & Visuals

A 200-mod pack is heavy. This stack is why it still runs: a chain of rendering, memory and tick optimisations, plus one mod that's a genuine visual upgrade. Most need zero configuration — but it's worth knowing what each one buys you.

The optimisation stack

Distant Horizons — the visual upgrade

The headliner. Distant Horizons adds a Level-of-Detail (LOD) system: it generates simplified, low-detail geometry for terrain far beyond your normal render distance and draws it cheaply. The result is mountains and coastlines visible kilometres away — render distances that would be impossible to load as full chunks — at a modest performance cost. The first time you crest a hill with it on, the world looks completely different.

  • It builds LODs from chunks you've visited, so distant terrain "fills in" as you explore. A freshly-generated world starts sparse and improves.
  • Toggle & tune it in its dedicated config screen (reachable from Options → and its own button). The key sliders are LOD render distance and quality — turn them up for views, down if your GPU struggles.
  • It works with Rubidium here; keep your vanilla render distance modest and let Distant Horizons handle the far field for the best frames-to-view ratio.
CustomSkinLoader, quietly

The pack also bundles CustomSkinLoader, which fetches player skins from multiple sources so everyone shows up correctly even on offline/alternate servers. Nothing to configure — it just keeps players from looking like Steve.

Config libraries do the wiring

Placebo and Carbon Config (and the broader config libs in §07) provide the shared config and registry helpers many of these mods read. They have no gameplay of their own — if you see them in the mod list, they're dependencies, not features.

06 — One ingot to rule them

Almost Unified

This one deserves its own section because players notice it directly. In a big pack, several mods each add their own copper, tin, lead, silver, steel, etc. — functionally identical metals with different names and textures. Left alone, that means duplicate JEI entries, recipes that demand "Mod A's tin" when you only have "Mod B's tin," and ore-doubling lines that won't accept each other's output.

What it actually does

Almost Unified reads the shared ore/ingot/dust tags (forge:ingots/tin, forge:ores/silver, and so on) and picks one canonical item per material, then quietly rewrites recipe outputs to produce that chosen variant. Every mod's machines still accept any tagged equivalent as input (that's vanilla tag behaviour), but they all output the one unified version. The net effect:

  • JEI gets cleaner — fewer near-duplicate entries cluttering the item list.
  • Recipes stop being picky — you no longer hoard three brands of the same ingot hoping one matches.
  • Ore processing converges — whatever mod doubles your ore, the result slots straight into any other mod's recipes.
Why your tin all looks the same now

If you've wondered why every mod's tin/lead/silver suddenly shares one texture and name, that's Almost Unified choosing a winner per material. The choice is driven by a priority list in its config (the pack author sets which mod "wins" each metal). It doesn't delete the other items — it just stops new ones being produced.

It only unifies what's tagged

Materials without a common tag, or items the pack explicitly excludes, are left alone. So a few oddball metals may still appear in multiple forms — that's expected, not a unification failure.

If a mod "doesn't appear," check its library

When a content mod seems missing, its library dependency is usually the culprit — a version mismatch on Architectury, GeckoLib or Kotlin for Forge will silently disable the mod that needs it. The pack's launcher log (or a /spark health check) will name the offender.

08 — Sixty minutes in

A First-Hour QoL Checklist

You can play the whole pack on defaults, but five minutes of setup makes the next hundred hours smoother. Run down this list right after you spawn:

  1. Open JEI and learn R / U / A. Search a known item, press R for its recipe and U for its uses. Bookmark (A) your first big goal so you can re-find its recipe instantly.
  2. Confirm Jade is showing harvestability. Point at a stone and an ore — the tooltip should name the block, its mod, and whether your tool can mine it. Open Jade's config to trim any data lines you don't want.
  3. Set your first JourneyMap waypoints. Drop one on your spawn/base (default B), open the fullscreen map (J) to look around, and remember that deaths auto-mark a recovery waypoint.
  4. Open the FTB Quest book. Read chapter one to see the intended path, and check whether you're meant to join or create an FTB Team before progressing (especially on a server).
  5. Craft a Nature's Compass as soon as you have the materials — it pays for itself the first time a recipe demands a specific biome.
  6. Tune Distant Horizons. Open its settings, set an LOD render distance you like, and travel a bit to let the far terrain build. Pair it with a modest vanilla render distance for the best frames.
  7. Glance at the food HUD (AppleSkin) and trust that a tombstone (Corail Tombstone) will hold your loot if you die — then go mine without paranoia.
  8. If anything stutters, run /spark profiler for ~60 seconds and read the report. It'll point straight at the laggy mod or chunk.
That's the whole handbook

Bind those keys, set those waypoints, and the rest of the pack — every tech tree and magic system in the other guides — sits on top of a base that's fast, legible and forgiving. That was always the job of the mods on this page.
