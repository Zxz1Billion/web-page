The Six Classes

At character creation you pick one of six classes. Each is a bundle of small bonuses, matching penalties, and sometimes a class-locked recipe no one else can make. Only the Commoner is neutral. The choice is permanent, so this page lays out what each one gives, what it costs, and who it suits.

00 — The rules

How Classes Work

A class is a set of traits applied to your character. Most are small percentage tweaks (a little more damage here, a little less health there); a few are unique craftables that gate a whole weapon or item behind one class.

permanent

Chosen once

You pick at character creation. In vanilla you can't change class later, so pick for the run you want.

trade-offs

Give and take

Every class except the Commoner trades a bonus for a penalty. There is no strictly-best class, only fits for a playstyle.

not locked out

You can still do everything

Only a handful of recipes are class-locked. Any class can farm, smith, build and cook — just without that class's head-start.

New player? Pick Commoner

The Commoner has no penalties, so nothing on this list will surprise or hamper you while you learn. Every other class assumes you already know which trade you want. The numbers below are deliberately qualitative — the exact percentages show on the creation screen and are config-tunable.

01 — The all-rounder

Commoner

The default. No bonuses, no penalties — balanced at everything, weak at nothing.

Gives

A clean slate: full health, normal damage, normal hunger, normal harvesting. Nothing to plan around.

Costs

No head-start in any field. You craft and fight the ordinary way (spears and thrown spears instead of a bow or sling).

Best for

First-timers, blind playthroughs, and anyone who wants flexibility over specialisation. You can become a smith, hunter, builder or farmer freely — see The Arsenal and The Long Road.

02 — The ranged specialist

Hunter

A bow-and-wilderness expert. Kills game from range and butchers it efficiently — but suffers in the dark underground.

Gives

Stronger ranged combat and more efficient butchering (more meat/hide per kill). Thrives in forests, plains and mountains.

Costs

Weaker in close melee, and less comfortable in confined tunnels — a poor pure-miner.

Class-locked craftables

Only the Hunter crafts the crude bow, arrows and the recurve bow. That makes the Hunter the only class with a true craftable ranged weapon from the start (everyone else throws spears or, as a Malefactor, slings).

Playstyle

Open the fight at range, soften or drop prey before it closes, and live off the land. Pair with light gambeson/chain armor so you can keep firing (plate hampers the bow). See The Bestiary for what to hunt.

03 — The scavenger

Malefactor

A tenacious survivor who avoids unwanted attention and finds loot others miss — at the cost of a frail body in a fight.

Gives

Better foraging and more loot from cracked vessels and ruins. Good at slipping past trouble rather than facing it.

Costs

Frail (less health) and weak in melee — a heavy conscience, the game says. You can't trade blows.

Class-locked craftables

The Malefactor crafts the sling — a cheap ranged weapon with stone/clay ammo, the class's answer to its weak melee.

Playstyle

Loot-focused exploration: raid ruins and cracked vessels, sling from cover, and avoid stand-up fights. Lean on stealth and distance, not armor.

04 — The machinist

Clockmaker

A tinkerer specialised against the mechanical horrors of the deep, and a fixer of machinery — but frail and weak against anything else.

Gives

Big bonus damage versus locusts (the mechanical cave creatures), and the ability to repair and dismantle machinery.

Costs

Frail, and deals less damage to everything that isn't a locust. A narrow specialist.

Class-locked craftables

The Clockmaker crafts the tuning spear — a tool that hacks a bronze or corrupt locust with one hit and turns it into a pet that fights for you. (It does not seal rifts; see The Rust & The Gear.)

Playstyle

A deep-delving, machinery-loving niche. Strongest where locusts swarm; bring backup or a falx for everything else. Pairs well with heavy mechanical-power bases.

05 — The soldier

Blackguard

A close-quarters fighter who thrives in a skirmish and wears armor better than anyone — but eats more and fumbles delicate work.

Gives

More melee damage, more armor durability, and a smaller movement penalty from heavy armor — so plate slows a Blackguard less.

Costs

Hungrier (food drains faster), weak at range, and poor at harvesting food — no talent for delicate work.

Class-locked craftables

The Blackguard alone crafts the Blackguard shortsword and a shield — the only class that fields a shield in vanilla.

Playstyle

Front-line melee: stack the best plate you can (the penalty hurts you least), wade in with shortsword and shield, and keep a big larder for the hunger. See armor.

06 — The clothier

Tailor

A master of clothing — unique garments and cheap repairs to beat the winter — but a city-soul ill-prepared for raw wilderness.

Gives

Crafts unique clothing other classes can't, and repairs clothing far more efficiently — a real edge against cold and wear.

Costs

Ill-suited to wilderness hardship: weaker in the survival and combat departments that the Blackguard or Hunter shine in.

Class-locked craftables

The Tailor makes unique clothing items and gets a large discount on clothing repair. Warmth and durability for less material — valuable on long, cold saves. See Warmth & Wear.

Playstyle

A settled, base-building, long-haul class — strongest in harsh-winter worlds where staying warm is the real fight. Less about combat, more about thriving.

★ — The decision

Which Should I Pick?

Match the class to the run you want. When in doubt, Commoner.

Multiplayer tip

On a server, classes shine as roles: a Blackguard tank, a Hunter for meat, a Clockmaker for deep runs and machine repair, a Tailor kitting everyone out, a Malefactor scouting ruins. Solo, the Commoner or Hunter covers the most ground.
