00 — Pick a layout

Three ground-floor layouts, built for short trips

Three ways to arrange the same rooms, each cutting down how far you walk. All keep regular storage (tools, blocks, materials — not food) close to where it's used, and put the cold cellar down the stairs, since Vintage Story food storage wants cool, dark, sealed rooms away from the kitchen's heat. Skim them and pick the one that fits how you play.

Option A · The Great Room — fewest steps

One open, heated room for kitchen, dining and living, so there are almost no walls to walk around and a single hearth warms the lot — ideal for a cold biome. Regular storage sits as a central island plus a side workshop; the stairs drop to a cold cellar. Best if you want the least walking and don't mind an open-plan look.

Option B · Central Store — storage at the hub

A small storage room sits dead-centre with every room opening onto it, so fetching or dropping materials is one short step from anywhere. Dining, kitchen, workshop and living all ring the core. Best if you haul a lot between rooms and want storage equidistant from all of them.

Option C · Work Cluster — storage on the work path

Kitchen, store and workshop share one back strip, with the store between the kitchen and workshop so materials are a step from both — great when you're processing ore, clay or food prep in bulk. A big living room fills the front, stairs in the corner. Best if your base is a workshop first and a home second.

Vintage Story notes

Keep the kitchen's firepit/oven on stone with a block of clearance from timber. Put your food in the cold cellar down the stairs — small, sealed, no skylight scores best and keeps food longest — and leave these ground-floor stores for tools, blocks and ores, which don't care about temperature. A single central hearth (Option A) doubles as winter warmth. The detailed plan below is Option A worked up over three floors.

01 — Ground floor

Hall, dining, living, kitchen & study

The front door opens into a central hall that holds the staircase. To the left, a dining room at the back and a living room at the front (hearth and seating), linked by their own door. To the right, the kitchen (stove and counters, with a back door) and a study at the front. Doors off the hall reach every room.

02 — First floor

Three bedrooms off the landing

The stair lands on a central landing. The master bedroom runs down the left with its own ensuite at the back; bedroom 2 and bedroom 3 sit on the right, one over the kitchen and one over the study. Each has a bed, a wardrobe and windows on two sides.

03 — Cellar

Cold store & store room

Dug under the hall and reached by the same staircase, the cellar is split in two: a cold store lined with shelving for storage vessels and sealed crocks, and a general store room for barrels and chests. Keep it cool, dark and fully enclosed — no skylight — and food lasts longest.

04 — From plan to blocks

Building it

  • Set out the ground floor first at 12×9, walls two-to-three blocks high. Mark the two interior partitions (living/kitchen and the hall) before you raise them so the doorways line up.
  • Dig the cellar down under the hall, 8×7, walled in stone, before you floor the ground level over it. Put the staircase in the hall so one run reaches both the cellar and upstairs.
  • Floor and repeat upstairs for the two bedrooms, mirroring the partitions below so the walls stack and carry the roof.
  • Doors swing into the room shown by the arc; hang plank doors and glaze the windows once the walls top out.
  • Keep the stove on stone and a block clear of timber. A cool, sealed cellar with one solid door keeps food best.
