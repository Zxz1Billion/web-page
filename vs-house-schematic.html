The Four-Level House

A proper house plan you can actually follow, block by block: a cellar under the whole footprint, a front entrance into the living room with the kitchen to the right, two bedrooms upstairs, and a roof on top. A single staircase in the central hall runs down to the cellar and up to the bedrooms. Step the viewer up one course at a time — the material tally on the right counts every block as you go.

00 — The layout

The plan

Footprint is 13×8. The ground floor splits into three down its length: the living room on the left, a central hall holding the staircase, and the kitchen on the right. Upstairs mirrors it as two bedrooms either side of the landing. The cellar sits under the lot.

Living room

The front door opens straight into it. Windows down the west wall. An interior door links it to the central hall and the staircase.

Kitchen

To the right of the hall, through an interior door. A stove/hearth sits against the back wall, with windows on the east and front.

Two bedrooms

The staircase lands on a central landing; a door each side opens into a bedroom — one over the living room, one over the kitchen — each with a bed and windows.

Cellar

Stone-walled under the whole house, reached by the same staircase running down from the hall. Cool and dark — line the walls with storage vessels for food.

02 — While you build

Build notes

  • Dig the cellar first. The schematic stacks upward for clarity, but in-game you'll cut the cellar into the ground, wall it in stone, then build the ground floor as its ceiling.
  • One staircase, two directions. The hall stair core (shown in orange) drops to the cellar and climbs to the landing. Leave the stairwell open through each floor — the plan shows the hole in the floor courses.
  • Keep the stove off timber. Set the kitchen hearth on stone and keep planks a block clear of it, like any indoor fire.
  • Headroom. Rooms here are two courses tall for a readable plan; add a third wall course before the floor/roof if you want fuller ceilings.
  • Cellar food rule. A cool, fully-enclosed cellar with one solid door keeps food best — no skylight. Line the walls with storage vessels and crocks.
