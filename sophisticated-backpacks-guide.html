00 — Orientation

Start Here

Sophisticated Backpacks starts as a place to dump loot and quietly becomes the most useful single item you carry. The headline is storage — but the real power is the row of upgrade slots down the side of the GUI. Drop in the right cards and the bag picks up drops, magnets distant items, smelts ore, compacts dust, voids junk, and even crafts — all without you opening a single machine.

The two budgets to learn first

Every backpack has two limits that rise together with its tier: inventory slots (how much it stores) and upgrade slots (how many upgrade cards it can run). A higher tier is worth crafting almost entirely for the extra upgrade slots — that's where the automation lives.

The shape of the climb

  1. Craft a leather backpack. Eight leather around a chest-like core gets you a basic bag with a handful of slots and a couple of upgrade slots.
  2. Add your first upgrade. A Pickup Upgrade is the classic first card — items you collect drop straight into the bag.
  3. Climb a tier. Surround your bag with the next metal (Copper, then Iron…) in a crafting grid; your contents and upgrades come with it.
  4. Specialize the slots. Magnet, Filter, Smelting, Compacting, Void — pick the cards that match what you're doing.
  5. Wear it. With a back-slot integration (Curios) the bag rides on your back and opens with a keybind, no inventory slot used.
  6. Go bottomless. Add Stack Upgrades so each slot holds many vanilla stacks — now the pack swallows a mining trip whole.

01 — The ladder

Tiers

Backpacks come in six tiers. Each step up adds inventory slots and upgrade slots, and is crafted by placing the lower-tier bag in the center of a crafting grid surrounded by the next material. Crucially, upgrading keeps everything inside — contents, upgrades, fluid, energy and name all carry forward, so there's no penalty for climbing late.

Upgrading keeps contents

To climb a tier, put your current backpack in the center of a 3×3 crafting grid and surround it with eight of the next material. The result is a higher-tier bag with all of your items and upgrade cards intact. You never have to empty a backpack to improve it — climb whenever you can afford the metal.

Netherite is the only fireproof one

Only the Netherite backpack is fireproof — it won't burn in lava or fire, so you can wear it on Nether and lava-cave runs without losing everything. Every lower tier behaves like a normal item drop and can be destroyed by fire, so be careful carrying a Diamond pack across lava.

02 — Make it yours

Dyeing & Customization

Beyond raw storage, a backpack is yours to personalize. You can recolor it, name it, and — once you start running fluid or energy upgrades — top up the tank and battery it carries inside.

Color & name

  • Dye the body and trim. Combine the backpack with dyes in a crafting grid to recolor it. The mod separates the main cloth from the trim/accents, so you can two-tone a pack — handy for telling your mining bag from your build bag at a glance.
  • Clientside coloring. The recolor is purely cosmetic and clientside-friendly — it doesn't change behavior, just how the pack looks on your back and in the GUI.
  • Name it. Rename the backpack in an anvil (or via the GUI rename field) so a quick glance tells you which loadout you're holding. Named bags keep their name through tier upgrades.

The built-in tank & battery

A backpack carries more than items. Certain upgrades give it functional storage of its own:

  • Fluid tank — the Tank Upgrade turns the pack into a portable fluid container, holding lava, water, or any mod fluid for transport and for feeding fluid-using upgrades.
  • FE battery — the Battery Upgrade gives the bag an internal Forge Energy buffer, both to charge tools you carry and to power energy-hungry upgrades inside the pack.
Color-code your loadouts

If you run several backpacks (a mining bag, a combat bag, a build bag), dye each a distinct color and name it. The dyed cloth shows on your back in the Curios slot, so you can swap to the right pack without opening any GUI.

03 — The heart of it

Upgrades

This is the whole point. Upgrades are crafted cards you slot into the column down the side of the backpack GUI. Each one bolts a new behavior onto the bag, and a higher-tier pack runs more of them at once. Below is the working catalogue — the Advanced versions of pickup/magnet/compacting add a filter so you control exactly what they touch.

Collection & movement

collect

Pickup Upgrade

Items you collect (mob drops, mined blocks) flow straight into the backpack instead of your hotbar.

collect

Magnet Upgrade

Pulls nearby dropped items and XP toward you and into the bag from a radius — no need to walk over each drop.

collect+filter

Advanced Pickup / Magnet

The same as above but with a filter, so you only hoover up the items you actually want.

restock

Restock Upgrade

Keeps your hotbar/inventory topped up from the bag — runs out of torches, the bag refills the stack.

deposit

Deposit Upgrade

When you open a chest (or on demand), pushes matching items out of the bag into that container — fast unloading.

Filtering & cleanup

filter

Filter Upgrade

A pure filter that controls what the backpack will accept at all — whitelist or blacklist what's allowed in.

void

Void Upgrade

Deletes filtered items on pickup — cobblestone, dirt, rotten flesh — so junk never fills a slot. The trash compactor of the bag.

tools

Tool Swapper

Automatically equips the right tool for the block/mob you're hitting, pulling it from the backpack.

In-bag processing

process

Compacting / Advanced

Auto-crafts 9 (or 4) of an item into its block form — ingots→blocks, nuggets→ingots. The Advanced version takes a filter.

process

Smelting / Auto-Smelting

Smelts items inside the bag using fuel; the Auto version cooks anything smeltable as it comes in. Ore in, ingots out.

process

Stack Upgrade

Multiplies how much each slot holds — many vanilla stacks per slot. The single biggest capacity multiplier (see §04).

process

Crafting Upgrade

Adds a 3×3 crafting grid inside the backpack GUI — craft on the go, pulling ingredients straight from the bag.

process

Feeding Upgrade

Auto-eats food from the bag when your hunger drops — set a filter so it never wastes your golden carrots.

Slots are scarce — choose deliberately

Even a Netherite pack runs only around seven upgrades at once. Processing and workstation cards each take a slot, so a "do everything" backpack isn't realistic. Build purpose-specific packs (mining, combat, building) rather than one overloaded bag — and remember a few upgrades (like Stack) effectively want their own slots.

04 — Control & capacity

Filters & Stacking

Two systems make the upgrades precise and the bag enormous: the filter built into many cards, and the Stack Upgrade that blows up capacity.

How filter cards work

Filtered upgrades (Advanced Pickup/Magnet, Filter, Void, Deposit, Feeding, Advanced Compacting…) open a filter panel where you drop sample items and choose how they're matched. The matching modes stack on top of a whitelist/blacklist toggle:

Filter matching modes
ModeBehavior
WhitelistOnly the listed items match — everything else is ignored.
BlacklistEverything except the listed items matches — great for "void all junk but keep ores."
Tag matchMatch by item tag (e.g. forge:ores) so one entry covers a whole category across mods.
Mod matchMatch every item from a given mod — "accept anything from this mod."
DurabilityDistinguish by damage/durability — useful for tools and gear handling.
Fuzzy / NBTIgnore data-component / NBT differences so variants of an item all match the same rule.
Tag & mod filters scale with your modpack

Instead of listing every ore by hand, use a tag like forge:ores or mod match. As you add mods, the filter automatically covers their items too — one well-chosen tag entry replaces dozens of manual ones.

The Stack Upgrade

The Stack Upgrade raises the stack size each slot can hold — a single slot stores many vanilla stacks. Stack the upgrade itself (and use higher multipliers) and a modest 5×9 grid of slots can hold the loot of an entire mining session. This is what makes the pack feel "bottomless": you're not adding many slots, you're making each slot hold far more.

Stacking interacts with everything

Stack Upgrades multiply capacity for all contents, which is why a mining bag with a couple of Stack Upgrades plus a Void filter can run for an hour without filling. But each Stack Upgrade takes an upgrade slot — on lower tiers you'll trade automation slots for capacity, so plan the balance.

05 — The bag does the work

Automation in a Bag

Slot the right upgrades together and the backpack stops being passive storage and starts running processes while you simply walk around. These are the combinations that earn the mod its reputation.

The walking item filter

Magnet + Pickup + Void is the classic combo. The magnet drags every nearby drop toward you, pickup funnels it into the bag, and a blacklisted Void upgrade deletes the junk on the way in — so you sweep a mob farm or mining tunnel and only the items you actually want survive. Add an Advanced Magnet's filter and you have a self-walking sorter.

Auto-eat while you fight

The Feeding Upgrade watches your hunger and auto-eats food from the bag when it drops. Filter it to your preferred food (steak, not those spare golden carrots) and you never stop to eat mid-fight or mid-build. Pair with Restock so your hotbar food never empties either.

Ore runs without a base

Two processing chains turn a mining trip into finished product:

  • Auto-Smelting cooks ores into ingots as you pick them up — you come home with ingots, not raw ore (feed it fuel or a Battery Upgrade if the install supports it).
  • Compacting then folds nine ingots into a block, so the bag stores them at a ninth of the footprint. With a Stack Upgrade on top, the capacity is staggering.

Interacting with chests

Restock and Deposit are the bag's hands. Restock keeps your inventory topped from the bag (torches, blocks, ammo). Deposit pushes matching items out into any chest you open — so unloading at base is one click, and a filtered Deposit only dumps the categories you've sorted to that chest.

Order your upgrades intentionally

Some upgrades act in slot order — for example, you generally want a Void upgrade to process junk before a Stack/Compacting upgrade wastes space on it. If a combo isn't behaving, try reordering the cards in the upgrade column; the GUI applies them top-to-bottom.

06 — On your back

Putting It On Your Back

A backpack you have to hold in your hand or hotbar is half the value. The mod integrates with a dedicated back slot so the pack is worn — freeing your inventory and letting you open it with a keybind from anywhere.

The back-slot integration

  • Curios / back slot. With Curios API installed (very common on 1.19.2 Forge packs), the backpack equips into the back Curios slot. It renders on your character and no longer occupies an inventory slot.
  • Open with a keybind. A configurable keybind opens the worn backpack instantly — no need to take it off or dig through your inventory. This is the single biggest quality-of-life win.
  • Works without Curios too. Without the back slot, the bag still functions from your inventory or hand; the keybind opens whichever bag it finds. Curios just makes it seamless.

Synergies once it's worn

A worn pack runs its upgrades continuously in the background. Magnet, Pickup, Feeding, Restock and Tool Swapper all operate while the bag sits on your back — so the automation in §05 only truly shines once the pack is equipped rather than carried. The Everlasting-style keep-on-death behaviors that some packs configure (or that companion mods provide) pair well here: lose your inventory, keep your loaded backpack.

Set the open keybind early

Bind the "open backpack" key the moment you craft your first pack. Once it's muscle memory you'll treat the backpack as a second inventory you carry everywhere — and the worn pack's passive upgrades (magnet, feeding) just work without you thinking about them.

07 — Loadouts

Tips & Build Ideas

Because upgrade slots are limited, the best play is several purpose-built packs rather than one overloaded bag. Here are three proven loadouts and how the mod compares to its main rival.

The mining backpack

  • Magnet sweep drops
  • Void junk (cobble/dirt)
  • Auto-Smelt ore→ingot
  • Compacting ingot→block
  • Stack ×N capacity

Drop down a shaft and strip-mine for an hour: the magnet grabs every drop, Void deletes the cobble, Auto-Smelting cooks the ore, Compacting folds ingots into blocks, and Stack Upgrades make the whole haul fit. You return with smelted, compacted, sorted loot and a bag that's barely full.

The combat backpack

  • Feeding auto-eat
  • Restock top up hotbar
  • Magnet grab mob drops
  • Pickup straight to bag
  • Tool Swapper right weapon

Built for mob farms and dungeon dives: Feeding keeps your hunger up so you never stop fighting, Restock refills arrows/potions, the magnet+pickup pair hoovers drops, and Tool Swapper makes sure you're swinging the right weapon. You can fight indefinitely without opening a menu.

How it compares to Traveler's Backpack

The other big backpack mod, Traveler's Backpack, leans into built-in tanks and tools — large fluid tanks, a crafting grid, and optional sleeping-bag/abilities baked into specific themed backpacks. Sophisticated Backpacks instead leans into a modular upgrade system: a smaller base bag whose behavior you compose from cards, with deep filter logic and in-bag processing (smelting, compacting, voiding). Roughly: Traveler's is a fixed Swiss-army-bag, Sophisticated is a programmable one. For a side-by-side of both within a broader storage strategy, see the Storage hub cross-reference.

When in doubt, trust the GUI

Slot counts, upgrade caps, and exact filter modes vary slightly by version and pack config. The in-game backpack GUI and JEI are ground truth — the numbers here are approximate and meant to orient you, not to override what your install shows.
