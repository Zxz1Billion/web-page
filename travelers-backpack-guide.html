The Traveler's
Kit

A guide to Traveler's Backpack — not a wall of upgrade chips, but a single rich pack you wear in a dedicated back slot. It carries a wide inventory, two built-in fluid tanks, an on-board 3×3 crafting grid, tool slots, a roll-out sleeping bag, and a configurable hose for moving liquids. Themed variants add material-based abilities, and the Ender backpack ties straight into your ender chest. One pack, worn for the whole expedition.

Traveler's Backpack MC 1.19.2 · Forge Back slot · two tanks · crafting + Curios-friendly

00 — Orientation

Start Here

Where Sophisticated Backpacks gives you a plain bag you customise with modular upgrade chips, Traveler's Backpack hands you one feature-packed pack out of the gate. You equip it in a real back slot — it renders on your character — and it comes loaded with conveniences that make it a self-contained expedition kit rather than just extra slots.

slot

Worn on your back

Occupies a dedicated back slot (a built-in slot, or a Curios slot if Curios is installed) — it shows on your model and stays equipped while you adventure.

fluids

Two fluid tanks

Left and right tanks store water, lava, milk, or any modded fluid, filled from buckets or the world and tapped with the hose.

craft

On-board crafting

A full 3×3 crafting grid lives inside the GUI, so you can build on the go without a placed table.

camp

Sleeping bag & tools

Roll out a sleeping bag to skip the night anywhere, plus dedicated tool slots for the gear you reach for most.

The one idea to hold onto

Traveler's Backpack is rich-but-singular: the value is in everything that's already inside one pack — storage, two tanks, a crafting grid, tool slots, and a sleeping bag — plus a material/theme that grants an ability. You don't assemble it from modules; you pick (or upgrade to) the pack whose tier and theme fit the trip.

What a fresh pack gives you

  1. Inventory. A large grid of storage slots — the core of the pack and the reason you craft it first.
  2. Two tanks. Independent left/right fluid tanks for water, lava, milk, potions, or modded fluids.
  3. A crafting grid. A 3×3 grid right in the GUI for field crafting.
  4. Tool slots. Quick-access slots for tools so they don't clutter your main inventory.
  5. A sleeping bag. Deploy it to set your spawn and skip night without carrying a bed.
  6. An ability. Tied to the pack's material/theme — fire resistance, water breathing via the hose, regeneration, and so on.
Scope of this guide

This covers the everyday loop: wearing the pack, the tanks and hose, the tier/theme ladder, abilities, the sleeping bag, and crafting/upgrading. Exact stack counts, tank volumes, and which themes carry which ability vary by version and pack config — the in-game GUI and JEI are always ground truth.

01 — The fundamentals

Wearing & Using

The pack lives in a back slot and is opened from there with a keybind — no need to dig it out of your hotbar. You can also place it as a block in the world to access its contents (and sleep) without wearing it, which makes it double as a quick chest at camp.

Equipping & opening

  • Equip it by placing the pack in the back slot — the mod adds a dedicated slot (left of the player model in the inventory), or uses the Back Curios slot when Curios is installed.
  • Open it on the move with the configurable Open Backpack keybind (default B) — the full GUI pops up wherever you are.
  • Place it as a block to set it down; right-click the placed pack to open the same inventory, or sneak-break it to pick it back up with contents intact.
Two ways to carry, one pack

Worn, the pack is always with you and its abilities are active. Placed, it's a stationary access point — handy for a base chest, a temporary stash on a dig, or just so you can sit down and sleep next to it. The contents are the same either way.

The two fluid tanks

The pack's signature feature is its pair of tanks. Each holds a single fluid and is filled in several ways:

  • From buckets — drop a filled bucket into a tank's input slot to empty it in; an empty bucket pulls fluid back out.
  • From the world — use the hose (§02) to suck water, lava, or other source blocks straight into a tank.
  • By output — drain a tank into buckets or spill it back into the world with the hose.

Two tanks means you can carry, say, water in one and lava in the other — handy for an instant cobblestone/obsidian generator, dousing yourself, or keeping milk on hand to clear effects.

Pick up before you break

When picking the pack up — whether worn or placed — make sure you collect the pack item itself, not just empty it. A placed pack sneak-broken drops as an item with its inventory and tank contents preserved, so you don't lose your lava. Don't let it despawn after a death; grab it quickly like any valuable container.

02 — The fluid tool

The Hose

The hose is the tool that makes the tanks worth carrying. It's a configurable nozzle wired to your pack's tanks: switch its mode and which tank it draws from, then point it at the world to suck up fluids, spill them out, or drink straight from a tank.

What the hose does

Hose modes (cycle with the hose's mode key/right-click)
ModeEffect
SuckPull a fluid source block from the world into the selected tank — fill from a lake, lava pool, or a placed source.
SpillPlace the selected tank's fluid back into the world as a source block — instant water/lava on demand.
DrinkConsume from the tank directly — drink water to hydrate, milk to clear effects, or other drinkable fluids.

Choosing a tank

Because the pack has two tanks, the hose lets you select which tank it's connected to before acting. So one tool serves both reservoirs: suck water into the left tank, switch the hose to the right tank, suck lava — then spill whichever you need wherever you stand.

Field tricks worth memorising

Water: spill a source to make a quick farm pool, douse fire, or create a mob-soft-landing. Lava: spill + water for a cobble/obsidian generator, or as a weapon/lighting. Milk: keep a milk tank and use Drink mode to instantly strip away poison, wither, or nausea mid-fight. The hose turns the pack into a portable fluid utility belt.

Lava in a tank is still lava

A lava tank doesn't protect you from lava you spill — you can absolutely set yourself on fire. Pair a lava theme with a fire/lava-resistance ability (§04) before you start landscaping with the hose, and keep the water tank ready as a panic button.

03 — The range

Tiers & Themes

Traveler's Backpacks come in two overlapping flavours: material tiers that scale storage and tank size, and themed/cosmetic variants that change the look and, for the special ones, grant an ability. Most installs let you craft a base pack and dye/upgrade it toward the higher tiers.

Material tiers

The standard ladder climbs roughly with vanilla material progression — more inventory and bigger tanks the higher you go:

Themed & special variants

Alongside the plain material packs, the mod ships a wide set of themed backpacks — many are purely cosmetic (mob and biome flavours), while a few carry distinct functionality:

Diamond / NetheriteThe premium material packs, also presented as distinct themed variants with top-end capacity.

The Ender backpack is the standout

The Ender theme is the one most players go out of their way for: because it mirrors your ender chest, anything you drop in is reachable from every other ender chest you own. It's effectively a portable shared-storage portal — perfect for shuttling ores home from a mining trip without ever leaving the cave.

04 — Material perks

Abilities

The special packs don't just look different — many grant a passive or toggled ability tied to their material or theme. These are what turn the backpack from "more slots" into genuine survival gear, and most can be switched on and off while the pack is on your back.

The on-back toggle

Abilities only apply while the pack is worn. The mod provides an ability toggle key so you can enable or disable a pack's effect on the fly — useful when an effect (like constant water-spilling or a buff that costs a tank fluid) isn't wanted at that moment, or to save the resource it consumes.

Some abilities drink your tank

Several effects consume fluid from the pack's tanks while active — water breathing pulls from a water tank, for example. If an ability seems to stop, check that the relevant tank isn't empty, and use the hose to top it up. The toggle key lets you turn the effect off to conserve fluid when you don't need it.

Trust the tooltip

The exact roster of abilities and which themed pack grants each one shifts between mod versions and is sometimes tweaked by modpacks. Hover the pack in JEI or read its in-game tooltip/GUI to confirm what your specific pack actually does before you rely on it in the field.

05 — Camp anywhere

Sleeping Bag & Travel

Every Traveler's Backpack carries a built-in sleeping bag — deploy it to sleep without hauling a bed around. For long expeditions far from base, this is the feature that justifies the pack's name: you can always set the night to morning and reset your respawn point wherever you've wandered.

Using the sleeping bag

  • Deploy the bag from the pack (a button in the GUI, or by placing the pack and interacting) to roll it out on the ground in front of you.
  • Sleep in it as you would a bed at night — skipping to morning and (depending on config) setting your spawn to that spot.
  • Retract it afterwards; the bag packs back into the pack so you carry nothing extra.
Why it matters on long trips

On a multi-day mining run, a deep dungeon crawl, or a trip to another dimension/planet, you rarely have a bed handy and don't want to lose the night to mob spam. Roll out the bag, skip to morning, set your spawn, and keep moving. It's the single biggest quality-of-life win the pack offers for exploration.

Spawn-set behaviour is config-dependent

Whether the sleeping bag sets your respawn point (versus only skipping night) is a setting some packs toggle, and dimension rules (the Nether/End won't let you "sleep" normally) still apply. If your respawn didn't move, check the mod config or test in a safe spot before betting your inventory on it.

06 — Building & growing

Crafting & Upgrades

You craft the first pack at a normal crafting table from the era-appropriate material, then climb the tier ladder or branch into a theme. The crafting grid inside the pack means that once you have one, you can keep building on the move.

Crafting your first pack

  1. Gather the base materials. A leather pack is the entry point — leather plus a few common items (string, the tank components, and a sleeping-bag/wool element) in a crafting table.
  2. Craft the pack at a table; the result is a wearable backpack with its inventory, both tanks, the crafting grid, tool slots, and sleeping bag already built in.
  3. Equip it in the back slot and open it with the keybind to confirm everything's there.
  4. Fill the tanks from buckets or with the hose, and you're ready to travel.
Always check the recipe in JEI

Exact recipes — base pack, each tier, every themed variant, and the hose — differ by version and are sometimes adjusted by modpacks. Look the item up in JEI/EMI in your own world rather than trusting a fixed recipe; the mod also provides recipes for crafting themed packs and dyeing plain ones.

Upgrading between tiers

  • Tier up by combining a lower pack with the next material (e.g. surrounding/combining it with iron, then gold, diamond, netherite) to grow inventory and tank size while keeping contents.
  • Re-skin / theme by crafting the themed variant or dyeing a plain pack — themes change appearance and, for special packs, the ability.
  • Customise with the mod's options (dye colours, and the tool/tank layout you keep things in) to suit how you travel.
Upgrade with the pack empty (or carefully)

When combining a pack into a higher tier, make sure the upgrade preserves your inventory and tank fluids in your version — many do, but it's safest to verify on a spare pack or empty a precious one first. Never throw an upgrade craft together with your only loot inside until you trust the recipe.

07 — Picking the right pack

Tips & Comparison

Both backpack mods in this pack are excellent, and they solve different problems. Traveler's Backpack is the exploration kit; Sophisticated Backpacks is the modular automation kit. Knowing which to reach for saves you a lot of fiddling.

Traveler's vs. Sophisticated

Which back-slot mod for which job
If you want…Reach for
Built-in tanks, a hose, field crafting, a sleeping bag, and abilities for travelTraveler's Backpack
A blank bag you tune with upgrade chips (magnet, feeding, auto-smelt, crafting, pickup filters, stacking)Sophisticated Backpacks
Fluids carried on your back (water/lava/milk) and quick liquid placementTraveler's Backpack
In-place item automation — auto-pickup, filtering, void, and feeding while you mineSophisticated Backpacks
To sleep anywhere on a long dungeon/space trip without a bedTraveler's Backpack
To slot more upgrades over time and grow capacity modularlySophisticated Backpacks

Practical advice

  • Carry Traveler's for expeditions. Mining trips, dungeon dives, and space/dimension travel benefit most from its tanks, hose, sleeping bag, and abilities.
  • Carry Sophisticated for base-loop work. When you want auto-pickup, filters, and feeding while farming or grinding, the modular upgrades win.
  • The Ender pack is a power tool. Pair it with mining for instant ore-shipping home; it removes most return trips.
  • Match theme to task. Fire-resistance theme for Nether/lava work, water theme for ocean diving, before you commit to the trip.

★ — The path

Progression — kitting out the kit

Traveler's Backpack rewards a clear arc: craft one humble pack, learn what already lives inside it, then climb the tiers and pick a theme that matches where you're headed. By the end the pack stops being "extra slots" and becomes the base camp you carry on your back. Here's a route that gets you there without wasting loot on upgrades you don't yet trust.

  1. Craft a starter and wear it. Build a leather/standard pack at a table and equip it in the back slot. This single craft already hands you storage, both tanks, the crafting grid, tool slots, and a sleeping bag — you're not assembling modules, you're growing one pack.
  2. Learn the on-board kit. Open the GUI and use everything once: fill the two tanks from buckets, draw fluid with the hose, build something in the 3×3 crafting grid, and roll out the sleeping bag to skip a night. Knowing the pack cold is worth more than any tier jump.
  3. Tier up for storage. Combine your pack upward — iron, then gold, then diamond — to grow inventory and tank volume while keeping your contents. Each step is a comfort upgrade; do it when the bag starts feeling tight, not before.
  4. Pick a themed pack for its ability. Once you know the trip, choose the theme that earns its slot: Ender for ender-chest-anywhere shuttling, Netherite/blaze for fire and lava handling, a diving theme for underwater work. The cosmetic mob packs are fun; the special ones change how you play.
  5. Run it as your expedition base. Out in the field, lean on the whole kit at once: water in one tank for dousing and farms, lava in the other for cobble/obsidian, the hose as your fluid belt, and the sleeping bag to reset spawn and skip night wherever you wander.
  6. Match the pack to the next trip. Before each expedition, swap to the tier and theme that fit — a fire pack and a lava tank for the Nether, a diving pack and a water tank for the ocean — so the kit is always tuned to the terrain.
Why this path, vs. Sophisticated

This progression leans into what makes Traveler's Backpack an exploration kit: built-in tanks, a hose, field crafting, and a sleeping bag mean you can travel and camp self-contained, no return trips and no placed table. If instead you want a bag you grow with modular upgrade chips, that's Sophisticated's lane — see the Storage hub for the side-by-side, and The Ascent for where this fits in your wider playthrough.
