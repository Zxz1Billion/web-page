Vintage Story · v1.22.3 · The hand crafts

The Four Crafts

Vintage Story has no all-purpose 3×3 crafting table for the things that matter. Most early gear is shaped on a work surface. You chip a stone, stack clay voxel by voxel, or hammer glowing metal, following a blueprint from the Survival Handbook. These four minigames are the spine of the tech tree: knapping, clay forming, smithing and casting.

🪨 Knapping 🏺 Clay forming 🔨 Smithing 🫗 Casting

00 — Orientation

Overview

Where Minecraft hands you items from a recipe grid, Vintage Story makes you form them. Each of the four crafts is a small voxel puzzle. Which one you use depends on the material and the age you're in.

stone age

🪨 Knapping

Chip a loose stone into a tool head. This makes your first knife, axe, hoe, shovel and spear. No fire or metal required.

pottery

🏺 Clay forming

Build pottery up voxel by voxel: bowls, jugs, storage vessels, ingot & tool molds, and the all-important crucible. Everything is then fired in a pit kiln.

iron & steel

🔨 Smithing

Heat an ingot in a forge until it glows, set it on an anvil, and hammer it to a blueprint. The only way to shape iron and steel.

copper & bronze

🫗 Casting

Melt copper or bronze in a crucible and pour the liquid metal into clay molds. The fast route to early metal tools, and how you cast your first anvil.

The dividing line: cast vs. smithed

Copper and bronze are CAST. They melt at temperatures a crucible can reach, so you pour them into molds. Iron and steel are SMITHED. They melt too hot to cast practically, so you work them solid (but glowing) on the anvil with a hammer. Know which metal does which and you'll avoid confusion at the forge.

If you've just spawned, start at the stone age. The First Day walks through knapping your first knife and axe. For the full Stone→Copper→Bronze→Iron→Steel ladder these crafts climb, see The Long Road. For smelting and alloys specifically, see the metalworking guide.

01 — Stone by hand

Knapping

Knapping is the first craft anyone does. You lay a loose stone flat as a grid of voxels and chip away everything that isn't the tool, leaving the head behind. No fire and no workbench, just a stone, a surface, and a steady left mouse button.

How it works

  1. Place a knapping surface. With a loose stone selected on your hotbar, hold Shift and click the top of a solid block. The stone lays out as a flat voxel grid.
  2. Chip to the shape. Left-click the voxels you want removed. You're carving negative space. Chip everything outside the blueprint until only the target shape remains.
  3. The head pops off. When the pattern is complete, the finished tool head (a Knife Blade, Axe Head, etc.) drops into your inventory.
  4. Assemble the tool. Press E and combine the head + a stick in the 4-slot crafting grid to finish a usable tool.
Flint is special

Only flint can knap arrowheads. Every knappable stone (flint, obsidian, chert, granite, andesite, peridotite, basalt) makes the other heads — knife, axe, hoe, shovel, spear — but flint and obsidian heads are the sharpest and longest-lasting. Grab flint whenever you see it near gravel and water.

You need two stones to start, not one

Knapping consumes two stones per surface: one knappable stone (flint or an igneous rock) that becomes the tool head, plus one extra loose stone as the working base it's chipped on. Carry a small stack of plain stones so you're never stuck with material but no surface.

Which stone, and why obsidian wins

Tool-head quality scales with the stone. The knappable stones, in order of resulting strength:

  • Obsidian — the strongest stone head, sharper and longer-lived than flint. Rare (volcanic glass), so save it for the tools you use most.
  • Flint — the only stone that can knap arrowheads, and a top-quality material for every other head. Your bread-and-butter stone-age stone; found loose near gravel, sand and water.
  • Igneous & other rock — chert, granite, andesite, basalt, peridotite. Knap every head except arrowheads (flint-only), just at lower durability than flint or obsidian.
There's no undo — but there's a reset

Knapping has no undo on a single chip. If you remove a voxel the blueprint wanted kept, the recipe is spoiled and the surface is wasted (you lose that one knappable stone). The fix is cheap: stones are everywhere, so just lay a fresh surface and try again. Go slowly on the first few — chip the obvious outer corners first, then work inward toward the edge of the shape.

Read the blueprint, not this page

Knapping patterns are precise and can shift slightly between versions. Hover the recipe and press H for the canonical outline before you start chipping. The viewer above is a teaching aid, not a pixel-perfect spec.

A clean knapping routine

  1. Open the recipe picker first. After laying the surface, a list of valid recipes appears (filtered by the stone you're using — flint shows knife and axe, others don't). Pick the head you want before chipping.
  2. Chip the corners and outer field. The voxels furthest from the shape are always "remove." Clear them first to reveal the silhouette.
  3. Trim the edge last. The cells right next to the kept shape are where mistakes spoil a recipe. Slow down here.
  4. Combine immediately. Press E and drop the head + a stick (a bone also works for some heads) into the 4-slot grid.
Knap in bulk for arrows

Arrow and spear heads are cheap and you'll burn through arrows fast. When you find a flint or chert patch, knap a whole stack of heads in one sitting so fletching later is just heads + sticks + feathers at the grid.

02 — Building with earth

Clay Forming

Clay forming is the opposite of knapping. Instead of chipping away, you add voxels, building a vessel up layer by layer until it matches the blueprint. It's how you make every piece of pottery, including the crucible and molds that the metal crafts depend on.

How it works

  1. Dig clay. Find clay deposits (often near water) and dig red, blue, or fire clay. Fire clay is the heat-tolerant kind needed for crucibles and molds.
  2. Start a clay form. Select clay in hand and Shift-click a surface to lay out a form, then pick the item you want from the recipe list.
  3. Add the voxels. Left-click adds a voxel where the blueprint calls for one. Right-click removes. Work the build up one layer at a time until it's complete.
  4. Fire it in a pit kiln. Stack the unfired item with dry grass plus firewood/peat/coal over it and light it. After roughly 10–20 in-game hours it hardens into usable, watertight ceramic.
Fire clay for anything that meets metal

Ordinary red/blue clay is fine for bowls, jugs and storage. But a crucible and molds have to survive molten-metal heat, so they must be made from fire clay. A crucible is cheap, roughly 2 clay → 1 crucible (or about 13 clay → 4 in a batch), and fired crucibles are what you'll smelt copper and bronze in.

Green to keep, orange to clear

The clay form shows you the current layer's blueprint with coloured voxels:

  • Green voxels = clay the layer needs. Left-click adds a voxel here.
  • Orange voxels = clay that must be removed from this layer. Right-click clears it.
  • When a layer is complete, the next layer appears above it automatically. You always work bottom to top, one course at a time.
  • When the last layer is finished, the dialog closes and the finished (unfired) item jumps into your inventory.
One clay chunk goes a long way

A single dug clay block stacks and forms many voxels. You set how many copies of an item the form makes before you start (more clay in hand = more output), which is why a crucible can come out 2-for-2 clay singly but ~13 clay for 4 in a batch — batching is slightly more clay-efficient and far faster than forming one at a time.

Layer explorer — a crucible

Clay forming is additive, bottom to top, so this viewer steps through Y-layers to mirror how you build the piece in-game. Step up from the solid base disk through the rising ring walls.

Batch your firing

A pit kiln can fire many items at once, and it takes the same hours either way. Form a whole stack of bowls, molds and crucibles, then fire them all in one overnight burn rather than one at a time.

Building and firing a pit kiln

An unfired clay item is fragile and useless until baked. The pit kiln is the stone-age way to fire pottery:

  1. Dig a 1-block hole (or use a corner) and place the raw item(s) at the bottom. Many items can share one kiln.
  2. Stack the fuel layers on top: dry grass, then sticks (up to 8 are accepted), then fuel — firewood, peat, or coal. A kiln usually needs about 4 pieces of fuel; bulky items like a storage vessel need two fuel layers to fully cover.
  3. Light it. The pile must completely cover the pottery or it won't fire.
  4. Wait it out. Firing takes ~10–20 in-game hours depending on fuel: firewood is slowest, charcoal is fastest. When the smoke clears, dig out the hardened ceramic.
Pit kiln fuel — same result, different speed

Rain ruins a pit kiln

An open pit kiln can be doused by rain before it finishes. Build under an overhang, a roof, or at least a one-block lip, and check the weather before committing a stack of crucibles to an overnight burn. The later beehive kiln is enclosed and weatherproof, and fires bricks/tiles in larger batches once you've got the materials for it.

03 — Hammer & anvil

Smithing

Smelting metal isn't enough to make a tool. For iron and steel, you have to shape it solid. Heat an ingot in a forge until it glows, lay it on an anvil, and hammer it to a voxel blueprint, one strike at a time. It's the most involved of the four crafts and the gateway to the late game.

How it works

  1. Heat the metal. Put an ingot in a forge and wait until it glows. Dull red is too cold, orange→yellow is working temperature. White means you're about to ruin it.
  2. Set it on the anvil. Place the hot ingot on an anvil of the right tier and the blueprint appears as a voxel grid.
  3. Choose a hammer op & strike. Select an operation, either flatten/hit (push down), upset (push material inward), or split. The recipe shows which voxels to add or remove. Hammer until the shape matches.
  4. Reheat as needed. Metal cools fast and can't be worked cold. Put it back in the forge to reheat and keep going. Bigger pieces need several trips.
Tier your anvil to your metal

Bigger and harder work needs a stronger anvil. The progression runs copper → bronze → iron → steel anvils, each able to shape its own tier and one tier above. A bronze anvil works iron, and an iron anvil works steel. Your first anvil is usually cast in copper or bronze (see Casting) before you ever smith iron on it.

Read the heat colour

Metal only moves while it's hot enough. As a rule, a metal becomes workable above about 50% of its melting point, and the glow tells you where you are. Watch the colour, not the clock:

Heat colour → what it means at the anvil
GlowStateDo
Dull grey / blackToo coldStop — striking now spoils the work. Reheat in the forge.

Iron blooms are the exception

A raw iron bloom can be hammered (to consolidate it into ingots) at a much lower bar — roughly above 700°C — and lead works at essentially any temperature. Everything else wants that orange-to-yellow window.

The four hammer operations

Each ingot is 42 voxels of metal; a plate is 81. You don't add material at the anvil, you move and remove it, so plan the shape around the voxels you start with:

  • Hit / flatten — pushes the top voxel down a layer. The everyday "knock it flat" stroke.
  • Upset (directional: up/left/down/right) — pushes voxels inward from an edge to pile material where you need it.
  • Split — permanently removes a voxel. Use it to cut notches, eyes and tips. There's no putting a split voxel back.
  • Right-click on the recipe toggles the plan view so you can see which voxels still need adding (green) versus removing.

What's new in 1.22

  • Metal tongs. Hold hot work safely and move it between forge and anvil without burning your hands.
  • A new forge with bellows. Pump the bellows to drive the fire hotter and heat metal faster. Without a worked bellows, forge fuel reaches only about 60% of its rated temperature.
  • Quenching & tempering. Dunk a finished tool head in water to quench, then temper it to trade some durability for extra working power. A heat-treatment loop on tool heads.

Fixing a botched piece

Smithing forgives more than knapping does. A few rules keep your metal recoverable:

  • Progress is saved. Lifting a half-finished work item off the anvil and putting it back does not lose your progress — that's how you reheat. Pop it in the forge, bring it back to orange, return it to the anvil.
  • Spoiled it anyway? A ruined or unwanted work item can be chiselled back into metal bits and re-smelted in a crucible into a fresh ingot. You lose time, not material.
  • Don't strike cold metal. Hammering once the glow fades to grey is the single most common way to wreck a piece. When in doubt, reheat.
Recover the metal, not the regret

Because work items chisel back into bits and bits re-smelt into ingots, the only thing a smithing mistake truly costs is fuel and time. Practise the harder shapes (axe, pickaxe) on cheap copper before you commit a hard-won steel ingot.

Blueprint explorer

Step through a few simple anvil blueprints. Filled cells are hot-metal voxels that must be present in the final shape. Flatten, upset and split until the ingot matches.

Automate the grind

Hammering by hand is slow and repetitive. Once you have rotational power, a helve hammer can do the striking for you. See the mechanical power guide for building one off a windmill or water wheel.

04 — Pour & cool

Casting

Casting is the quickest path to metal tools, and the only way to shape copper and bronze. Melt the metal fully liquid in a crucible and pour it into clay molds, then let it cool into a solid part. No hammering and no blueprint puzzle. Heat, pour, wait, extract.

How it works

  1. Melt the metal. Load copper or bronze (ore or bits) into a fired crucible and heat it in a fire pit or forge until it's fully molten.
  2. Place a mold. Set a fired clay mold on the ground, either an ingot mold (one ingot each; two fit per block) or a tool mold (axe, pickaxe, knife, hoe and more).
  3. Pour. Right-click the mold with the hot crucible to fill it with liquid metal.
  4. Cool & extract. Wait for the metal to solidify, then take the finished ingot or tool head out. The clay molds are reusable, so pour again and again.
You cast your first anvil, too

An anvil is itself cast. Pour copper or bronze into an anvil mold to make the first one. That cast anvil is what lets you smith iron and steel later, so casting bootstraps smithing.

Units, nuggets and ingots

Casting runs on a single currency: units of metal. Everything converts cleanly:

  • 1 ore nugget = 5 units.
  • 1 ingot = 100 units = 20 nuggets.
  • A crucible holds up to 4 stacks of 128 nuggets = 2,560 units of one metal/alloy at a time — enough for 25 ingots in a single melt.
  • Most molds need 100 units to fill (one ingot's worth). Tool and weapon heads are all 100 units.

Plan the anvil pour

An anvil swallows 900 units = 180 nuggets = 9 ingots. Don't try to top it up from dribs and drabs — fill a crucible with at least that much copper or bronze and pour it in one go, or the metal cools between pours and you waste a reheat.

Cast · Smithed · Knapped

Three ways to make a tool head, with different materials and motions. The whole page in one glance:

poured

🫗 Cast

Copper & bronze. Melt in a crucible, pour into a clay mold, cool, extract. Reusable molds, no minigame. A low melting point makes this practical.

hammered

🔨 Smithed

Iron & steel. Heat to glowing, then hammer solid metal on an anvil to a voxel blueprint, reheating as it cools. Too high-melting to cast.

chipped

🪨 Knapped

Stone. Lay a loose stone flat and chip away the negative space until the tool head remains. No heat at all, the stone-age craft.

Shapeless vs shaped

Most recipes here are shapeless — drop the parts anywhere in the grid. Shaped ones depend on placement: rotate the saw around a plank for slabs and stairs, and use the chisel with a hammer on placed stone to carve bricks, slabs and gears. Exact counts are in the Handbook (H).

The other recipes aren't here

The voxel crafts above cover the rest: tool heads come from knapping; the crucible, molds, bowls and crocks are clay-formed then pit-fired (not grid-crafted); metal tools are cast or smithed.
