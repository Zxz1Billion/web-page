Vintage Story · v1.22.3 · Food & Farming

The Larder

Eating well in Vintage Story is a system. You don't just stave off a hunger bar; you balance five separate nutrition groups that, kept full, make you tougher. The loop: forage and hunt to survive, till soil and rotate crops to thrive, then cook it all into meals that last. A varied diet raises your max health.

00 — Two systems at once

Saturation & Nutrition

Hunger in Vintage Story is two stacked systems. You manage saturation, the bar that drains as you move, mine, and fight, and five separate nutrition bars. Keep them all topped up. Letting any run dry costs you.

Saturation — the hunger bar

The short-term fuel gauge. Every action drains it; eating any food refills it. Run it to empty and you starve, losing health over time. Saturation is the moment-to-moment number.

Nutrition — five slow bars

Under saturation sit five long-term bars, one per food group. Eating a food adds satiety and tops up the nutrition bar(s) of its group. A fuller, more even set of bars raises your maximum health. Eating one group leaves you frail.

Base max health
15 HP
Per full group
+2.5 HP × 5
Max possible
27.5 HP (all full)
Saturation cap
1500 satiety

You start at 15 HP. Each of the five nutrition bars kept full adds +2.5 max HP, so a fully-nourished seraph reaches about 27.5 HP, nearly double. You gain 40 nutrition (of a food's group) for every 100 saturation it restores, and gain none if you eat while already full. Saturation tops out at 1500.

The one rule that matters

Eat the rainbow. Saturation asks "are you full?"; nutrition asks "are you eating variety?" The higher-max-health character has five reasonably full bars, not forty berries. Every figure here is collected in The Codex.

01 — Eat the rainbow

The Five Nutrition Groups

Every edible item belongs to one or more of five groups: Fruit, Vegetable, Protein, Grain and Dairy. Each group fills its own bar as you eat foods of that type. The combined fullness of those bars sets your max health. A balanced eater is harder to kill; someone living on a single group stays frail no matter how stuffed.

The five nutrition groups and where to find them
GroupExample foodsEasiest early source
FruitBerries (many bush types), tree fruitForage berry bushes
VegetableTurnip, carrot, cabbage, onion, mushrooms, cattail rootForage cattail roots & safe mushrooms

Dairy is the slow one

Fruit, vegetable, protein, and grain are all reachable in your first days. Dairy lags. It needs tamed, milkable animals and a churn/cheese workflow, so that bar trails the others for a while. Bring it up once your homestead is established.

02 — Before you farm

Foraging & Hunting

Your first meals come from the land as it is. Foraging covers four nutrition groups before you've tilled any soil; a little hunting handles protein.

forage · fruit

Berries

Right-click a berry bush to pick it without destroying the plant. It will fruit again. Breaking the bush kills it, so harvest, don't smash.

forage · veg

Cattails & roots

Cut cattails at the water's edge for cattail roots (roast them for veg nutrition) plus tops that double as cordage.

forage · veg

Mushrooms

Common and good veg, but some are poisonous. Press H and check the Handbook before eating an unfamiliar one. Cook the safe ones.

forage · seeds

Wild crops & seeds

Wild grasses and crops drop the first seeds you'll plant. Gather them now; they bootstrap your farm.

hunt · protein

Hares & chickens

Easy first prey. Kill them, then butcher with a knife for raw meat (cook it before eating), plus hide and fat.

hunt · protein

Deer & bigger game

More meat, more risk. Larger animals reward the same knife-butchering with hide and fat for tallow, leather, and candles later.

Always butcher with a knife — and cook the meat

Hitting a corpse without a knife wastes most of the carcass. The knife yields raw meat, hide, and fat. Raw meat can make you ill, so roast it on a fire pit first. For where animals live and how to hunt them safely, see The Bestiary.

03 — Grow your own

Farming & Soil Fertility

To farm, take a hoe to grass-covered soil to till it into farmland, plant a seed, and let water (or rain) plus warmth grow it. Soil isn't infinite. Each plot tracks three nutrients, and crops eat the one they prefer.

The three soil nutrients

Every farmland plot carries its own levels of three nutrients. Different crops drain different ones. If any nutrient a crop needs hits 0%, that crop stops growing until it recovers.

N

Nitrogen

Drained by turnip & cabbage. Replenish with saltpeter.

P

Phosphorus

Drained by parsnip, onion & pumpkin. Replenish with bone meal.

K

Potassium

Drained by carrot, the grains (spelt, rye, rice), flax & soybean. Replenish with potash.

Don't trust the "leafy/root/grain" rule of thumb

Vintage Story assigns a nutrient per crop. It does not follow the "leafy = N, root = P, grain = K" heuristic many guides repeat. Carrots eat potassium but turnips eat nitrogen (two roots, different nutrients), while the grains (spelt, rye, rice) and flax all eat potassium. Check the table below, not the rule of thumb.

Crop rotation

Each crop drains mostly one nutrient, so alternate. Plant a nitrogen-hungry crop, then a phosphorus crop, then a potassium crop. The two nutrients a given crop isn't using recover between plantings.

A correct N → P → K rotation on one plot: turnip / cabbage (eat Nitrogen) → parsnip / onion (eat Phosphorus) → carrot / rice (eat Potassium) → repeat Each step drains a different nutrient, so the other two recover before they're tapped again.

Fertilizers & soil tiers

Don't wait for nature. Apply fertilizer pre-emptively to offset what a crop will consume, rather than waiting for a bar to bottom out.

Fertilizers — what each one restores
FertilizerRestoresUse for
Saltpeter+ NitrogenLeafy crops
Bone meal+ PhosphorusRoot crops
Potash+ PotassiumGrains & flax
Compost+ all three, evenlyGeneral top-up between rotations

Soil comes in tiers that cap maximum fertility: bony 0% · barren 5% · low 25% · medium 50% · high 65% · terra preta 80% (the richest). A plot regenerates back up to its tier's cap after harvest, so better soil lets you push crops further before nutrients run out. Rotation and fertilizer still matter on every tier. Terra preta is crafted (high-fertility soil + compost + bone meal + charcoal). Compost is rot sealed in a barrel (~20 days; ~64 rot → 16 compost).

Climate & cold-climate farming

Crops care about temperature and season. Flax likes temperate ground, turnips bolt in hot summers, and rice and pumpkin want warmth. Match crops to your biome and the calendar (see The Lay of the Land for climate & seasons). In cold climates, grow indoors. A greenhouse extends your season, covered in The Homestead.

04 — Turn ingredients into meals

Cooking

Raw ingredients keep you alive. Cooked meals are efficient: more satiety per item, better nutrition, and far longer shelf life. Three cookers, each for a different job.

cooker

Fire pit

Your first cooker. Roasts single foods: a piece of meat, a vegetable. Quick, no clay required, and the base everything else is built on.

cooker

Cooking pot

A clay pot, formed and fired, set on a fire pit. Makes multi-ingredient stews, soups, and porridge.

cooker

Clay oven

Bakes bread, dough and other baked goods. Pair it with a quern to turn your grain harvest into loaves.

The cooking pot, in detail

The pot has 4 ingredient slots; each holds up to 6 items (or 6 L of a liquid). The first two slots determine the meal type; the other two are optional ingredients that raise nutrition and satiety. A meal's satiety is the sum of its ingredients, improved by cooking, so a four-slot pot beats eating the same foods raw and separately.

Make your first stew

  1. Form & fire a clay cooking pot. Shape it from clay on a crafting/clay surface, then fire it in a pit kiln (or kiln) until it's a usable pot.
  2. Set the pot on a lit fire pit. The fire pit is the heat source; the pot sits on top of it.
  3. Fill the first two slots to pick the meal type. Two slots of vegetables → vegetable stew; two of meat → meat stew. This choice defines the dish.
  4. Add optional ingredients in slots 3–4. Onion, extra veg, a little grain. Each raises nutrition and satiety. More variety, better meal.
  5. Cook, then collect into bowls. Let it cook, serve it into bowls, and eat. One pot feeds you for several meals, and stew keeps ~6 days.

Bread, from grain to loaf

Don't eat cereal grain as-is. Grind it into flour at a quern (a hand mill, or power it; see The Turning Gear), mix flour into dough, then bake the dough in a clay oven into bread. That's your durable grain-group staple.

04 · The recipe book

Cooking Recipes

Meals are built from ingredient classes, not fixed recipes. Drop compatible items in the cooking pot on a firepit and a meal forms. The pot has 4 ingredient slots; the first two decide the meal type, slots 3–4 are optional extras that raise nutrition and satiety. One pot yields several portions (commonly 6). The Survival Handbook (H) lists every exact combination.

What counts as what

Each slot is filled by an ingredient class, not a specific item. Knowing which class an item falls in is the whole game of cooking.

Reading these cards

The first two slots set the dish, so two of one class is the minimum. Slots 3–4 are optional and exist to add satiety and a second or third nutrition group. Jam is the exception: it demands all four slots — two fruit plus two pours of 0.2 L honey — and cooks 6 servings. Soup needs enough water in the pot to register as soup rather than a stew. Fruits in a jam need not match.

Baking — quern & clay oven

Grind grain to flour at a quern, mix 1 flour + 1 L water into dough, then bake. Every grain type makes dough — spelt, rye, rice, amaranth, sunflower and cassava each give their own bread. Cooking grain into bread raises its satiety roughly +300% over the raw grain, so baking is the efficient grain staple.

Watch the bake — three stages

Dough passes through part-baked → baked → charred. From a fully heated oven that's about 40 s, 60 s and 85 s. Pull it at baked: a perfect loaf restores the most satiety and keeps longest; part-baked gives less, and charred is wasted grain. A firepit can bake but the clay oven does it faster and more evenly.

Pies

A pie is the densest baked good: many ingredients in, several rich servings out. Lay 2 dough on a table to form an empty crust, then add filling. A pie must be filled four times — 2 of the same class per fill, 8 total — before it is full and bakeable. Bake the raw pie in a hot clay oven (one pie at a time).

Partial pies still feed you

You needn't fill all four levels before baking — a partial pie bakes too, just with less filling and lower nutrition than a full eight-ingredient pie. Mixing vegetables and meat instead of one class makes a pot pie. Pies and bread carry the grain group plus whatever filling you chose, so a meat or veg pie tops two bars at once.

Dairy — milk to cheese

Dairy is the late bar. Milk comes from female bighorn sheep and goats, and only after they give birth: hold a bucket on the animal to milk it. Early generations usually refuse — rejection is high at generation 0–1 and drops to none by generation 3, so breed up your herd first. A lactating animal gives about 10 L every day for ~20 days after birthing.

Milk drunk straight gives dairy satiety but spoils in days. Cheese is the point: it keeps for over a year and is usable in cooking. The process runs in a barrel, then on a shelf.

  1. Curdle the milk. Seal 1 pickled vegetable with ~20–25 L of milk in a barrel for 3 days to get curdled milk.
  2. Make curds (cottage cheese). Add 1 salt per 5 L of curdled milk and seal 1 more day. The result is cottage cheese — edible dairy on its own.
  3. Press out the whey. With ~25 L cottage cheese in the barrel, press it with a piece of linen to get a curd bundle. Set the bundle down, add a stick handle, and twist to squeeze out the liquid.
  4. Salt the raw cheese. Unfold the bundle and rub in 5 more salt — that gives raw salted cheese, ready to age.
  5. Age it on a shelf (14–20 days). Place it on a shelf in a cool, enclosed room or cellar. Aged enclosed / lit it becomes cheddar; aged in an open, dark space it becomes blue cheese.

What cheese is good for

Cheese is the only practical way to keep the dairy bar filled long-term, and it stores for years. It also slots into scrambled eggs as an extra. Wax your cheddar for the longest shelf life of any food in the game.

Cook for variety, not just calories

Satiety is the sum of the ingredients, so a full pot feeds well — but different ingredients matter more. A meal with 3 distinct ingredients is hearty, 4 distinct is hefty, and a varied meal refills several nutrition bars at once. Pour liquids with a bucket (up to 1 L per click) or a bowl (0.1–0.2 L) for fine amounts like jam's 0.2 L honey.

Meal strategy

Early: a pot of 2–4 vegetables (forage cattail root and safe mushrooms, then carrots) is the cheapest filling meal — veg stew keeps 6 days and needs no flour, eggs or dairy. Roasted meat covers protein until you can spare it for stew.

Once settled: build hefty (4 distinct) meals that span groups — e.g. a meat stew of meat + onion + carrot + grain, or a soup of water + meat + two vegetables. One hefty pot pushes several nutrition bars at once, which is what raises max health. A meat or veg pie adds the grain bar on top of its filling and yields rich servings for the work.

Make it keep

A cooked pot holds several portions. Move meals into a crock in a cool cellar to stretch shelf life, and turn berry gluts into jam. See Keeping It Fresh.

05 — New in 1.22

Fishing

Version 1.22 added a fishing overhaul: 20+ freshwater fish species with location-specific populations and their own bait and lure preferences. Fish are a renewable source of the protein group. Once you learn a water's species, they're a steady supply.

How to fish

  • Craft your gear. Build rods, lures, and traps. Rods are active fishing; traps work passively while you do other things.
  • Match bait to the catch. Different species favour different bait and lures, and populations vary by body of water. Scout what lives where, then bring the right tackle.
  • Cook or dry the catch. Fish count as protein and can be cooked (roasted or into stew/soup) or dried for preservation.
A standing protein supply

Once your hunting grounds thin out, fishing with traps is the low-effort way to keep the protein bar full. Set traps near your homestead and check them as part of your routine.

06 — Don't let it rot

Keeping It Fresh

Every food spoils over time. Preservation extends shelf life, turning a glut of summer harvest into winter survival. The full preservation system and the cellar build are in The Homestead. Here's the short version.

salt

Salting

Cure raw meat or fish with salt in a barrel. A big jump in shelf life over raw.

dry

Drying racks

Hang whole meat on a meat rack (~2 days) to dry-age it; bake fruit in a hot oven for dried fruit; dry seaweed for recipes.

seal

Sealed crocks & cellars

Crocks of cooked meals or pickles, sealed with fat or beeswax and kept in a cold cellar, last far longer than food in the open.

Preservation methods, side by side

Every method below trades effort for time. Stack them — a sealed crock inside a cellar beats either alone. Exact numbers are config- and version-dependent; treat these as representative in-game days and let the Handbook win.

Fresh meals spoil fastest — cook to portion, not to stockpile

Cooked dishes are short-lived: porridge and scrambled eggs ~3 days, stews and soup ~6 days. Don't cook more than you'll eat unless you can crock-and-cellar it. For bulk winter stores, lean on the durable methods — salting, pickling, drying, jam and cheese — and keep raw harvest in storage vessels in the cellar.
