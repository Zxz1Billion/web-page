01 — Metals

Metals & Melting Points

Metals that melt at ≤ 1340 °C can be smelted in a crucible and cast into molds. Iron, meteoric iron and steel melt above that cap, so they are never cast; they're smithed (hammered hot). A metal becomes workable on the anvil above roughly half its melting point, and cools as you hammer, so you reheat repeatedly.

Useful working temperatures

The forge tops out at 1100 °C — enough to work every metal, since a metal becomes malleable above ~50% of its melting point. Below that it stops deforming and you must reheat. Overheat past the melt point on the anvil and the work is ruined.

Temperatures that matter at the forge & anvil

Forge maximum1100 °CHottest the forge will take any item
Metal workable> ~50% meltCopper from ~540 °C; iron glows orange-yellow

Quench (steel tool head)815–1050 °CThen plunge in water — hardens, costs durability
Temper550–700 °CReheat, air-cool to ≤200 °C — restores toughness
Pottery bakes faster> 200 °CClay-oven cooking; below 160 °C it stalls
Heat colour = temperature

Judge a workpiece by its glow: dark red ~600 °C · cherry/orange ~900 °C · yellow ~1100 °C · white >1200 °C. When the colour fades back toward dull red, return it to the forge. Quench & temper detail in Fire & Metal.

Voxels per workpiece

Smithing is a voxel game: fill the green outline, clear the purple. An ingot supplies 42 voxels, a metal plate 81 voxels — bigger items need a plate or repeated reheats.

02 — Alloys

Alloy Ratios

Alloys are mixed by combining molten metals in a crucible to an exact percentage window: even 0.1% outside the range fails the melt. Tool strength runs black bronze > tin bronze > bismuth bronze > copper; tin bronze is roughly twice copper's durability.

Every base-game alloy & its exact component window
AlloyComposition (by units)Use
Tin bronze88–92% Cu · 8–12% SnTools/armor — strongest common bronze
Bismuth bronze50–70% Cu · 20–30% Zn · 10–20% BiTools/armor — cheapest, weakest of the three
Black bronze70–90% Cu · 8–16% Au · 8–16% AgTools/armor — highest durability, costly
Brass60–70% Cu · 30–40% ZnDecorative (lanterns, fittings); not a tool metal
MolybdochalkosCopper + leadDecorative (lanterns)

Mod check

Cupronickel, leaded bronze and chalcopyrite/garnierite/pentlandite ores are not in the base game — they come from add-on mods (e.g. Advanced Geology). This Codex covers vanilla 1.22.3.

Tool-metal durability rank

Every tool of a given metal shares a base durability; the metal is the whole story. The ladder, weakest → strongest, with copper as the baseline:

04 — Fuels

Fuels & Heat

Two rules. Without a bellows worked, a fuel reaches only ~60% of its rated temperature (so a fire pit needs the bellows to melt copper). And the bloomery accepts any fuel ≥ 1200 °C — charcoal, anthracite or bituminous coal — but not lignite.

05 — Tools, anvils & quantities

Tool Tiers, Anvils & Units

A tool's material sets its tier, and the tier decides three things: how fast it works, how long it lasts, and for a pickaxe, which ores it can mine at all. Ore has a minimum pick tier: most ores need copper, but iron ore needs a bronze (tier-3) pickaxe, and meteoric iron needs an iron pick. So bronze is doubly mandatory: a bronze pick to mine iron ore and a bronze anvil to work it.

Pickaxe mining-tier gate — what each tier can mine
TierTool materialCan mine
1Stone · FlintSoft stone, clay, loose stones — not metal ore
2Copper (also gold/silver/obsidian, weaker)Most ores — copper, tin, zinc, bismuth, coal… not iron
3Bronze (tin · bismuth · black)Adds iron ore & harder stone
4IronAdds meteoric iron, ilmenite & other high-tier ores
5Meteoric ironPremium iron-class
6SteelEverything — the fastest, toughest tools in the game

Mining speed (worst → best)

Stone › Flint › Obsidian › Gold › Silver › Copper › Bismuth bronze › Tin bronze › Black bronze › Iron › Meteoric iron › Steel

Durability (worst → best)

Stone › Flint › Obsidian › Gold › Silver › Copper › Tin bronze › Bismuth bronze › Black bronze › Iron › Meteoric iron › Steel

Note the swap: tin bronze mines faster, bismuth bronze lasts a little longer — black bronze beats both. Gold/silver tools exist but are soft (decorative/novelty).

Anvils & quantities

The other tier rule: an anvil works its own metal tier and one tier above it (plus everything below). So a bronze anvil shapes the higher-tier iron, and a copper anvil (two tiers below iron) cannot.

Anvil → what it can work

  • Copper anvil → copper + all bronze
  • Bronze anvil → bronze + iron (the iron gate)
  • Iron anvil → iron + steel & meteoric iron
  • The hammer's metal doesn't matter (normal or helve).

Quantities (1 ingot = 100 units)

  • 1 ingot = 100 units = 20 ore nuggets (1 nugget = 5u)
  • Most tool/ingot molds = 100 units
  • Anvil = 900 units · helve hammer = 200 units
  • Crucible holds a large batch — several ingots per pour
"Tier" means five different things — don't mix them up

Material/tool tier (stone→steel, above) sets mining speed & what a pick can mine. Anvil tier gates what you can smith. Damage tier (1–3) rates an attack; armor/protection tier rates a defence — the two are compared in combat (Bestiary). And soil tier (bony→terra preta) caps farm fertility (§08). Same word, five unrelated ladders.

05b — Armor

Armor Types & Protection

Armor reduces incoming hits in two steps: a flat reduction is subtracted first, then a percentage cuts what remains. Each piece carries its own tier; the higher the metal, the more it protects and the longer it lasts. The trade-off is weight — heavy plate slows you and spoils ranged accuracy.

Armor materials — protection tier, flat & percent reduction
ArmorTierFlatPercentNote
Leather10.660%Cheap; minimal move penalty
Gambeson20.770%Quilted cloth; strong, light base layer
Tailored gambeson20.7575%Tailor class only
Brigandine~3midmidLeather + plates; copper-age metal armor
Chain~3–4midmidBalanced; lighter than plate
Scale~4highhighBetween chain and plate
Plate (copper→steel)up to 6highestup to ~97%Steel plate ≈ near-zero damage; heaviest, biggest move penalty

Where hits land (per blow)

  • Body — 50% of hits (armor it first)
  • Legs — 30%
  • Head — 20%
  • Each slot armored independently; an uncovered slot takes full damage.

Tier match-up (per tier gap)

  • Attack tier ≤ armor tier: +0.1 flat · +3% each step
  • Attack tier > armor tier: +0.2 flat · +15% each step (penalty)
  • So out-tiered armor leaks badly — match the threat's damage tier.

Move-speed penalties are per piece and stack: a set listing −7% on each of head/body/legs costs −21% total. Full fitting guide in Warmth & Wear.

06 — Prospecting

Prospecting-Pick Readouts

Toggle the pick's two modes with the held-item function key (default F). Density finds the region; node finds the deposit.

Density Search — relative-density ladder

Sample 3 rock blocks within ~16 blocks; reports each ore's probability in ‰ (per-thousand). Words low → high:

Miniscule › Very Poor › Poor › Decent › High › Very High › Ultra High

Decent or better = a real chance the ore generates there. Some ores cap low by design — even Miniscule means "present."

Node Search — size ladder

Break one block; scans a radius (default ~6 blocks) and gives direction + size. Words small → large:

Trace › Small › Medium › Large › Very large › Huge amounts

Anything but "No ore node nearby" means ore within range — in any direction, including up/down.

08 — Agriculture

Crops & Soil

Vintage Story assigns a drained nutrient per crop — it does not follow the "leafy=N / root=P / grain=K" rule of thumb. Rotate N → P → K so each plot's other nutrients recover.

Growth & climate rules

  • Growth stops below 0 °C and slows ~10% for every °C under 10 °C — so winter farming wants a greenhouse (+5 °C) or a warm climate.
  • Each crop has a cold and a hot tolerance: rye is the most cold-hardy cereal, rice the most heat-loving (and it wants standing water).
  • A crop drains one nutrient (N, P or K) as it grows; the others slowly recover. Rotate N → P → K so no single nutrient bottoms out.
  • Watered farmland grows faster; rain or a nearby water block keeps it moist. Dry soil stalls.
  • Harvest seeds back from mature crops to replant — keep a seed buffer before eating a harvest.

09 — Vitals

Nutrition & Health

You have a saturation bar (energy) and five nutrition bars. A varied diet raises your maximum health.

Base max health
15 HP
Per full group
+2.5 HP × 5 groups
Max possible
27.5 HP (all 5 full)
Saturation cap
1500 satiety
  • Five groups: Fruit · Vegetable · Protein · Grain · Dairy — each fills its own bar and adds up to +2.5 max HP (so all five full = +12.5, total ≈ 27.5 HP).
  • Gain rate: for every 100 saturation eaten you gain 40 nutrition of that food's group(s). Eating while already full adds no nutrition.
  • Saturation drains from sprinting, cold, healing, a torch in the offhand and most armor; at 0 you starve and take damage.

10 — Kitchen & larder

Meals & Preservation

A cooking pot has 4 ingredient slots (up to 6 each / 6 L liquid) and yields up to 6 servings; the first two slots set the meal type. Preservation factors stack multiplicatively.

Preservation multipliers (lower = slower spoilage)

MethodEffect

Storage vessel (grain)×0.50

A cellar scores 100% − up to 40% (skylight) − up to 50% (cooling) and holds ≈ 5 °C; it only helps when the outside is warmer. Stack the methods: salted or dried food, in a sealed crock, inside a high-scoring cellar lasts longest. Full build in The Homestead.
