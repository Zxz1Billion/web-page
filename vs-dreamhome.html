The Dream Home

Not a starter shack — the home you build once the base is established: a huge three-storey great house with a hall, kitchen, parlour, two bedrooms, a study and a dormered loft; massive farmland beside it to feed two people year-round; and a walk-through greenhouse for greens through winter. Every part is drawn in 3D, with a course-by-course build plan and an approximate material bill.

00 — The whole estate

The estate at a glance

The great house sits to one side; the massive field runs along the other, with the walk-through greenhouse between them so you can carry greens straight indoors. Build the house first for shelter, then fence and till the field, then raise the glasshouse.

01 — The home

The great house

Three storeys of timber-frame over a stone plinth, with a hall wing and a kitchen wing. Ground floor: great hall, kitchen and parlour. First floor: two bedrooms and a study. Above: a loft lit by dormers. Three hearths warm the whole house.

Footprint18×13 + two wings
Key materialsStone plinth, oak frame, plaster, shingles
Best forA huge two-person home: great hall, kitchen, parlour, two bedrooms, a study and a loft

Course-by-course plan

  1. Site & footprint — Pick flat ground and mark out the 18×13 + two wings-block footprint, clearing a 24×19 working area around it.
  2. Materials (approx.) — Gather roughly ~970 plaster-on-frame, ~360 cobblestone, ~360 wooden shingles, ~35 dark glazing, ~12 dark timber and ~9 limestone brick. These are estimates from the massing — exact counts depend on how thick you build the walls and how you finish it.
  3. Plinth & foundation — Lay a cobblestone plinth — a 18×13 footing 1 course high — to lift the house off wet ground, then floor inside.
  4. Walls & openings — Raise the walls in plaster-on-frame — a 18×13 block 8 courses high, a 6×3 block 6 courses high and a 6×3 block 5 courses high — keeping each course level and the corners plumb. As the courses rise, leave a 2×3 doorway in the front (south) wall, 18 windows in the front (south) wall from about course 3 and 2 windows in the right-hand (east) wall from about course 3; bridge each opening with a lintel, then glaze the windows once the wall tops out.
  5. Columns & pillars — Raise 2 round limestone brick columns about 1 across and 3 courses tall, spaced evenly; cap each where it meets the roof.
  6. Upper floors & balconies — Once the lower walls carry, lay a 5×2 deck at about course 4 in limestone brick for the upper floor, gallery and balconies, then carry the walls on up.
  7. Frame & posts — Stand 4 corner posts and studs in dark timber, then tie them with sill, rail and brace beams; fill the panels between with plaster or boards.
  8. Roof — Set the rafters and build a gabled roof, ridge running left-to-right, rising about 5 blocks in wooden shingles with plaster-on-frame gable ends and two gabled roofs, ridge running front-to-back, rising about 2 blocks in wooden shingles with plaster-on-frame gable ends. Start at the eaves and lap each course upward so it sheds rain and snow.
  9. Dormers — Frame three dormers into the front roof slope, each with a little window and its own small gable, to light the loft.
  10. Chimney — Build 3 cobblestone chimney stacks, carrying the flue up past the ridge so smoke draws clear.
  11. Finishing — Three storeys of timber-frame over a stone plinth, with a hall wing and a kitchen wing. Ground floor: great hall, kitchen and parlour. First floor: two bedrooms and a study. Above: a loft lit by dormers. Three hearths warm the whole house.

02 — The land

The farmland

A big fenced field beside the house, split into strips: grain, roots and greens, with every third strip left fallow to rotate. A scarecrow stands guard and a tool-and-seed barn sits at the corner.

Footprint20×14 field
Key materialsTilled soil, post-and-rail fence, seed
Best forFeeding two people year-round — grain, roots and greens on a rotating field

Course-by-course plan

  1. Site & footprint — Pick flat ground and mark out the 20×14 field-block footprint, clearing a 25×19 working area around it.
  2. Materials (approx.) — Gather roughly ~55 logs, ~25 packed soil, ~16 thatch, ~6 log posts and ~4 planks. These are estimates from the massing — exact counts depend on how thick you build the walls and how you finish it.
  3. Foundation course — Lay the first course as a solid cobblestone ring on the outline to keep damp out of the walls, then floor the inside.
  4. Walls & openings — Raise the walls in logs — a 4×3 block 3 courses high — keeping each course level and the corners plumb. As the courses rise, leave a 1×2 doorway in the front (south) wall; bridge each opening with a lintel, then glaze the windows once the wall tops out.
  5. Frame & posts — Stand 1 corner posts and studs in log posts, then tie them with sill, rail and brace beams; fill the panels between with plaster or boards.
  6. Roof — Set the rafters and build a gabled roof, ridge running left-to-right, rising about 2 blocks in thatch with logs gable ends. Start at the eaves and lap each course upward so it sheds rain and snow.
  7. Surroundings — Run a post-and-rail fence around the plot, plant a tree or two for shelter and till and sow the beds.
  8. Finishing — A big fenced field beside the house, split into strips: grain, roots and greens, with every third strip left fallow to rotate. A scarecrow stands guard and a tool-and-seed barn sits at the corner.

03 — Glass & greens

The walk-through greenhouse

A glasshouse you walk straight through, end to end: a door at each gable, a central timber path, and raised planting beds down both sides under a peaked glass roof. The stone knee-walls hold warmth so crops keep through winter.

Footprint6×11 glasshouse
Key materialsStone knee-wall, glass panes, timber ridge
Best forYear-round greens — walk in one end and out the other, beds either side

Course-by-course plan

  1. Site & footprint — Pick flat ground and mark out the 6×11 glasshouse-block footprint, clearing a 11×16 working area around it.
  2. Materials (approx.) — Gather roughly ~260 glass panes, ~13 planks and ~11 cobblestone. These are estimates from the massing — exact counts depend on how thick you build the walls and how you finish it.
  3. Plinth & foundation — Lay a cobblestone plinth — a 1×11 footing 1 course high — to lift the house off wet ground, then floor inside.
  4. Walls & openings — Raise the walls in glass panes — a 6×11 block 3 courses high — keeping each course level and the corners plumb. As the courses rise, leave a 1×2 doorway in the front (south) wall and a window in the front (south) wall from about course 2; bridge each opening with a lintel, then glaze the windows once the wall tops out.
  5. Frame & posts — Stand the corner posts and studs in dark timber, then tie them with sill, rail and brace beams; fill the panels between with plaster or boards.
  6. Roof — Set the rafters and build a gabled roof, ridge running front-to-back, rising about 2 blocks in glass panes. Start at the eaves and lap each course upward so it sheds rain and snow.
  7. Surroundings — Till and sow the beds.
  8. Finishing — A glasshouse you walk straight through, end to end: a door at each gable, a central timber path, and raised planting beds down both sides under a peaked glass roof. The stone knee-walls hold warmth so crops keep through winter.

Build in this order

Plinth and ground floor of the house first (somewhere dry to sleep), then its upper storeys and roof. Fence and till the field next so food is growing while you finish the house. Raise the greenhouse last — it wants spare glass, which comes after you've a bloomery running.
