A Home Worth Keeping

Most bases stay an ugly dirt box forever. This guide builds one complete, beautiful home instead — a timber-and-cob cottage with a hearth hall, a kitchen, a bedroom, a workshop and a cool cellar, dressed with windows, a porch, a path and a garden. Follow it room by room, or steal the parts you like.

00 — Before the first block

The Vision

A home reads as beautiful when it has proportion, depth, material variety and light — not when it's big. Aim small and finished over large and bare.

shape

Massing first

Decide the footprint and roofline before detail. A pitched roof, a chimney and a porch already make a "house" instead of a box.

materials

Two or three, no more

Pick a wall material, a trim material and a roof material. Cob + timber + thatch, or stone + plaster + shingle. Variety reads as craft; ten materials read as noise.

light

Warm light

Glazed windows by day, lanterns and a hearth by night. Light is what makes a room feel lived-in (and keeps drifters out).

This build

A ~8×6 cottage, cob-and-timber walls, a shingled gable roof, a stone chimney, a small porch, and a partial cellar dug beneath. Big enough for every early need, small enough to actually finish. Technique lives in The Architect; this page is the plan.

01 — Where & what it stands on

Site & Foundation

Build where you'll actually live: a temperate, moderate-rainfall spot with wood, water and game nearby, and flat-ish ground. A gentle slope is a gift — it gives you a free cellar under the high side.

  1. Clear and level a footprint. Mark out 8×6 (plus a block of margin). Flatten it; a clean base stops things spawning or burrowing under the floor.
  2. Lay a stone base course. Ring the footprint one block high in stone brick or cobble. It lifts the timber off wet soil, stops rot-rooms, and visually "grounds" the build.
  3. Floor it. Plank or stone-brick the interior. Leave a 1-wide stair down in one corner for the cellar (next section's bonus).
A free cellar

Dig a ≤7×7×7 room under the floor and seal it with solid stone/soil walls and a solid door — that's a working cellar for food (the size limit matters; bigger rooms don't preserve). Full rules in The Homestead.

02 — The shell

Walls & Roof

Walls give the house its face; the roof gives it its silhouette. Spend your effort here — it's 80% of the look.

Walls with depth

  • Frame the corners with timber posts, then fill the panels with cob or plaster. The dark posts against pale infill is the classic timber-frame look.
  • Add a diagonal brace per panel with the chisel, and let a beam or two sit proud of the wall for shadow. Depth — not colour — is what makes a wall interesting.
  • Two storeys? Jetty the upper floor out one block over the lower for a cosy, weighty overhang.

The roof

  • Pitch it. A flat roof is a box; a gable or hip is a home. Use roofing blocks (thatch early, clay shingle or slate later) and smooth the slope with stairs/slabs.
  • Overhang the eaves by a block all round — it casts shadow, sheds rain and reads as deliberate.
  • Run a chimney up one gable end from the hearth. A roofline broken by a chimney instantly looks inhabited.
Copy the cottage

The exact course-by-course shell — foundation → frame → windows → top plate → eaves → ridge — is the worked example in The Architect → Full Build. This page handles the rooms.

04 — Furnishing it

Room by Room

A room reads as that room when it has its one signature fitting plus a little clutter. Don't leave floors empty.

heart

Hearth hall

A stone hearth against the back wall, a table with stools centred on a rug, a lantern overhead. This is the room guests "see".

cook

Kitchen

A firepit with a cooking pot, a quern for flour, shelves of crocks and a water barrel. Keep it next to the cellar stair.

rest

Bedroom

A bed in a corner, a chest for clothes, a small rug and a bedside candle. (Remember: the bed is for sleeping; a temporal gear sets respawn.)

work

Workshop

An anvil on a stump, a small forge with a hood, a tool rack and an ingot chest. Vent it to an outside wall.

store

Pantry / cellar

Down the stair: shelves of crocks, barrels of salted meat and pickles, a storage vessel of grain. Cool, dark, sealed.

tidy

Storage nook

Labelled chests by the door and a couple of storage vessels. A place for everything keeps the lovely rooms uncluttered.

05 — Glass & flame

Windows & Light

  • Glaze the windows. Fire quartz into glass (in a bloomery, ≥1200 °C — not sand) and frame panes with timber. Two per long side, framed by the corner posts, looks intentional.
  • Light low, not high. A few lanterns and candles on walls and tables beat a grid of ceiling torches. Lanterns and candles never burn out or get rained on.
  • Keep it sealed and lit. A fully enclosed, well-lit interior is both cosy and drifter-proof — light level keeps the dark things out.
Colour with light

Self-craftable glass comes in clear, smoky, pink, violet and green; lanterns can be lined for warmth. A single coloured pane over the door is a lovely signature. Full palette in The Architect.

06 — The setting

Garden & Approach

A house in bare dirt never looks finished. The setting sells it — give the eye a path, a threshold and some green.

  • A porch or threshold. Two posts and a small shed roof over the door give shelter and a sense of arrival.
  • A path. Lay stone or gravel from the door out into the world. A path implies a lived-in place more than anything else.
  • A garden. A small fenced kitchen-garden of vegetables and herbs, a fruit tree or two, maybe a beehive. Function and beauty at once.
  • A well & a fence. A covered well for water and a low fence to frame the plot. Add a lamp post by the gate for night.
  • Landscape the edges. Break the straight ground line with shrubs, a woodpile, a bench. Asymmetry reads as natural.
Build the extras

The well, fence, garden beds, beehive, woodshed and gazebo are all in the Pattern Book — drop them around the plot.
