A working field manual for XNet — McJty's fast, cheap, low-lag logistics mod that carries items, fluids, energy, and logic over one slim network of cables. One Controller, a fistful of Connectors, and a handful of channels replace a spaghetti of pipes, ducts, and wires. This guide runs from your first two-Connector item route to a logic-gated, color-coded base spine.

XNet MC 1.19.2 · Forge Items · Fluids · Energy · Logic Cheap & low-lag

00 — Orientation

Start Here

XNet is a single logistics network that moves items, fluids, Forge Energy, and logic signals at once. Instead of a pipe for items, a duct for fluids, and a wire for power, you run one set of cheap cables, tap blocks with Connectors, and configure everything from a single Controller GUI. It is famously fast and light on the server tick, which is why it became many players' default router.

core

One network, four jobs

A single cable run can carry item, fluid, energy, and logic channels simultaneously — no separate transport per resource.

cheap

Low cost, low lag

Connectors and the Controller are inexpensive, and XNet's central-controller design keeps tick cost down versus per-pipe simulation.

control

One GUI to rule it

Inputs, outputs, filters, counts, priorities, redstone, and speed are all set from the Controller's channel screens.

smart

Built-in logic

Logic channels read inventories, tanks, energy, and redstone, then drive colors and redstone outputs for conditional automation.

Where it fits

XNet is the connective tissue of the Automation & Power hub: it is the cheapest way to feed and drain a wall of machines, balance energy across them, and gate them on conditions. Pair it with a processing mod (Mekanism, Create, Thermal) for the machines, and let XNet do the routing.

The shape of the climb

  1. Place a Controller. Run a short line of Network Cable from it.
  2. Tap two blocks. Put a Connector on the face of a chest and on the face of a machine.
  3. Make a channel. Open the Controller, create an Item channel, and assign one Connector as input (extract) and the other as output (insert).
  4. Filter & tune. Add filters, count limits, priority, and redstone control so it moves only what you want.
  5. Add fluids, energy & logic. The same Connectors and cables now carry the rest — just add more channels.
  6. Gate it. Use a Logic channel to switch machines on/off based on stock, tank level, or energy.

01 — The wiring

Building a Network

An XNet network has three physical parts: a Controller (the brain), Network Cable (the wire), and Connectors (the taps that touch your blocks). Everything else is configuration.

The physical network parts
PartDoesNotes
ControllerThe single brain of one network; holds every channel and its settings.One per network; provides a fixed number of channel slots.
Network CableCarries the network between Controller and Connectors.Cheap; comes in colored variants you can mix without crossing networks.
ConnectorTaps one block face into the network for input/output.The workhorse — every machine/chest you touch needs one.
Advanced ConnectorA Connector with longer reach / remote operation.For taps farther from the cable than a normal Connector allows.
Antenna + Wireless RouterLinks separate networks wirelessly, including across dimensions.The Router bridges networks; the Antenna extends/boosts its range.

Cables vs. Connectors

This is the key distinction. Cable is inert wire — it just carries the network from A to B and connects to other cable and Connectors. A Connector is where the network actually touches a block: place it on the face of a chest, tank, or machine and that block becomes available to your channels. A block with no Connector on it is invisible to XNet.

Color-coded cable

Network Cable comes in colors. Cables of different colors do not connect to each other, which lets you run several independent networks side by side (even touching) without them merging. Use a wrench/screwdriver to recolor, and keep a color convention per controller.

Reaching farther

A normal Connector must sit adjacent to the cable. When a machine is out past easy reach, the Advanced Connector extends the distance the network can operate over, so you do not have to thread cable into every cramped corner. For genuinely remote or cross-dimensional links — a far mining outpost, a Nether power plant — use the Wireless Router to bridge two networks, and add an Antenna to push the wireless range. Wireless links are configured per channel and are the clean way to share a few resources without dragging cable across the world.

Channels, not infinity

A Controller exposes a limited number of channel slots, and each network is one Controller. If you outgrow it, the answer is usually another Controller for a different area (and a Wireless Router if the two must talk) rather than one mega-network — that also keeps each GUI readable.

02 — The model

Connectors & Channels

XNet's whole mental model is: Connectors are taps, channels are the rules. Each Connector you add becomes available to the Controller; each channel decides which Connectors are inputs and which are outputs, and exactly how resources flow between them.

The four channel types

What each channel type moves
ChannelMovesTypical use
ItemItems between inventoriesFeed machines, pull outputs, sort into storage.
FluidFluids between tanks/machines (mB)Move lava, water, mod fluids; balance tanks.
EnergyForge Energy (FE)Distribute and balance power across machines.
LogicSensor results → colors / redstoneConditional control: gate machines on stock/level.

Using the Controller GUI

Open the Controller and you get a row of channel slots. The flow is always the same:

  1. Create a channel. Pick a free slot and choose its type (Item, Fluid, Energy, Logic). Name it so future-you can read the screen.
  2. Add Connectors as endpoints. Each Connector on the network shows up; assign the ones this channel uses and mark each side as an input (extract from that block) or output (insert into that block).
  3. Configure each endpoint. Per-side settings — filters, counts, priority, redstone, speed — live on each Connector's tab within the channel.
  4. Repeat per resource. The same Connector can appear in an Item channel, a Fluid channel, and an Energy channel at once; channels are independent.
Inputs pull, outputs push

In XNet wording, an input takes resources out of the block it sits on and into the network; an output takes resources from the network and puts them into the block. Read it as "input to the network" / "output to the world" and the GUI stops being confusing.

  • One Controller per network
  • Many Connectors per network
  • 4 channel types
  • One Connector → many channels

03 — Moving items

Item Channels

The Item channel is the one you will use most: pull crafting outputs out of machines, push ingredients in, and feed storage. Each Connector endpoint carries a full set of insert/extract settings, so a single channel can be as dumb as "chest → furnace" or as smart as a sorted, rate-limited, redstone-gated bus.

Per-connector item settings
SettingWhat it does
FilterRestrict to specific items or tags; set whitelist (only these) or blacklist (everything but these).
Count / amountKeep a target stock: e.g. "insert until 64 here" or "extract but leave 16 behind". Stops overfilling and starvation.
PriorityWhen several outputs compete, higher priority is served first; great for "fill the machine before overflow storage".
Round-robinSpread items evenly across multiple equal-priority outputs instead of stacking the first one.
Redstone controlRun only when the Connector's redstone state matches (off / on / pulse), so external logic can gate it.
Speed / rateHow often the endpoint operates and how much it moves per operation. Faster = more throughput, configurable per side.

Keeping machines fed and drained

The bread-and-butter pattern is a pair of endpoints on each machine: an output that inserts ingredients (with a count limit so it never jams the input slot) and an input that extracts finished product into storage. Add a filter so only the right item goes into the right slot, and a priority so active machines win over a dump chest.

Count limits beat clogging

Set extract to "leave N" so you never strip a machine's last input, and set insert to "keep at most N" so you never overstuff. This is how XNet keeps a furnace array fed without ever flooding it — the count target does what a pipe with a "stocking" upgrade does, but natively and per side.

Tags are your filter friend

Filtering by tag (e.g. all logs, all ores) instead of one item id makes a route survive new items appearing in your inventory and works across mods. Whitelist a tag for tidy buses; blacklist a tag to keep junk out of precious storage.

04 — Fluids & power

Fluid & Energy Channels

The same cables and Connectors that move your items move fluids and Forge Energy. Add a Fluid channel to shuttle lava, water, or mod fluids between tanks and machines; add an Energy channel to spread FE across a base. Both use the familiar input/output endpoint model with their own rate and priority controls.

Fluid channels

Each endpoint is an input (drain a tank/machine into the network) or output (fill one from it), measured in millibuckets per operation. Use filters to keep fluids separate, priority to fill the important tank first, and count limits to hold a buffer level — for example, keep a machine's internal tank topped to 8,000 mB without overflowing it.

Energy channels

An Energy channel distributes FE from a battery/generator endpoint (input) to machine endpoints (outputs). It is the cleanest way to power a wall of machines from one buffer without running separate wires. Rates are configurable per side, and priority decides who gets fed first when supply is tight.

Fluid & energy endpoint controls
ControlFluidEnergy
DirectionInput (drain) / Output (fill)Input (source) / Output (sink)
RatemB / opFE / op
PriorityFill order across outputsFeed order across machines
Count / levelTarget buffer (mB)Keep-charged threshold (FE)
RedstoneGate on signalGate on signal
Balancing with priority + round-robin

To spread power or fluid evenly, give the outputs equal priority and enable round-robin so each tick a different machine is served. To prioritize instead — keep the reactor's coolant tank full before topping anything else — give that endpoint the highest priority and let the rest take what is left.

Rate is per operation, not infinite

Each endpoint moves a capped amount per operation at a set speed. For high-throughput power or fluid spines, raise the per-side rate/speed and add more endpoints rather than expecting one Connector to carry an entire base's load.

05 — Conditional control

Logic Channels

Logic is what lifts XNet from "smart pipes" to "automation framework". A Logic channel reads sensors on its endpoints — inventory contents, fluid level, energy level, redstone — evaluates conditions, and emits results as channel colors or redstone outputs that the rest of your network (and the world) can react to.

What it can sense and emit

The logic system
Reads (sensors)Emits (outputs)
Item count in an inventory (≥, ≤, =, slot filters)A redstone signal out of a Connector
Fluid amount in a tank (threshold)An internal color on the network
Energy stored in a buffer (threshold)Combine several sensors before emitting
Incoming redstone state on a ConnectorDrive other channels' redstone-control settings

How the pieces connect

  1. Sense. A Logic endpoint watches a block: "is there less than 16 ingots in this chest?", "is this tank below 25%?", "is this buffer under 1M FE?".
  2. Evaluate. The channel turns sensor states into a result — XNet uses colored channels as named on/off lines you can reference elsewhere.
  3. Emit. The result sets a color or pushes a redstone signal out of a Connector into the world.
  4. React. Other channels read that color (or that redstone) via their redstone control setting, switching item/fluid/energy flow on or off.
The classic: auto-craft on demand

Sense "fewer than 64 plates in storage" → emit a color → an Item channel that feeds the press only runs while that color is active. The press turns itself off when stock is full and back on when it drains. No external redstone wiring, all inside XNet.

Keep logic readable

Name your colors/lines by intent ("plates-low", "tank-ok") and keep one logic channel per concern. A wall of unnamed sensors is the one place an XNet network can get as confusing as the pipe spaghetti it replaced.

06 — The price tag

Recipes & Cost

XNet's pitch is that it is cheap. The components lean on common materials, so you can wire a whole base for the price of a few stacks of AE2 cable or a chest of pipes. Treat the exact recipes below as the shape of the cost — check JEI in your pack for the precise crafting grids.

The facade trick

The XNet Facade wraps cable to look like any block you give it, so you can hide an entire network inside your walls and floors and keep the build clean — at almost no material cost.

When XNet beats pipes (and AE2)

vs pipes

Cheaper & lighter

One cheap network does items, fluids, energy, and logic. No per-pipe entity simulation means less tick cost than a base full of item pipes.

vs pipes

Central config

Filters, counts, priorities, and round-robin live in one GUI instead of being scattered across servos and upgrades on every pipe.

vs AE2

No channels-to-manage

XNet routes between real blocks without ME channels, controllers-of-controllers, or crystal growth. Faster to set up for plain logistics.

caveat

Not a storage network

XNet routes; it does not give you a searchable item terminal or autocrafting graph. For mass storage, pair it with AE2/RS — XNet feeds them.

Pick the right tool

Use XNet for routing, feeding, draining, balancing, and gating. Use a storage mod for a unified inventory and autocrafting. They complement each other — many bases run XNet as the muscle and AE2/RS as the brain of storage.

07 — Doing it well

Patterns & Tips

XNet rewards a little discipline. A handful of habits keep a network fast, readable, and easy to extend as the base grows.

The habits that pay off

  • One Controller per base area. A controller for the smeltery, one for the farm, one for power. Smaller GUIs, fewer channel-slot fights, and a failure stays local. Bridge them with a Wireless Router only where they truly need to share.
  • Color-code Connectors and cable. Different cable colors are different networks — use that to physically separate concerns even when runs overlap. Within a network, a naming convention on channels does the same job.
  • Filter discipline. Whitelist by tag where you can; blacklist junk out of storage. Every endpoint should have a deliberate filter, not "everything".
  • Count limits everywhere. "Keep at most N" / "leave N behind" prevents both clogging and starvation and is the single biggest reliability win.
  • Gate machines with logic. Run presses, smelters, and reactors only when stock is low or buffers allow — saves power and stops overproduction.
  • Hide it with facades. Clean builds keep their networks behind walls; facades make that nearly free.
Combining with Mekanism / Create arrays

XNet shines as the loader/unloader for big machine arrays. Point Item outputs into each Mekanism factory or Create machine, pull products back with prioritized inputs, run an Energy channel to power the row, and use a Logic channel to switch the whole array off when the output chest is full. One cheap network drives a wall of machines no pipe layout could match for tidiness.

Watch the channel budget

Each Controller has limited channel slots. Group related endpoints into one channel (many machines can share one Item channel with per-side filters) before spending a slot, and split to a second Controller when an area genuinely outgrows the first.

Sanity check before scaling

Before you wire a hundred machines, prove the pattern on two: confirm filters, counts, priority, and any logic gate behave exactly as intended, then replicate. A subtle filter mistake copied across a base is the worst kind of debugging. The in-game GUI and JEI are the ground truth — when this guide and your pack disagree, believe the game.

★ — The path

Progression — from one controller to a base network

You do not wire a whole base on day one. XNet grows from a single Controller doing one stupid-simple job into the routing spine of your entire base, one channel at a time. This is the order most players actually walk it — each step builds on the last, and nothing here asks you to tear out what you already built.

  1. One Controller, one item route. Place a Controller, run a little Network Cable, and tap two blocks with Connectors — a chest and a machine on the same machine or two adjacent ones. Make a single Item channel: one input, one output. Watch it move. That is the whole mod in miniature.
  2. Grow the item channels with filters & colour-coding. Add more Connectors and more Item channels as your machine count grows. Give every endpoint a deliberate filter (whitelist by tag where you can), set count limits so nothing clogs, and use coloured cable to keep separate concerns physically apart even where runs overlap.
  3. Add Fluid & Energy channels to the same network. The cables and Connectors you already placed can carry more. Drop a Fluid channel to feed tanks and an Energy channel to power the whole row of machines from one buffer — no new wire, just new channels on the same Controller.
  4. Introduce Logic channels for conditional automation. Now make it think. A Logic channel senses stock, tank level, or energy and emits a colour or redstone that gates your item/fluid/energy flow — presses that run only when storage is low, machines that idle when the output chest is full.
  5. Reach remote sites with Antenna / wireless. When a mining outpost or a Nether power plant is too far to cable, bridge networks with a Wireless Router and extend its range with an Antenna. Share just the few resources that need to cross, per channel, without dragging cable across the world.
  6. Consolidate the base on one or two Controllers. With the patterns proven, settle on a clean layout: one Controller per major area (smeltery, farm, power), bridged only where they must talk. Group related endpoints into shared channels, hide the runs with facades, and you have a readable, low-lag spine for the whole base.
Where this sits in the bigger climb

XNet is the muscle of the Automation hub — the cheap router that feeds, drains, balances, and gates everything else. Once your base spine is consolidated, fold it into the broader base progression in The Ascent, where XNet routing meets storage, processing, and power as one system.
