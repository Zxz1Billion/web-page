The Long Road

Getting from bare hands to a steel pickaxe is a ladder of metals, and you cannot skip a rung. Stone unlocks copper, copper unlocks bronze, bronze unlocks iron, and iron unlocks steel. Each tier needs the right ore, a way to reach working heat, the right anvil, and tools made from that metal before the next tier opens. This is the whole climb, numbered, with real values.

00 — Orientation

How to Read This Road

Vintage Story gates its whole tech tree behind metal. There is no "tech you research." There is only the next metal, and each one is harder to get hot, harder to shape, and stronger than the last. Learn the gate and the rest of the game makes sense.

Every metal tier on this road needs the same four things. Miss one and you're stuck, so check them off in this order:

  1. The ore (or native metal). Find it first, from surface nuggets, prospecting, or mining a vein.
  2. A way to reach working temperature. A fire pit melts copper. Iron and steel need a bloomery or furnace and charcoal, not just firewood.
  3. The right anvil. An anvil works its own metal tier and one tier above it. Iron is worked on a bronze anvil, and steel on an iron anvil. Iron will not work on a copper anvil (copper is two tiers below iron). This is the trap that strands most new players.
  4. Tools cast or smithed from it. Copper and bronze are cast in molds. Iron and steel are smithed (hammered) on an anvil. The new pickaxe unlocks the next tier's mining.
The two hardest gates, named up front

(1) Stone tools cannot mine ore veins. You need a copper pickaxe to mine, so casting copper is the real start of the game. (2) Iron cannot be worked on a copper anvil. You must reach bronze and cast a bronze anvil before iron is possible. Plan around those two and you'll never be confused about why you're stuck.

The anvil rule, stated exactly

The anvil trap strands more players than anything else, so here is the precise rule from the wiki: an anvil works its own metal tier, one tier above, and everything below. So a bronze anvil covers everything up to and including iron; the only things it can't work are steel and meteoric iron. Note also how each anvil is made: copper and bronze anvils are cast in a mold, while iron, meteoric-iron and steel anvils are welded together from two halves on an existing anvil.

Which anvil works which metal (config-tunable — Handbook is ground truth)

CopperCast in anvil moldCopper, and tin/bismuth bronze (one tier up)
BronzeCast in anvil moldUp to and including iron (the iron gate)

Cast vs. smithed — which metals do which

The melting point decides the method. Anything that melts at or below 1340 °C can be melted in a crucible and cast into molds (copper, bronze, and their components). Iron, meteoric iron and steel melt above that, so they can't be cast at all — they're smithed (hammered) from a bloom or blister on an anvil. If you ever go hunting for an "iron mold," that's the sign you've misread the tier.

01 — The start

The Stone Age

You begin in the Stone Age with nothing but knapped flint. The goal of this age is the pottery that lets you handle metal: a fired crucible to melt ore in, and clay molds to pour it into. Everything here is a prerequisite for the Copper Age.

Tools, fire and fibre

If you've done The First Day, most of this is already behind you. Knap a knife, axe, spear, shovel and a hoe (the hoe is for farming later). The exact knapping patterns are stepped out in The Four Crafts → Knapping. Build and light a fire pit, cut firewood, and make torches (stick + dry grass) for safe light.

You also need cordage. Cut cattail tops or papyrus with your knife and craft them into twine, which weaves into baskets for portable storage. Twine also binds many later recipes, so make a stack.

Pottery — the real work of the Stone Age

Pottery is a four-step pipeline: dig clay → clay-form an item → build a kiln → fire it. Do it carefully. A cracked crucible at the copper stage costs you hours.

  1. Dig clay. Find clay deposits (grey-brown patches, often near water). There are three kinds: blue and red clay (general pottery) and fire clay. The crucible must be fire clay because it has to survive molten-metal heat.
  2. Clay-form the vessels. Hold clay and Sneak (Shift) + right-click a surface to open the clay-forming grid (the layer-by-layer build is shown in The Four Crafts). Form a bowl (eating), a crock and storage vessel (food storage / sealed meals), a jug (water), and most importantly a crucible (roughly 2 clay → 1 crucible, fire clay only).
  3. Form your molds. Clay-form an ingot mold and a set of tool molds (pickaxe, axe, knife, etc.). You cast metal into these, so make the molds you'll want in copper.
  4. Build a pit kiln & fire it. Place the unfired pottery on the ground, stack 8 firewood around/over each piece (more pieces need a bigger pit), cover and ignite. A pit-kiln firing takes roughly 10–20 in-game hours depending on fuel. Let it burn out fully before you collect the hardened pottery.
The crucible is the gate, not the bowl

You can reach copper with just a fired crucible + an ingot mold + one tool mold. Everything else (bowls, crocks, jugs) is quality-of-life. If you're impatient, fire the crucible, ingot mold, and a pickaxe mold first. That's the minimum to start smelting.

First cooked meals & a bed

Clay-form and fire a cooking pot too. Set on a fire pit, it turns raw ingredients into meals (soups, porridges, stews) that feed multiple nutrition groups at once. The full system is in The Larder. Finally, craft a bedroll/bed from cattail/twine so you can sleep through dangerous nights. (Binding your respawn point comes later, with a temporal gear — sleeping alone doesn't set spawn in current versions.)

Roughly how long this age takes

Effort, not a fixed clock — but for a first-timer the Stone Age is usually 1–3 in-game days: a day to survive and tool up (see The First Day), then a day or so to find clay, form the pottery, and run the pit kiln. The pit-kiln firing itself eats ~10–20 in-game hours, so start it and go do other chores while it burns.

You're ready for the Copper Age when…

You have a fired crucible, an ingot mold, and at least a pickaxe tool mold; a fire pit you can fuel with charcoal (peat won't melt copper); and a safe, lit base to work from. Everything else (bowls, crocks, cooking pot, bed) is comfort — nice to have, not a gate.

Common Stone-Age mistakes

Forming the crucible from ordinary clay instead of fire clay (it'll fail at metal heat) · cracking pottery by collecting it before the kiln fully burns out · firing only the crucible and forgetting a mold to pour into · trying to melt copper on a plain-firewood fire pit (you need charcoal).

02 — Chalcolithic

The Copper Age

Copper is the first metal and the most forgiving. It shows up as native nuggets on the surface, and it melts cool enough that a fire pit can do it. This age turns your fired crucible into your first real tools, and your first pickaxe, which is what finally lets you mine.

Find the copper

Walk ridgelines and exposed stone and pick up surface copper nuggets. You can gather a starter batch by hand, no mining required. The copper-bearing ores you'll see are:

Common copper sources in the early game
SourceLooks like / whereNotes
Native copperOrange-red nuggets & veins in surface stoneSmelts at the best ratio; easiest start
MalachiteGreen ore in/on stoneVery common copper ore
CupriteDark red oreGood copper yield
TetrahedriteGrey metallic oreCopper-bearing; also a later source of other metals
Stone tools can't mine veins

A flint or stone pickaxe cannot break ore blocks in a vein. You can only pick up loose surface bits. That's the point of copper: a copper pickaxe unlocks mining. Until you cast one, you are limited to what's lying on the surface.

Smelt & cast

  1. Load the crucible. Put copper nuggets/ore into your fired crucible. Metal is counted as 100 "units" = 1 ingot. Fill to the number of ingots you want (the crucible holds several).
  2. Heat it in the fire pit. Place the crucible on a burning fire pit fuelled with charcoal (it burns hotter than firewood; peat is too cool). Copper melts at about 1084°C, which a charcoal fire pit reaches. Watch the temperature readout climb until the contents read "molten".
  3. Cast an ingot. Pick up the hot crucible and right-click your ingot mold to pour. Let it cool, then collect the ingot. Cast at least one ingot for trading/storage.
  4. Cast tools. Pour molten copper into your tool molds. Make the pickaxe first, then axe, knife, shovel, hoe, propick. Combine each cast head with a stick to finish the tool. Full casting walkthrough: The Four Crafts → Casting.

Cast an anvil, build a forge

You'll smith from the Bronze Age on, and smithing needs two things you set up now: a copper anvil (cast it from copper; it's a large pour, several ingots) and a forge to heat metal hot enough to work on that anvil. The forge is a small masonry firebox you fuel with charcoal. The full smithing rig is detailed in The Forge & The Anvil.

Two milestones, remember them

A copper pickaxe unlocks ore mining. A copper anvil unlocks smithing. With both, you can dig deep for the alloy metals the Bronze Age needs.

Counting metal — the units system

All metal is measured in units: 100 units = 1 ingot. A single ore nugget smelts to roughly a fraction of an ingot, so it takes several nuggets per ingot. A crucible holds a stack of material and melts it together, and a typical pour is sized in whole ingots. Most copper tool heads cost on the order of one ingot's worth; an anvil is a big pour of several ingots, so smelt up before you cast it.

Roughly how long this age takes

Often the longest single stretch for new players, because it's gated on finding metal on foot. Surface copper gathering plus your first smelt can be a single busy day; getting enough copper to cast a full tool set and an anvil usually runs 2–4 in-game days. Stock charcoal in advance — every smelt and every later forge run burns it.

You're ready for the Bronze Age when…

You've cast a copper pickaxe (so you can finally mine veins), a prospecting pick (to find alloy ores), and a copper anvil + forge (so smithing is set up for later). With a pick and a propick you can go underground and hunt tin.

Common Copper-Age mistakes

Trying to mine veins with a stone pick (impossible — cast copper first) · melting copper on plain firewood instead of charcoal · casting tools before the pickaxe and stalling your mining · forgetting the anvil + forge so the Bronze Age can't smith · letting the crucible cool before you pour (work hot).

03 — The alloy age

The Bronze Age

Bronze is copper improved with a second (or third) metal. It's stronger and more durable than pure copper. Bronze is also the only way into iron, twice over: you need a bronze pickaxe to mine iron ore (a copper pick can't) and a bronze anvil to work it. This age is about prospecting for alloy metals and getting the ratios right in your crucible.

Now you can really mine

With your copper pickaxe and a prospecting pick (propick), go underground. The propick has two modes — toggle with F:

  • Density Search mode — sample a few blocks within ~16 blocks and it reports the probability of each ore in parts-per-thousand. This tells you what to expect in the area, not exactly where. Use it to decide whether a region is worth digging.
  • Node Search mode — break one block and it scans a radius, pointing you toward a specific nearby deposit and its rough size. Use it once Density mode says an area is promising.

Ore lives in disc-shaped deposits seeded into specific rock types (sedimentary, metamorphic, igneous) at random heights. Surface nuggets are only the shallow tip — the big deposits sit deep, and for some ores you'll be digging well below Y 72 to reach what Density mode forecasts. Sink a staircase or vertical shaft, sample as you go, and dig toward the strongest readings. The alloy metals you're hunting:

Alloy metals for bronze (and their ores)
MetalOreUsed in
TinCassiteriteTin bronze (fast, hard-hitting)
ZincSphaleriteBismuth bronze
BismuthBismuthiniteBismuth bronze

Mixing bronze — the exact ranges

You alloy in the crucible. Add the component metals in the right proportions, then melt. The game accepts a range for each component. Stay inside it and you get bronze. Drift outside and the melt fails or yields the wrong metal. The three bronzes:

Bronze alloy ranges (v1.22.3 — config-tunable)
BronzeRecipe (by %)Strength
Tin bronze88–92% copper + 8–12% tinMines faster & hits harder; black bronze is best overall
Bismuth bronze50–70% copper + 20–30% zinc + 10–20% bismuthMore durable tools/weapons than tin; mines slower; cheap if you lack tin
Black bronze70–90% copper + 8–16% gold + 8–16% silverHighest durability/strength of all bronzes
Think in units, not just percent

Metal is counted in units (100 = 1 ingot). For a clean tin-bronze ingot, aim for about 90 units copper + 10 units tin in the crucible. For a five-ingot pour, scale to ~450 copper + ~50 tin. The crucible shows a running total and tells you when the mix is a valid alloy before you melt.

A worked tin-bronze example

Per the wiki, melting 92 bits copper + 8 bits tin yields 5 ingots of tin bronze while spending as little tin as possible — tin is the scarce metal, so this is the efficient mix. Bismuth bronze, if you lack tin, wants roughly 2–4 bismuth + 10–14 copper + 4–6 zinc nuggets per batch. Always let the crucible confirm "valid alloy" before you melt.

Cast bronze tools and a bronze anvil

Casting bronze is identical to casting copper. Pour the molten alloy into your ingot and tool molds. Replace your copper tools with bronze for the durability bump, and cast a bronze anvil. That anvil is the gate: iron cannot be worked on a copper anvil, so a bronze (or better) anvil is mandatory before the Iron Age. Also cast a bronze pickaxe for harder ore.

Don't proceed to iron without these two

A bronze anvil and a bronze pickaxe are the iron-age entry tickets. If you head to iron with only copper gear, you'll mine the ore and smelt a bloom, then be unable to hammer it. Cast the anvil first.

Roughly how long this age takes

Bound by finding tin, which is genuinely scarce — cassiterite generates rarely and its shallow nuggets are thin on the ground. Expect to spend real time prospecting. If tin won't turn up, fall back on bismuth bronze (zinc + bismuth) to get moving, then upgrade to tin bronze later. Budget 2–5 in-game days, most of it underground with the propick.

You're ready for the Iron Age when…

You've mixed a valid bronze, cast a bronze pickaxe (mines iron ore that a copper pick can't) and — the real gate — a bronze anvil (the only way to hammer an iron bloom). With both in hand, iron is finally on the table.

Common Bronze-Age mistakes

Mixing an invalid ratio and wasting a melt (watch the crucible's "valid alloy" prompt) · burning scarce tin on tools you'll soon replace in iron · heading to iron with only a copper anvil and getting stuck on the bloom · not casting a bronze pickaxe and being unable to mine iron ore.

04 — The bloomery

The Iron Age

Iron is the first metal you cannot cast. Its melting point is too high for a crucible-and-fire-pit setup. Instead you smelt ore into a spongy "bloom" in a bloomery, then hammer that bloom on an anvil to squeeze out the slag and consolidate it into wrought iron. It's the most involved tier so far, and it all runs on charcoal.

Find iron ore

Iron ores, easiest first
OreWhereNotes
Limonite (bog iron)Surface/shallow, often in wet lowlandsThe easiest starting iron; gather it without deep mining
HematiteIn stone, commonReliable mid-game iron
MagnetiteIn stoneGood yield; same bloomery process

Make charcoal first

The bloomery runs on charcoal, so make a batch before you smelt:

  1. Stack firewood on the ground in a pile (a charcoal pit), a solid cube of logs.
  2. Cover it with soil/dirt on all sides and the top so air can't reach the wood directly.
  3. Ignite it and let it smoulder. The buried firewood converts to charcoal over time. Dig it out once it's done. Make a lot, because iron and steel devour fuel.

Bloomery → bloom → wrought iron

  1. Build a bloomery (a tall clay/brick shaft over a fire base). See The Forge & The Anvil for the exact build.
  2. Charge it. Layer iron ore + charcoal into the shaft. The fuel must reach at least ~1200 °C — charcoal, anthracite or black coal qualify; plain firewood does not. A bloomery holds up to 120 nuggets.
  3. Run it ~10 hours. Light it and let it work for roughly 10 in-game hours. The conversion is about 20 iron-ore nuggets → 1 iron bloom, so a full 120-nugget charge yields up to 6 blooms.
  4. Collect the bloom. The output is an iron bloom — a rough, slag-filled lump, not a usable ingot.
  5. Hammer the bloom. Heat the bloom in your forge, set it on a bronze or iron anvil, and hammer it. This consolidates it into wrought-iron ingots. A copper anvil cannot do this step.
  6. Smith iron tools + an iron anvil. Iron is smithed, never cast. Heat an ingot, lay it on the anvil, and hammer to the tool's voxel pattern. Smith a full iron tool set and an iron anvil — note this one is welded from two parts, not cast — because steel needs it.

The blast furnace (mod)

A popular mod feature (not vanilla): the blast furnace smelts iron more efficiently and in bigger batches than a single bloomery run. If you run such a mod it's worth building once you're making iron in volume for steel. In vanilla, the bloomery is your iron furnace start to finish.

Iron is smithed, not cast

Don't look for an "iron mold." There isn't one. Every iron item comes off the anvil under a hammer. If you're trying to pour iron, you've misread the tier.

Roughly how long this age takes

The first big infrastructure age: charcoal pit, bloomery, forge, and a lot of hammering. Smelting is hands-off (~10h per run), but you'll run several charges, and hand-hammering blooms is slow. Plan on several in-game days to outfit yourself in iron — and far more charcoal than you think. This is the point where a helve hammer (see Mechanical Power) starts paying for itself.

You're ready for the Steel Age when…

You're producing iron in volume from a bloomery (or a blast furnace), you've consolidated blooms into wrought-iron ingots, and — the steel gate — you've welded an iron anvil. A steady charcoal supply and a working forge round it out.

Common Iron-Age mistakes

Running out of charcoal mid-smelt · fuelling the bloomery with something that won't hit ~1200 °C · expecting a usable ingot straight from the bloomery (it's a bloom — hammer it) · forgetting to weld an iron anvil and blocking steel · hammering blooms by hand forever instead of building a helve hammer.

05 — The endgame

The Steel Age

Steel is iron with carbon driven into it. You make it by carburizing iron ingots in a cementation furnace, packing them in charcoal and baking them for days, to get blister steel. Like an iron bloom, that must then be hammered into usable steel. It's the slowest, most fuel-hungry tier, and the top of the ladder.

The cementation process

  1. Build a cementation furnace. It has an inner chamber (the "coffin") where the iron sits. Build details: The Forge & The Anvil.
  2. Pack the coffin. Place iron ingots surrounded by charcoal inside. The charcoal is the carbon source and is not consumed by the carburizing reaction. You still burn fuel to keep the furnace hot, but the packing charcoal stays.
  3. Keep it hot for days. Fire the furnace and maintain heat. A full firing of a batch of blister-steel ingots takes about 6.67 in-game days. Set it, fuel it, and work other projects while it bakes.
  4. Hammer the blister steel. The output, blister steel, isn't finished. Like a bloom it must be hammered on an anvil (or a helve hammer) to become usable steel.

What the steel rig wants

To run steel at a reasonable pace, assemble the whole shop:

  • A cementation furnace (the carburizer).
  • An iron anvil + a forge for hammering blister steel into steel and smithing the final tools.
  • A pulverizer to crush ore for better yields (and to feed efficient ironmaking). See Mechanical Power below.
  • Lots of fuel: charcoal, and ideally coke/coal for the hotter, longer burns. Steel eats fuel, so stockpile before you start a firing.

Two details that catch people out: the packing charcoal is the carbon source and is mostly not consumed (only ~8 of 40 pieces per firing), so you reuse it; and unlike lower tiers, getting blister steel hot enough to hammer requires bellows on the forge. Break open the lid and coffin with a pickaxe to retrieve the blister-steel ingots.

Steel tools & armour are the top tier

Steel gives the strongest, longest-lasting tools and the best armour in the game. A steel pickaxe handles the hardest ore. Steel armour is your best defence when temporal storms turn nasty. From here, progression is about volume and convenience, not a new metal.

Roughly how long this age takes

The slowest tier by far, because the furnace bakes for nearly 7 in-game days per batch. The build (refractory furnace, pulverizer, bellows-equipped forge, iron anvil) is a project in itself, and you'll burn enormous fuel — ~200 coal per 16-ingot batch. Realistically the Steel Age is measured in real-world hours of play spread across many in-game days. Start a firing, then go do everything else while it cooks.

You've finished the climb when…

You've hammered blister steel into usable steel and smithed a steel pickaxe (and ideally steel armour). That's the top of the metal ladder — there's no tier above steel. From here the game is about volume, automation and exploration, covered in What's Next.

Common Steel-Age mistakes

Starting a firing without enough fuel stockpiled to finish it · trying to hammer blister steel without bellows on the forge · building the furnace from ordinary blocks instead of refractory (they melt) · expecting usable steel straight from the furnace (it's blister steel — hammer it) · skipping the pulverizer and starving the operation of ore.

Ore & Mining Reference

One table to keep open while you climb: which ore belongs to which age, where it tends to sit, and how to find it. Distribution and depths are world-gen dependent — treat these as tendencies and let the propick and Handbook give you the real picture in your world.

How to prospect efficiently

  1. Density mode first. Hold the propick, press F to Density Search, and sample as you travel. Readings come back in parts-per-thousand per ore — strong numbers mean "dig here," not "here exactly."
  2. Go deep where it's promising. Sink a staircase shaft. Many big deposits sit well below the surface, so don't give up after the first few stone layers.
  3. Switch to Node mode to home in. Once Density mode is hot, break a block in Node Search to get a direction and rough size for a specific deposit.
  4. Mine the disc. Deposits are disc-shaped — once you hit ore, fan out along the layer rather than tunnelling straight through.
Match the pick to the rock

A pickaxe only mines ore veins of its tier and below: stone can't touch veins at all, copper opens basic mining, bronze is needed for iron ore, and steel handles the hardest stone fastest. If a vein won't break, you've out-run your pick — go up a tier.

Stability drops as you dig

Underground is where temporal stability falls and drifters appear. Carry torches, light your shafts as you go, and don't mine through a temporal storm. The deeper you push for ore, the more this matters — full system in The Rust & The Gear.

07 — The thread that runs through it all

Mechanical Power

Running parallel to the metal ladder is mechanical power: gears, axles and rotational machines that automate the grind. You start it by hand in the Stone Age and scale up to water and wind by the Iron/Steel age. Treat these as milestones woven between the tiers, not a separate game.

stone age

Hand quern

An early hand-cranked quern grinds grain into flour for bread (and crushes other materials). Cheap, manual, and the first machine most players build.

bronze+

Windmill / waterwheel

A windmill or waterwheel spins an axle network to drive machines automatically, with no more cranking. Site it where you have wind or flowing water.

iron / steel

Helve hammer

A power-driven helve hammer hammers blooms and blister steel for you. A big time-saver once you're producing iron and steel in volume.

iron / steel

Pulverizer

A powered pulverizer crushes ore to boost yields, stretching every vein further. Important when steel is eating your iron supply.

The full power network (axles, gears, clutches, toggles and how to route rotation around your base) is laid out in Gears & Sails.

★ — Beyond steel

What's Next

Reaching steel is when the world starts pushing back. Two systems scale with your progress and deserve a read once your forge is running.

Storms intensify

As you advance, temporal storms grow more frequent and more violent, spawning tougher drifters even in daylight. Steel armour and a defensible, well-lit base matter more the deeper you go. The full stability-and-storm system is in The Rust & The Gear.

The map opens up

Steel-era exploration is about translocators (fast travel across the world) and traders (buy the metals and gear you're short on). Where to find them and how to use them safely is in Ruins, Roads & Traders.

You've climbed the ladder

Stone → copper → bronze → iron → steel, every rung in order. If your progress bar above reads 100%, you've built the entire core tech tree of Vintage Story from bare hands. Keep the First Day habits (stay lit, stay fed, stay walled) when those storms get serious.
