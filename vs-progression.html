The Long Road

From bare hands to a steel pickaxe is not a recipe — it's a ladder of metals, and you cannot skip a rung. Stone unlocks copper, copper unlocks bronze, bronze unlocks iron, and iron unlocks steel. Each tier needs the right ore, a way to reach working heat, the right anvil, and tools made from that metal before the next tier opens. This is the whole climb, numbered, with real values.

00 — Orientation

How to Read This Road

Vintage Story gates its whole tech tree behind metal. There is no "tech you research" — there is only the next metal, and each one is harder to get hot, harder to shape, and stronger than the last. Internalise the gate and the rest of the game makes sense.

Every metal tier on this road needs the same four things. Miss one and you're stuck, so check them off in this order:

  1. The ore (or native metal). You have to find it — surface nuggets, prospecting, or mining a vein.
  2. A way to reach working temperature. A fire pit melts copper; iron and steel need a bloomery or furnace and charcoal, not just firewood.
  3. The right anvil. You can only smith a metal on an anvil of equal-or-higher tier. Iron will not work on a copper anvil — this is the trap that strands most new players.
  4. Tools cast or smithed from it. Copper and bronze are cast in molds; iron and steel are smithed (hammered) on an anvil. The new pickaxe is what unlocks the next tier's mining.
The two hardest gates, named up front

(1) Stone tools cannot mine ore veins — you need a copper pickaxe to mine, so casting copper is the real start of the game. (2) Iron cannot be worked on a copper anvil — you must reach bronze and cast a bronze anvil before iron is even possible. Plan around those two and you'll never be confused about why you're stuck.

The ladder at a glance

  • StoneCHALCO-PREP

    Knap tools, make fire, then pottery: a fired crucible and molds. No metal happens until these exist.

  • CopperCHALCOLITHIC

    Gather native copper & ores, smelt in the crucible over a fire pit, cast a pickaxe + anvil. The pickaxe unlocks mining.

  • BronzeALLOY AGE

    Add tin / zinc / bismuth (or gold+silver) to make bronze. Cast a bronze anvil + pickaxe — the gate into iron.

  • IronBLOOMERY

    Make charcoal, smelt ore in a bloomery into a bloom, then hammer it into wrought iron on a bronze/iron anvil.

  • SteelCEMENTATION

    Carburize iron in a cementation furnace into blister steel, then hammer it into steel. Endgame tools & armour.

01 — The start

The Stone Age

You begin in the Stone Age with nothing but knapped flint. The goal of this age is not metal — it's the pottery that lets you handle metal: a fired crucible to melt ore in, and clay molds to pour it into. Everything here is a prerequisite for the Copper Age.

Tools, fire and fibre

If you've done The First Day, most of this is already behind you. Knap a knife, axe, spear, shovel and a hoe (the hoe is for farming later); the exact knapping patterns are stepped out in The Four Crafts → Knapping. Build and light a fire pit, cut firewood, and make torches (stick + dry grass) for safe light.

You also need cordage: cut cattail tops or papyrus with your knife and craft them into twine, which in turn weaves into baskets for portable storage. Twine is also the binding for many later recipes, so make a stack.

Pottery — the real work of the Stone Age

Pottery is a four-step pipeline: dig clay → clay-form an item → build a kiln → fire it. Do it carefully; a cracked crucible at the copper stage costs you hours.

  1. Dig clay. Find clay deposits (grey-brown patches, often near water). There are three kinds: blue and red clay (general pottery) and fire clay — the crucible must be fire clay because it has to survive molten-metal heat.
  2. Clay-form the vessels. Hold clay and right-click a surface to open the clay-forming grid (the layer-by-layer build is shown in The Four Crafts). Form a bowl (eating), a crock and storage vessel (food storage / sealed meals), a jug (water), and most importantly a crucible (roughly 2 clay → 1 crucible, fire clay only).
  3. Form your molds. Clay-form an ingot mold and a set of tool molds (pickaxe, axe, knife, etc.). You cast metal into these, so make the molds you'll actually want in copper.
  4. Build a pit kiln & fire it. Place the unfired pottery on the ground, stack 8 firewood around/over each piece (more pieces = a bigger pit), cover and ignite. A pit-kiln firing takes roughly 10–20 in-game hours depending on fuel; let it burn out fully before you collect the hardened pottery.
The crucible is the gate, not the bowl

You can technically reach copper with just a fired crucible + an ingot mold + one tool mold. Everything else (bowls, crocks, jugs) is quality-of-life. If you're impatient, fire the crucible, ingot mold, and a pickaxe mold first — that's the minimum to start smelting.

First cooked meals & a bed

Clay-form and fire a cooking pot too: set on a fire pit it turns raw ingredients into proper meals (soups, porridges, stews) that feed multiple nutrition groups at once — the full system is in The Larder. Finally, craft a bedroll/bed from cattail/twine to set your spawn point, so a death doesn't send you back to the map's edge.

02 — Chalcolithic

The Copper Age

Copper is the first metal and the most forgiving: it shows up as native nuggets on the surface, and it melts cool enough that a humble fire pit can do it. This age turns your fired crucible into your first real tools — and, critically, your first pickaxe, which is what finally lets you mine.

Find the copper

Walk ridgelines and exposed stone and pick up surface copper nuggets — you can gather a starter batch by hand, no mining required. The copper-bearing ores you'll see are:

Common copper sources in the early game

Native copperOrange-red nuggets & veins in surface stoneSmelts at the best ratio; easiest start
MalachiteGreen ore in/on stoneVery common copper ore

TetrahedriteGrey metallic oreCopper-bearing; also a later source of other metals
Stone tools can't mine veins

A flint or stone pickaxe cannot break ore blocks in a vein — you can only pick up loose surface bits. That's the whole point of copper: a copper pickaxe is the key that unlocks mining. Until you cast one, you are limited to what's lying on the surface.

Smelt & cast

  1. Load the crucible. Put copper nuggets/ore into your fired crucible. A unit of metal is 100 "units" = 1 ingot; fill to the number of ingots you want (the crucible holds several).
  2. Heat it in the fire pit. Place the crucible on a burning fire pit fuelled with charcoal or peat (they burn hotter than plain firewood). Copper melts at about 1084°C, which a charcoal/peat fire pit reaches. Watch the crucible's temperature readout climb until the contents read "molten".
  3. Cast an ingot. Pick up the hot crucible and right-click your ingot mold to pour. Let it cool, then collect the ingot. Always cast at least one ingot for trading/storage.
  4. Cast tools. Pour molten copper into your tool molds — make the pickaxe first, then axe, knife, shovel, hoe, propick. Combine each cast head with a stick to finish the tool. Full casting walkthrough: The Four Crafts → Casting.

Cast an anvil, build a forge

You'll want to smith from the Bronze Age on, and smithing needs two things you set up now: a copper anvil (cast it from copper — it's a large pour, several ingots) and a forge to heat metal hot enough to work on that anvil. The forge is a small masonry firebox you fuel with charcoal; the full smithing rig is detailed in The Forge & The Anvil.

Two milestones, remember them

A copper pickaxe unlocks ore mining; a copper anvil unlocks smithing. With both, you can finally dig deep for the alloy metals the Bronze Age needs.

03 — The alloy age

The Bronze Age

Bronze is copper improved with a second (or third) metal. It's stronger and more durable than pure copper — and, decisively, a bronze anvil is the only way into iron. This age is about prospecting for alloy metals and getting the ratios right in your crucible.

Now you can really mine

With your copper pickaxe and a prospecting pick (propick), go underground. The propick samples the rock and reports nearby ore density — use it to follow promising readings. The alloy metals you're hunting:

Mixing bronze — the exact ranges

You alloy in the crucible: add the component metals in the right proportions, then melt. The game accepts a range for each component — stay inside it and you get bronze; drift outside and the melt fails or yields the wrong metal. The three bronzes:

Bronze alloy ranges (v1.22.3 — config-tunable)
BronzeRecipe (by %)Strength
Tin bronze88–92% copper + 8–12% tinBest of the common bronzes — beats bismuth bronze
Bismuth bronze50–70% copper + 20–30% zinc + 10–20% bismuthWeakest of the three, but cheap if you lack tin
Black bronze70–90% copper + 8–16% gold + 8–16% silverHighest durability/strength of all bronzes
Think in units, not just percent

Metal is counted in units (100 = 1 ingot). For a clean tin-bronze ingot, aim for about 90 units copper + 10 units tin in the crucible; for a five-ingot pour, scale to ~450 copper + ~50 tin. The crucible shows a running total and tells you when the mix is a valid alloy before you melt.

Cast bronze tools and a bronze anvil

Casting bronze is identical to casting copper — pour the molten alloy into your ingot and tool molds. Replace your copper tools with bronze for the durability bump, and cast a bronze anvil. That anvil is the gate: iron cannot be worked on a copper anvil, so a bronze (or better) anvil is mandatory before the Iron Age. Make sure you also have a bronze pickaxe for harder ore.

Don't proceed to iron without these two

A bronze anvil and a bronze pickaxe are the iron-age entry tickets. If you head to iron with only copper gear, you'll mine the ore, smelt a bloom — and then be unable to hammer it. Cast the anvil first.

04 — The bloomery

The Iron Age

Iron is the first metal you cannot cast — its melting point is too high for a crucible-and-fire-pit setup. Instead you smelt ore into a spongy "bloom" in a bloomery, then hammer that bloom on an anvil to squeeze out the slag and consolidate it into wrought iron. It's the most involved tier so far, and it all runs on charcoal.

Find iron ore

Iron ores, easiest first
OreWhereNotes
Limonite (bog iron)Surface/shallow, often in wet lowlandsThe easiest starting iron — gather it without deep mining
HematiteIn stone, commonReliable mid-game iron
MagnetiteIn stoneGood yield; same bloomery process

Make charcoal first

The bloomery runs on charcoal, so you make a batch before you smelt:

  1. Stack firewood on the ground in a pile (a charcoal pit) — a solid cube of logs.
  2. Cover it with soil/dirt on all sides and the top so air can't reach the wood directly.
  3. Ignite it and let it smoulder. Over time the buried firewood converts to charcoal — dig it out once it's done. Make a lot; iron and steel devour fuel.

Bloomery → bloom → wrought iron

  1. Build a bloomery (a tall clay/brick shaft over a fire base) — see The Forge & The Anvil for the exact build.
  2. Charge it. Layer iron ore + charcoal into the shaft (the Handbook shows the ore-to-fuel ratio). Light it and let it run hot.
  3. Collect the bloom. When it finishes, the bloomery yields an iron bloom — a rough, slag-filled lump, not a usable ingot.
  4. Hammer the bloom. Heat the bloom in your forge, set it on a bronze or iron anvil, and hammer it. This consolidates it and you work it into wrought-iron ingots. (This is the step a copper anvil cannot do.)
  5. Smith iron tools + an iron anvil. Iron is smithed, never cast: heat an ingot, lay it on the anvil, and hammer to the tool's voxel pattern. Smith a full iron tool set and an iron anvil (the anvil you need for steel).
1.22 — the blast furnace

Vintage Story 1.22 adds (and refines) the blast furnace, which smelts iron more efficiently and in bigger batches than a single bloomery run. Once you're producing iron in volume — especially heading into steel — it's worth the build. The bloomery is still the right first step.

Iron is smithed, not cast

Don't waste time looking for an "iron mold" — there isn't one. Every iron item comes off the anvil under a hammer. If you're trying to pour iron, you've misread the tier.

05 — The endgame

The Steel Age

Steel is iron with carbon driven into it. You make it by carburizing iron ingots in a cementation furnace — packing them in charcoal and baking them for days — to get blister steel, which (like an iron bloom) must then be hammered into usable steel. It's the slowest, most fuel-hungry tier, and the top of the ladder.

The cementation process

  1. Build a cementation furnace. It has an inner chamber (the "coffin") where the iron sits. Build details: The Forge & The Anvil.
  2. Pack the coffin. Place iron ingots surrounded by charcoal inside. The charcoal is the carbon source — and notably it is not consumed by the carburizing reaction (you still burn fuel to keep the furnace hot, but the packing charcoal stays).
  3. Keep it hot — for days. Fire the furnace and maintain heat. A full firing of a batch of blister-steel ingots takes about 6.67 in-game days. This is a "set it, fuel it, and live your life" process — work other projects while it bakes.
  4. Hammer the blister steel. The output, blister steel, isn't finished — like a bloom it must be hammered on an anvil (or a helve hammer) to become usable steel.

What the steel rig wants

To run steel at any reasonable pace you'll want the whole shop assembled:

  • A cementation furnace (the carburizer).
  • An iron anvil + a forge for hammering blister steel into steel and smithing the final tools.
  • A pulverizer to crush ore for better yields (and to feed efficient ironmaking) — see Mechanical Power below.
  • Lots of fuel: charcoal, and ideally coke/coal for the hotter, longer burns. Steel eats fuel; stockpile before you start a firing.
Steel tools & armour are the top tier

Steel gives the strongest, longest-lasting tools and the best armour in the game. A steel pickaxe tears through the hardest ore; steel armour is your best defence when temporal storms turn nasty. From here, progression is about volume and convenience, not a new metal.

06 — The thread that runs through it all

Mechanical Power

Running parallel to the metal ladder is mechanical power — gears, axles and rotational machines that automate the grind. You start it by hand in the Stone Age and scale it up to water and wind by the Iron/Steel age. Treat these as milestones woven between the tiers, not a separate game.

stone age

Hand quern

An early hand-cranked quern grinds grain into flour for bread (and crushes other materials). Cheap, manual, and the first machine most players build.

bronze+

Windmill / waterwheel

A windmill or waterwheel spins an axle network to drive machines automatically — no more cranking. Site it where you have wind or flowing water.

iron / steel

Helve hammer

A power-driven helve hammer hammers blooms and blister steel for you — a huge time-saver once you're producing iron and steel in volume.

iron / steel

Pulverizer

A powered pulverizer crushes ore to boost yields, stretching every vein further — essential when steel is eating your iron supply.

The full power network — axles, gears, clutches, toggles and how to route rotation around your base — is laid out in Gears & Sails.

★ — Beyond steel

What's Next

Reaching steel isn't the end of the world — it's when the world starts pushing back. Two systems scale with your progress and deserve a read once your forge is humming.

Storms intensify

As you advance, temporal storms grow more frequent and more violent, spawning tougher drifters even in daylight. Steel armour and a defensible, well-lit base matter more the deeper you go. The whole stability-and-storm system is in The Rust & The Gear.

The map opens up

Steel-era exploration is about translocators (fast travel across the world) and traders (buy the metals and gear you're short on). Where to find them and how to use them safely is in Ruins, Roads & Traders.

You've climbed the ladder

Stone → copper → bronze → iron → steel, every rung in order. If your progress bar above reads 100%, you've built the entire core tech tree of Vintage Story from bare hands. Now go make something beautiful with it — and keep the First Day habits (stay lit, stay fed, stay walled) when those storms get serious.
