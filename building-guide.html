An architect's field manual for the four mods that turn To the Moon 2.0 from a tech grind into a place worth living in. Chipped explodes every vanilla block into hundreds of textures; Supplementaries and Decorative Blocks fill your rooms with props that actually do things; and Building Gadgets lets you place, swap and copy whole structures in a heartbeat. 00 — Orientation

Start Here

Pretty builds are not one skill — they are three working together. You need a palette (textures that read well at a glance), props (functional and decorative blocks that give a space purpose), and speed (tools so the labour of placing thousands of blocks never kills the idea). This guide maps one mod onto each, then a fourth that multiplies them all.

Think of these four mods as the three pillars of a build, plus the crane that raises them:

palette

Chipped

Takes vanilla blocks you already have and offers a huge wall of decorative variants. Pure texture choice — no new materials to grind, just taste.

props · function

Supplementaries

A grab-bag of functional and decorative blocks: lanterns, signs, faucets & jars, planters, flags, pulleys, safes. The stuff that makes a room feel inhabited.

props · form

Decorative Blocks

Elegant, simple builder blocks — beams, braziers, seats, thatch, palisades. The bones of medieval and cozy architecture.

power tool

Building Gadgets

Place, swap, copy and clear blocks by the volume. The crane that lets the other three pillars go up in minutes, not evenings.

The mental model

Every good build in this pack moves through the same loop, regardless of theme. Hold this in your head and the rest of the guide is just the parts catalogue:

  1. Rough the shape — block out walls, floors and roofs fast with the Building Gadget (§04), in any cheap placeholder block.
  2. Swap the texture — once the massing looks right, use the Exchanging Gadget to flip the placeholder to your real material in one drag.
  3. Pick the palette — run that material through Chipped (§01) to choose the exact variant that sells the theme.
  4. Dress the set — fill it with props from Supplementaries (§02) and Decorative Blocks (§03): lighting, seating, signage, storage, greenery.
JEI is your index

All four mods register cleanly with JEI. If you can't remember which workbench makes a variant or how a faucet is crafted, look the block up in JEI and read the recipe and usage tabs. This guide describes things qualitatively on purpose — the in-game GUIs are the authoritative source.

01 — The palette explosion

Chipped — One Block, A Hundred Faces

Chipped (by terrariumearth) is the simplest pillar to explain and the most transformative in practice. It adds no new materials and almost no new mechanics. Instead, it takes blocks you already have — stone, oak, glass, terracotta, wool, sandstone, bricks and many more — and lets you craft them into a sprawling library of decorative variants: tiled, chiselled, mossy, cracked, layered, pillared, and countless other textures. counts approximate

How it works: the workbenches

Chipped routes each family of base block through a themed workbench. You craft the bench once, place it, then open it and pick a base block; a scrollable palette of every variant for that block appears, and you craft the one you want. The benches are organised by material so the right tool always feels obvious:

noteThe exact bench-to-block mapping can shift between Chipped versions and is sometimes consolidated. If a base block isn't where you expect it, search the block in JEI — its usage tab points you at the correct bench.

The cardinal rule: it's cosmetic

Chipped variants are purely decorative. A Mason Table tile of stone behaves like stone; a Chipped oak plank still crafts and burns like oak. Most variants are reversible — you can usually convert a variant back to its base block, so experimenting costs you nothing but the trip to the bench.

free taste

No new grind

You spend the same ingots, logs and stone you already mine. The only "cost" is choosing — which is the whole point.

read at range

Texture variety

Real buildings mix textures. A wall of one stone reads flat; a wall that alternates tiles, bricks and chiselled blocks reads built.

pairs with §04

Exchanger-friendly

Because variants share a base, the Exchanging Gadget swaps between them across a whole wall in one stroke.

Workflow tip

Craft a few stacks of your three or four favourite variants before you start building, and keep them on the hotbar. With the variants in your inventory, the Building Gadget (§04) can place them by the volume — Chipped's palette plus a gadget is the fastest way to put up a beautiful wall in the game.

02 — The grab-bag

Supplementaries — Props That Do Things

Supplementaries (by MehVahdJukaar) is a giant, lovingly-detailed grab-bag of blocks that sit halfway between decoration and function. Where Chipped changes how a wall looks, Supplementaries changes how a room works — lighting it, labelling it, storing in it, and filling it with the small fixtures that make a space feel lived-in. It's powered by the author's Selene library and is deeply config-driven, so almost everything can be toggled. selection approximate

The decorative line-up

The other half of the mod is pure atmosphere. These are the blocks you reach for when a room is built but feels empty:

  • Sconces, Candle Holders & Lanterns — a wide range of wall and standing light sources in many styles; the connective tissue of any cozy interior.
  • Ropes — strung between blocks for hanging lanterns, climbing, and connecting pulleys.
  • Planters — grow flowers and crops decoratively indoors or on sills.
  • Flags & Banners — large hanging cloth for halls, gates and faction identity.
  • Globe & Clock — desk and wall fixtures; the Clock genuinely tells in-game time.
  • Bellows & Ash — forge and hearth detailing for workshops and smithies.
  • Daily Goods — a trading fixture offering rotating daily deals.
Selene config

Supplementaries exposes an unusually rich config (and an in-game config screen). If a block feels overpowered, too common in loot, or you simply don't want it, you can almost always disable or tune it without removing the mod. Check the config before assuming a behaviour is fixed.

Don't over-furnish

Because the mod offers so much, the classic mistake is cramming every surface. Lean on a few repeated motifs — one lantern style, one sign style, one planter — so a build reads as designed rather than as a parts bin.

03 — The builder's kit

Decorative Blocks — Bones For Cozy Builds

Decorative Blocks (by stohun) is the most restrained of the three palette mods, and that restraint is its strength. It's a tight set of elegant, simple builder blocks — the structural and comfort pieces that vanilla never quite supplied. Where Supplementaries fills a room with objects, Decorative Blocks gives the room its frame: beams overhead, seats to sit on, thatch on the roof, a brazier for warmth. It's the natural toolkit for medieval, rustic and cozy architecture. selection approximate

Why it pairs so well with Chipped

Decorative Blocks supplies the shapes vanilla lacks (beams, lattice, seats); Chipped supplies the textures. Build the frame in Decorative Blocks, fill the panels with a Chipped variant, and a plain box becomes a timber-framed cottage with almost no extra blocks.

04 — The power tools

Building Gadgets — Build By The Volume

Building Gadgets (by Direwolf20) is the power-builder's toolkit: four gadgets that place, swap, copy and clear blocks in bulk, turning hours of block-by-block labour into a few clicks. They draw blocks either from your inventory or from a stockpile of Construction Paste, and they're what make the rest of this guide practical at scale.

The four gadgets

The gadget line-up
GadgetWhat it doesPulls blocks from
Building GadgetPlaces and extends areas of a chosen block instantly — floors, walls, surfaces, large flat or shaped fills.Inventory or Construction Paste
Exchanging GadgetSwaps existing blocks for a new block in place — retexture a wall without tearing it down.Inventory or Construction Paste
Copy-Paste GadgetCopies a structure to a template, then pastes that template elsewhere as a ghost preview you confirm.Construction Paste (for the paste)
Destruction GadgetClears whole volumes of blocks in one action — demolition and site-clearing.—

Each gadget has a mode and a range you adjust through its menu (and a configurable hotkey). The Building Gadget, for example, can lay a surface, build a wall, or grid out a fixed area; the Destruction Gadget can carve a box of an adjustable size. Hold the gadget and the world shows a ghost preview of what will happen before you commit.

Construction Paste & the paste workflow

Construction Paste is the gadgets' universal building material — a neutral substance that the gadgets can shape into any block when paired with a template. You craft paste and store it in a Paste Container (which clips onto a gadget to feed it) or produce/hold larger volumes via the Construction Chamber / Charging-station style block. This is what makes the Copy-Paste Gadget able to rebuild a structure even when you don't have all the original blocks on hand.

  1. Make Construction Paste and load it into a Paste Container, then attach the container to the gadget that needs it.
  2. Copy a structure: aim the Copy-Paste Gadget, select the two corners of the region, and it stores the design to a Template.
  3. Position the paste: switch to paste mode and place a ghost preview of the template where you want the copy to appear.
  4. Align & rotate the ghost until it sits correctly — the preview shows exactly which blocks will be placed.
  5. Confirm: the gadget spends Construction Paste (and any required blocks) to materialise the structure instantly.
  6. Reuse the template anywhere — copy a tower once, paste a skyline.
Inventory vs. paste

For everyday building, feeding the Building Gadget straight from your inventory is simplest — it just consumes the real blocks. Reach for Construction Paste when you're copy-pasting structures whose exact block list you don't have, or when you want a single material reservoir to draw from.

It costs energy

The gadgets run on stored energy (and the blocks/paste they place). A drained gadget won't build — keep it charged at a charger/energy block, and remember that bigger ranges and bigger pastes drain it faster. Plan a charging spot near your build site.

05 — Method

A Build Workflow, End To End

Here is the whole pattern book in one sequence — the method that ties all four mods together. Follow it once and it becomes second nature; every build in the pack, from a tavern to a space station, is a variation on these five moves.

  1. Rough the massing — with the Building Gadget (§04), block out the footprint, walls and roof in any cheap placeholder (cobble, dirt). Don't fuss the texture yet; you're solving silhouette and proportion. Use the Destruction Gadget to undo mistakes by the volume.
  2. Swap to real materials — once the shape reads right, drag the Exchanging Gadget across each surface to flip the placeholder to your chosen block. Walls become stone, roof becomes wood, in seconds.
  3. Choose the exact variant — take that material to the matching Chipped bench (§01) and pick the precise texture: tiled vs. bricked stone, panelled vs. plank wood. Mix two or three variants for depth, and Exchange them in where you want accents.
  4. Add the frame — lace in Decorative Blocks (§03): beams across the ceiling, lattice in the windows, thatch or beams on the roofline, palisades around the yard.
  5. Dress the interior — finish with Supplementaries (§02): sconces and candle holders for light, seats and a brazier for warmth, item shelves and a notice board for storage and signage, planters and flags for life.
order matters

Shape before skin

Resist texturing early. A beautifully-textured wall in the wrong place is just expensive rework. Lock the silhouette first.

copy yourself

Template the repeats

Built one perfect window bay or one tower? Copy-Paste it (§04) along the facade instead of rebuilding. Consistency is free.

light last

Light to taste

Place lighting after the room is built and furnished, so you can see where shadows actually fall and aim sconces accordingly.

The exchange trick

The single highest-leverage move in this pack is Exchanging Gadget + Chipped variants. Build a whole structure in one base block, then audition entire palettes by exchanging the wall to a new variant in one stroke. You can re-theme a finished build in minutes — try the same cottage in oak, then dark oak, then spruce, and keep the winner.

Tie it to the rest of the pack

These mods are the wrapper; the other guides supply the machinery that fills the build. Use them together:

  • Industrial Base → house a Mekanism ore-processing line or reactor behind plated metal and faucet-and-jar plumbing. Hide cables in Decorative Blocks beams.
  • Space Station → frame an Ad Astra launch pad or moon base in frosted glass and bulkhead ribs, with notice boards as control consoles.
  • Cozy Tavern → pair with Farmer's Delight for a working kitchen behind the bar panels, and Create contraptions for moving signage.

Steal proportions, not just blocks

A palette is more than a block list — it's also scale and repetition. Keep window sizes, beam spacing and lantern intervals consistent across a build, and even a humble material set will read as intentional architecture.

07 — Field notes

Tips & Gotchas

The small things that separate a smooth build session from a frustrating one. Most of these are about the gadgets, because that's where the surprises live.

Building Gadgets

  • Source Construction Paste before a big paste. Copy-Paste and large fills can burn through paste fast — stock a Paste Container (or a Construction Chamber's worth) ahead of time so you're not interrupted mid-build. paste cost approximate
  • Watch the energy bar. Gadgets are powered; a flat gadget silently does nothing. Keep a charger at the build site and top up between sessions. Larger ranges drain faster.
  • Copy-paste alignment is the classic pitfall. The ghost preview anchors to where you point — take the moment to rotate and nudge it before confirming. A misaligned paste means a Destruction Gadget cleanup and a redo.
  • Match the gadget to the job. Use the Exchanging Gadget to retexture, not the Building Gadget — Building places into air, Exchanging swaps existing blocks. Mixing them up overbuilds or no-ops.
  • The Destruction Gadget is undo. Treat its adjustable box as your bulk eraser during the rough-massing phase; it makes experimentation cheap.

Chipped

  • Variants are cosmetic and usually reversible — experiment freely; you can convert most variants back to base.
  • Can't find a block's bench? Ask JEI. The block's usage tab points at the correct workbench when the mapping isn't obvious.
  • Stock favourites for the gadget. Pre-craft stacks of your chosen variants so the Building Gadget can place them by the volume.

Supplementaries & Decorative Blocks

  • Check the Selene config before assuming a Supplementaries behaviour (loot frequency, a block being enabled) is locked — most of it is tunable, including an in-game config screen.
  • Seats and faucets are placement-sensitive. Seats want a clear sit-space; faucets want a valid source block to drain. If a function doesn't trigger, check the adjacency before assuming a bug.
  • Restraint reads as design. Repeat a small set of motifs rather than using one of everything — the catalogue is huge, but a build doesn't need all of it.
Before you bulldoze

The Destruction Gadget and large Building/Exchanging operations are not trivially undoable beyond eyeballing it. On a shared world especially, double-check the ghost preview, mind your range, and don't fire a volume operation toward someone else's build. Measure twice, click once.
