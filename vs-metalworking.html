Fire & Metal

Turn rock into steel. Every metal needs three things: the material, enough heat to melt or work it, and the right anvil tier to shape it. Cast copper and bronze by pouring them molten into molds. Smith iron and steel by hammering them hot on an anvil. Iron and steel are never cast. Climb the heat ladder one furnace at a time.

00 — Orientation

Overview · The Heat Ladder

Metalworking is a ladder. The rungs are temperatures. Each new metal needs a hotter heat source and a tougher anvil than the last. The two families of metal are worked in different ways.

Cast metals — poured

Melt copper and the bronzes in a crucible and pour them molten into clay molds. The mold shapes ingots, tools, and the anvil itself. Casting is the early game.

Smithed metals — hammered

Iron and steel can never be cast. They leave a furnace as a spongy bloom or blister. Hammer them to consolidate, then heat and smith them to shape on an anvil.

Three requirements gate every metal:

1 · material

The ore or alloy

The right ore, or the right mix of metals for an alloy. Stone and flint can't mine ore. You need at least a copper pickaxe.

2 · heat

Enough heat

Hot enough to melt it for casting, or to bring it to working temperature for smithing. Copper's 1084°C sets the early bar.

3 · anvil

The right anvil tier

For smithed metals, the anvil must be made of a hard-enough metal. A copper anvil can't smith iron. Steel requires an iron anvil.

The heat sources climb in order, each hotter than the last:

  • 1 Fire pit
  • 2 Forge
  • 3 Bloomery
  • 4 Blast furnace mod
  • 5 Cementation furnace
Read the ladder top to bottom

Sections 02–04 are the cast path (crucible → alloy → mold) for copper and bronze tools. Sections 05–10 are the smith path (forge → anvil → bloomery → cementation) for iron and steel. Charcoal (07) fuels almost everything past the forge.

01 — Raw materials

Ores & Where They Hide

You need ore before you can smelt, and ore is mostly underground. Surface metal nuggets mark a vein below them. Ore must be mined with a metal pickaxe; flint and stone won't break it. The pick has a tier gate: copper mines most ores, but iron ore needs a bronze (tier-3) pickaxe and meteoric iron needs an iron one. Different metals sit at different depths and in different rock types. The full tier ladder is in the Codex.

Find it before you dig it

Don't tunnel blind. The prospecting pick reads the density of nearby ore, and a node-search mode hunts specific deposits. The full method and the ore-by-depth chart are in The Lay of the Land. Pick up surface nuggets as you walk. Each one marks a vein worth a propick reading.

Depth & host rock decide what you find

Copper sits at the surface almost everywhere (native copper bits, or surface malachite in limestone country) — except where the top layer is bauxite. The three iron ores share a depth band: all generate between roughly 0.05 and 0.85 of sea level (about Y5–Y93 on a default-height world), but each prefers a different host rock, so the rock you're digging through tells you which iron to expect. Match your search to the stone type, not just the depth.

Fuels

Fuel varies in how hot and how long it burns. From handy to high-grade:

Two fuel rules that catch people out

(1) Bellows matter. Without a worked bellows, a fuel reaches only about 60% of its rated temperature. A fire pit needs the bellows (or a forge) to melt copper. (2) The bloomery takes any fuel that reaches ≥1200°C: charcoal, anthracite, or bituminous (black) coal. It will not take lignite, which is too cool.

You need copper before you need iron

Ore demands a metal pickaxe, so copper, the first metal you smelt, is what unlocks mining. A copper pickaxe mines copper, tin, and the other early ores, but not iron. For iron ore you must first reach bronze and cast a bronze pick. The order is fixed: copper pick → mine tin → bronze pick → then hunt for iron. See the casting path below, then The Long Road for the full Stone→Steel arc.

02 — Melting it down

The Crucible & Smelting

To melt ore you need a crucible, a fire-clay vessel that holds ore nuggets while heat does the work. Form it from fire clay on the clay-forming grid and harden it in a pit kiln before it can take the fire. Both steps are covered in The Four Crafts.

  1. Fill the crucible. Right-click ore nuggets into it. It holds a large batch, enough for several ingots in one pour (1 ingot = 100 units = 20 nuggets). Use one metal for pure copper, or a deliberate mix for an alloy (section 03).
  2. Heat it. Place the filled crucible in a fire pit (or later a forge) and feed it fuel. With charcoal and the bellows worked, a fire pit reaches copper's melting point (1084°C). Without a bellows, most fuels manage only ~60% of their rated heat.
  3. Watch it melt. The contents turn molten once they pass the metal's melting temperature. The crucible's tooltip shows the temperature and the units of liquid metal inside.
  4. Pour. Pick up the hot crucible and right-click a mold to pour (section 04). Mind the heat; hot metal will burn you.

Copper's 1084°C is the headline number

Every bronze is mostly copper, so they all need a fuel that hits roughly copper's melting point. If your crucible stalls below 1084°C, switch to charcoal and bank the fire pit with plenty of fuel.

The 1340°C line: castable vs furnace-only

There's a hard cut-off. Metals that melt at or below ~1340°C can be liquefied in a crucible and cast — copper, the bronzes, tin, lead, zinc, silver, gold and more. Metals above that line — iron, meteoric iron and steel — can never be reached by a crucible. They're won as a solid bloom or blister from a furnace and then smithed, never poured. This single threshold is why the game splits into a cast half and a smith half.

Crucible capacity, in numbers

A crucible holds up to 4 stacks of 128 nuggets of one metal or alloy — 2,560 units = 25 ingots per melt. Each nugget is 5 units; an ingot is 100 units = 20 nuggets. Smelt one big batch rather than many small ones to save fuel, but remember you can't mix two finished metals in the same crucible.

03 — Better than copper

Alloying · Bronze

Pure copper is soft. Mix the right metals in the crucible within tight percentage ranges and you get bronze, the workhorse of the early metal age. Fill the crucible with nuggets in the right proportions and smelt. The game registers the alloy if your ratios land inside the band.

The three bronzes (mix to these ranges in the crucible)
BronzeRecipe rangeStanding
Tin bronze88–92% Copper + 8–12% TinBest of the ordinary bronzes. Stronger than bismuth bronze.
Bismuth bronze50–70% Copper + 20–30% Zinc + 10–20% BismuthEasiest to source, but the weakest of the three.
Black bronze70–90% Copper + 8–16% Gold + 8–16% SilverStrongest and most durable bronze. Costly, since it eats gold and silver.
Strength order

Black bronze > tin bronze > bismuth bronze. Make whatever your ore lets you make first. Any bronze beats copper for tools and gets you a bronze anvil, which is required to smith iron (section 06).

All bronzes run hot

Copper makes up the bulk of every recipe, so its 1084°C dominates the melt. Whichever bronze you mix, plan for a charcoal-grade fire. The tin or bismuth melts long before the copper does, so the copper is always the limiting temperature.

04 — Pouring the shape

Molds & Casting

Casting turns copper and bronze into objects. Pour molten metal from the crucible into a clay mold, let it cool, and pop out the result. Molds are reusable, clay-formed and pit-kiln fired like the crucible.

mold

Ingot mold

Casts ingots, the standard storable and tradeable metal bar and the feedstock for the forge. A single ingot mold holds several ingots' worth of pour.

mold

Tool molds

Cast specific tool heads directly: pickaxe, axe, knife, hammer, and more. Each tool has its own mold shape.

mold

The anvil

The anvil itself is cast from metal poured into its mold. Your first anvil is copper or bronze. The anvil unlocks the entire smithing half of the game.

Top up a mold while it's hot

You can pour into a mold across multiple crucibles as long as the metal hasn't solidified. For the big 900-unit anvil pour, line up enough molten metal first; if it cools mid-fill you'll have to re-melt and start the pour over.

Iron and steel cannot be cast

No mold will hold iron or steel. Their melting points are out of reach of these furnaces, and the game forbids casting them outright. From iron onward, everything is smithed on an anvil.

05 — Working temperature

The Forge 1.22 overhaul

Use the forge to bring ingots, blooms, and partly-worked metal up to working temperature before taking them to the anvil. It doesn't melt metal for casting. It heats solid metal so it can be hammered. Version 1.22 reworked the forge into a deeper station.

new

Bellows

Pump the bellows to drive air into the fire and raise its temperature faster. A worked bellows keeps the forge pacing your hammering.

new

Tongs

Metal tongs hold and move hot work without burning yourself. Essential once metal is glowing.

new

Fuel mechanics

Fuel type now matters. Charcoal, coal, and coke burn hotter and longer than peat or lignite. Match the fuel to the metal you're heating.

new

Quench & temper

A quenching (water) and tempering system trades durability for power on tool heads. Get sharper, harder-hitting tools at the cost of how long they last.

The smithing workflow

  1. Heat the ingot (or bloom) in the forge until it glows at working temperature. Pump the bellows to get there faster.
  2. Move it to the anvil with tongs and set it on the anvil face.
  3. Hammer it toward the blueprint, voxel by voxel, following the Handbook plan.
  4. Reheat as it cools. Metal only takes shape while hot. When it dulls, return it to the forge and bring it back up. Repeat until the piece is finished, then quench or temper if desired.
Keep the bellows breathing

Keep the forge well-fed with the bellows worked. A hot forge means fewer reheats and more progress per heat. Stockpile charcoal or coke before a big smithing session.

What the bellows actually buys you

The bellows were added in 1.22 alongside the new forge model, and they change the maths of every heat source they're attached to:

  • No bellows = 60% of rated heat. An un-bellowed forge tops out at roughly 60% of the fuel's maximum temperature. With lignite (1100°C) that's barely enough to reach copper; with charcoal you clear it comfortably.
  • Worked bellows = full temperature, faster. Pumping drives the fire to the fuel's rated ceiling and gets there quicker, so metal spends less time below working heat.
  • A bellows can be hand-pumped or, later, driven by mechanical power so the forge and bloomery run hot without you standing on the lever — see Gears & Grindstones.

Quench & temper

1.22's heat-treatment loop lets you tune a finished tool head before you haft it:

  1. Heat the finished head back up in the forge.
  2. Quench it in water to harden — this raises its working power.
  3. Temper to dial back brittleness, trading some durability for a harder-hitting edge.
Working temperature is ~50% of melting point

As a rule a metal becomes smithable above about half its melting temperature, which is why the orange-to-yellow glow is the target at the anvil. Iron blooms consolidate at a lower bar (~700°C) and lead works at any heat. The exact thresholds per metal are in the Handbook (H).

06 — Shaping iron

The Anvil & Smithing Iron

You can't smith anything without an anvil, and the anvil's tier matters. An anvil works its own metal tier and one tier above it. A bronze anvil shapes the higher-tier iron; a copper anvil, two tiers below iron, cannot. This gate paces the back half of the game.

  • Copper anvilFIRST RUNG

    Your first anvil, cast from copper. Smiths copper and bronze, enough to bootstrap better tools and a bronze anvil.
  • Bronze anvilREQUIRED FOR IRON

    Iron requires at least a bronze anvil. A copper anvil won't smith iron. Cast a bronze one first. Bronze is a mandatory stepping stone.
  • Iron anvilREQUIRED FOR STEEL

    Steel requires an iron anvil. Once you can smith iron on the bronze anvil, make an iron anvil. It's the prerequisite for working blister steel into ingots (section 10).

To smith, heat an ingot in the forge (section 05) and hammer it to a Handbook blueprint on the anvil. The in-game plan shows which voxels to add and remove. The full hammering minigame, with worked examples, is in The Four Crafts.

Don't over-build the anvil

You never need a steel anvil to make steel — an iron anvil (tier 3) smiths steel ingots fine. A steel anvil is a quality-of-life upgrade (more durable, marginally better results), not a gate. Spend your first hard-won steel on tools, not on an anvil you don't need yet.

Each tier casts the next

Copper → bronze → iron is a chain of anvils. Use each anvil to make the tools and parts for the next metal, then build the next anvil. You can't skip a rung.

07 — The key fuel

Charcoal

Charcoal unlocks the high-temperature half of the ladder. It's the usual bloomery fuel and the carbon source for steel, so make charcoal before you smelt iron. Make it in a charcoal pit by smothering burning logs so they smolder instead of burning to ash.

  1. Stack firewood logs. Lay a pile of firewood on the ground. The bigger the pile, the more charcoal per burn.
  2. Cover it completely. Bury the entire stack in soil/dirt (any non-flammable cover works) so no wood is exposed. Any gap lets it burn to ash instead of smoldering.
  3. Ignite, then seal fast. Light the pile and close the last cover block within ~30 seconds of ignition. Sealed, it smolders rather than flames.
  4. Wait ~18 hours. Over roughly 18 in-game hours the smoldering wood converts to charcoal. Dig it out once it's done.
Seal it or lose it

An uncovered or leaky pit, or one not sealed within ~30 seconds of lighting, burns your firewood to nothing. Cover every side, and make a lot. A single big pit can convert well over a thousand firewood, and the bloomery and cementation furnace are hungry.

08 — Smelting iron

The Bloomery

Iron can't be cast or melted in a crucible, so smelt it in a built bloomery, a furnace that reduces iron ore into a spongy iron bloom. The bloom isn't a finished ingot. Hammer it to drive out the slag and consolidate the metal.

  1. Build the bloomery and load it with iron ore (up to 120 nuggets) and a high-heat fuel: charcoal, anthracite, or bituminous coal (anything reaching ≥1200°C; not lignite). Load ore before fuel.
  2. Fire it (~10 hours). It yields 1 bloom per 20 ore nuggets, up to 6 blooms a burn. Then break it open.
  3. Collect the iron bloom. It's a rough, porous lump, not a clean ingot.
  4. Hammer the bloom on an anvil (bronze or better) to consolidate it into wrought-iron ingots.
  5. Forge & smith those ingots into tools on the anvil, the same as any other metal.

A bloom is not an ingot — yet

What you pull from a bloomery is a porous, slag-laced iron bloom. Heat it (it works above ~700°C) and hammer it on a bronze-or-better anvil to squeeze out the slag and consolidate it into wrought-iron ingots. Skip this step and you have no usable iron. A helve hammer automates the tedium once you have rotational power.

Bog iron is the gentle start

Limonite (bog iron) sits near the surface, so your first iron rarely needs deep mining. Find a bog, gather limonite, and a bloomery turns it into your first iron tools without a blast furnace.

09 — Iron at scale

The Blast Furnace mod

Heads up: the blast furnace is not in vanilla Vintage Story. Vanilla smelts iron in the bloomery and makes steel by cementation. Popular mods (e.g. Steelmaking Expanded, Improved Metallurgy) add a blast furnace that smelts iron ore more efficiently and in larger batches, driven by bellows. If you run one of those mods, the rest of this section applies; otherwise the bloomery is your iron furnace.

Where it sits in the pipeline

With a blast-furnace mod the pipeline becomes ore → blast furnace → iron → cementation → steel. Vanilla is ore → bloomery → iron → cementation → steel. Either way the bloomery is fine for a first batch.

  • Bigger batches than the bloomery
  • Bellows-driven heat
  • Feeds the steel pipeline

Bloomery vs blast furnace

Both turn iron ore into iron; the blast furnace just does it at volume. Pick by how much iron you're after:

Choosing your iron smelter

Keep the bloomery — don't replace it

The bloomery never stops being useful. Build a blast furnace in addition when the cementation furnace's appetite for iron ingots outpaces what one-bloom-at-a-time can supply. Exact charge ratios and flux requirements are mod-dependent — read the mod's docs (or the in-game Handbook (H)) before you commit a stockpile of ore.

10 — Endgame

Steel & the Cementation Furnace

Steel is iron with carbon driven into it. Make it in a cementation furnace. The process is carburizing: pack iron ingots into the furnace's "coffin" surrounded by charcoal, then keep it hot for a long time so carbon migrates into the iron.

Process
Carburizing carbon into iron
Carbon source
Charcoal not consumed in the coffin
Full firing
~6.67 days per batch, kept hot
Raw output
Blister steel must be hammered
  1. Build the cementation furnace (a 5×5 footprint, ~6 blocks tall, of refractory/fire brick with a stone coffin) and pack its coffin with iron ingots surrounded by charcoal, up to 16 iron ingots per firing. The charcoal is the carbon source and is not consumed by the carburizing.
  2. Keep it hot. A full firing takes roughly 6.67 in-game days of sustained heat (≈200 pieces of coal), the longest single process in the game. Stockpile fuel before you start.
  3. Collect blister steel. You get 16 blister steel ingots (1:1 from the iron you packed). Like a bloom, they're not yet usable steel.
  4. Hammer the blister steel on an iron anvil (or under a helve hammer) to consolidate it into proper steel ingots.
  5. Smith the steel ingots into the best tools, weapons, and armour in the game.

The prerequisite chain for one batch of steel

Before a single blister-steel ingot exists you need: a pulverizer (to crush the minerals for refractory brick), enough fire clay fired into refractory blocks, a stone coffin, an iron anvil, a forge, 16 iron ingots already smelted and hammered, and a charcoal pile big enough to bury them and ~160 fuel to fire it for nearly a week. Stage every part before you light it — a half-fuelled firing that cools early wastes days.

The full steel setup is an industry

A working steel operation is more than the furnace. You also need a pulverizer, an iron anvil, a forge, and large stockpiles of fuel (charcoal, coke, or coal). The mechanical helpers, the helve hammer for hammering blooms and blister steel and the pulverizer, are covered in Gears & Grindstones. Steel's place in the tech tree is in The Long Road.

The heat ladder, complete

A fire pit melts your first copper at 1084°C. The crucible casts it. The forge and anvil shape bronze and iron. The charcoal pit and bloomery give you iron. After a ~6.67-day cementation firing and a few swings of the helve hammer, you're holding steel. That's the ladder, rock to steel, rung by rung.
