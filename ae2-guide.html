The ME
Network

A working field manual for Applied Energistics 2 — the digital storage and autocrafting mod that replaces a wall of chests with one searchable, wireless network. From growing your first Certus Quartz and grinding out processors, through wiring channels and the ME Controller, into mass storage cells, crafting CPUs, and the import/export buses that automate the whole base. AE2 is not a grid you build once — it is a system you grow.

AE2 15.x (rv-rewrite) MC 1.19.2 · Forge + pack addons ⚠ Channels will humble you

00 — Orientation

Start Here

Applied Energistics 2 is a digital storage and autocrafting network. Instead of sorting items into chests, you pour them into the network as data, then pull them back from a terminal anywhere — and ask the network to craft anything you have a recipe for, on demand, in bulk.

Four ideas hold the whole mod together. Get these straight first and every later block reads cleanly:

power

Energy (AE)

The network runs on stored power measured in AE. You feed it Forge Energy (FE/RF) through an Energy Acceptor; without power, the whole grid goes dark.

wiring

ME Cable

Every device connects to the grid with ME Cable. Cables carry power and the data channels that authorise each device.

limit

Channels

The central constraint. Each device that does work consumes one channel, and cables only carry so many. Channel planning is the AE2 skill.

brain

ME Controller

The multiblock heart. It generates channels for the whole network. Without one you are limited to a tiny ad-hoc grid.

The shape of the mod

You will progress in roughly this order, and this guide follows it: grow Certus Quartz and grind out processors (the universal bottleneck), set up power, lay cable and stand up a Controller, plug in storage and a terminal, then layer on autocrafting and import/export automation. Spatial IO and the pack's addons are the deep end.

The in-game Guidebook is your friend

AE2 ships an excellent illustrated Guidebook (you get one on first pickup of certus quartz, or craft it). It has every recipe and a working diagram for the Controller and crafting multiblocks. Pair it with JEI and you rarely need to leave the game. This page is the map; the Guidebook is the territory.

Don't fear channels — learn them

Most AE2 frustration is one mechanic: channels. Read §03 before you build anything permanent. A network that "randomly stops working" is almost always a channel that ran out, not a bug.

01 — Materials

Certus Quartz & the Processor Chain

Everything in AE2 is built from Certus Quartz, Fluix, and the three families of processor. The first hour is spent setting up renewable crystal growth so the processor grind stops being a chore.

Finding & growing crystals

Certus Quartz Ore generates underground; mining a meteorite is the bigger prize (see below). To make it renewable, you grow crystals from seeds in water:

  1. Charged Certus Quartz — pass plain certus quartz through a Charger (powered by AE/FE). Charged certus is needed for Fluix and for some crafts directly.
  2. Fluix Crystals — drop Charged Certus + Redstone + Quartz Dust into a block of water; they react and form Fluix in-world. Fluix is the mod's signature material (cables, glass, the Controller).
  3. Crystal seeds — craft Certus / Fluix seeds and toss them in water. They slowly grow into full crystals.
  4. Crystal Growth Accelerators — line a water channel with these (they need power) to speed seed growth dramatically. A short accelerated growth lane gives you effectively unlimited certus and fluix.
Quartz Grindstone for early dusts

Before you have power for a Charger, the hand-cranked Quartz Grindstone turns certus quartz, ores and more into dust (including the Quartz Dust you need for Fluix). It is your zero-power bootstrap tool.

Meteorites & the Inscriber presses

Scattered Meteorites bury a core of Sky Stone and, crucially, the Inscriber Presses — the only source of these tools. You need all three press types, plus the Silicon Press, to make processors. Use a Meteorite Compass to find the nearest one.

The Inscriber presses (found in meteorites)

Presses are reusable — don't lose them

A press is a permanent tool that never wears out. You only need one of each. If you can't find meteorites, the AE2 Things addon in this pack adds alternative ways to obtain presses and an Advanced Inscriber — see §09.

Building a processor

The Inscriber stamps printed parts; the processor is then crafted from the printed circuit + printed silicon + a redstone-class material. This is the single biggest early bottleneck in AE2, so automate it (see §06 and §07):

Automate inscribers early

A row of three Inscribers fed by an ME Pattern Provider (each set to a printed part), feeding a fourth assembling Inscriber, is the classic processor factory. Once the network can make its own processors on demand, the whole mod opens up.

02 — Power

Power & Energy (AE)

The network stores energy in its own unit, AE. Every device draws a small idle power even when doing nothing, and channels and active operations cost more. Run out of stored AE and the whole grid shuts down — so power is the first system to make robust.

input

Energy Acceptor

The on-ramp: converts external FE/RF (and other supported energy) into AE for the network. Point your generators at one of these.

buffer

Energy Cell

Stores a buffer of AE so the grid rides out demand spikes and brief power gaps. Place several near the Controller.

buffer+

Dense Energy Cell

A much larger AE buffer (roughly an order of magnitude more than a standard cell). Essential before big autocrafting jobs spike demand.

emergency

Vibration Chamber

Burns furnace fuel to make a trickle of AE directly. A clutch emergency generator to restart a dead network — not a primary source.

How the network spends power

Power draw comes from three places, roughly in increasing order:

  • Idle device drain — every connected device sips a small amount of AE continuously just to stay online. Big networks idle-drain a lot; this is normal.
  • Channels — the Controller and channel usage add to the baseline. More devices using channels = more idle cost.
  • Active operations — autocrafting (Molecular Assemblers, Crafting CPUs) and import/export buses moving items cost AE per action. A large craft can briefly draw far more than idle.
Size for the spike, not the average

A network can idle happily on a tiny generator, then brown-out the instant you queue a 10,000-item craft. Keep at least one Dense Energy Cell as a shock absorber, and make sure your FE input can sustain a busy crafting job — not just the idle baseline. numbers approximate / config-tunable

03 — The constraint

Cables, the Controller & Channels

This is the section that makes or breaks an AE2 player. Every device that does meaningful work needs a channel, channels are a finite resource carried by cable, and the ME Controller is the only thing that generates them in bulk. Learn this and you will never "randomly lose" a device again.

The channel rule

A channel is the network's authorisation token for an active device. Storage drives, terminals, import/export buses, interfaces, pattern providers, planes — each consumes a channel. Passive parts (the Controller itself, plain cable, Quartz Fiber) do not.

Cable types and channel capacity
Cable / sourceChannels carriedNotes
Glass / Covered / Smart Cable8Standard cable. Smart cable visually shows channel load — invaluable for debugging.
Dense Cable (Smart / Covered)32The backbone. Carries a full Controller's worth of channels along a trunk line.
ME Controller32 / faceEach exposed face of the Controller multiblock can emit up to 32 channels.
Ad-hoc (no Controller)8 totalA controllerless network is hard-capped at 8 channel-using devices, full stop.

The ME Controller multiblock

The Controller is a block of Controller cores you place together into a compact 3D structure. It has rules: it can be a cuboid up to a limited size, faces must stay valid, and an over-built or badly shaped Controller will visibly turn off (turning red) rather than work. Channels flow out of its faces onto your cable. The closer a device is (in cable hops) to the Controller, the easier it is to route a channel to it.

Smart cable is X-ray for channels

Use Smart Cable on your trunk lines while building. It animates coloured dots showing exactly how many channels are flowing through each segment. The moment a segment hits 8 (or 32 for dense), you can see the bottleneck instead of guessing.

Multiplexing with P2P Tunnels

The single most powerful channel tool is the P2P Tunnel (ME variant). It lets you stuff up to 32 channels through a single cable: one input P2P "swallows" up to 32 channels, runs them down one dense line, and a paired output P2P "spits" them back out at the far end. This is how you wire a large base without a forest of dense cable everywhere.

  • Memory Card — shift-click an input P2P to copy its frequency, then right-click outputs to bind them. This pairs tunnels.
  • P2P also tunnels other things — FE/RF, items, fluids, light, redstone — not just ME channels. It is a general-purpose multiplexer.
  • Quartz Fiber — a special connector that shares power between two networks without sharing channels or merging them. Used to power a subnet from a main grid.
Subnets dodge the channel tax

A device on its own small subnet (a separate network joined only by a Storage Bus or Quartz Fiber) doesn't spend a channel on your main grid. Heavy automation builders run many tiny subnets to keep the main network's channel count low. More on this in §10.

04 — Storage

Storage, Cells & the Byte Budget

The reason AE2 exists: dissolve your chests into a searchable digital pool. Storage lives in Storage Cells slotted into an ME Drive (or ME Chest), and understanding the types vs bytes cost is the key to not wasting capacity.

Drives and cells

An ME Drive holds up to 10 storage cells and exposes them all to the network (it uses one channel for the whole drive). An ME Chest holds a single cell but gives you a built-in terminal view of it — handy for early game.

Item storage cells (relative scale)
CellBytesItem typesUse
1k1,02463Early game; cheap.
4k4,09663General storage.
16k16,38463Bulk single-item stores.
64k65,53663The vanilla ceiling per cell.

There are also fluid cells (same tiers, for fluids) and, via addons, far larger and specialised cells (see §09).

Types vs bytes — the cost model

Each cell has two limits: a total byte capacity and a fixed number of distinct item types (typically 63). The byte math is the part that trips people up:

  • Storing a new item type reserves a chunk of bytes up front (a fixed overhead per type).
  • Beyond that overhead, items pack in tightly — one byte holds many items of an already-present type.
  • So a cell crammed with 63 different items wastes capacity on overhead, while a cell holding one item in bulk uses its bytes efficiently.
Partition with a Cell Workbench

Use the Cell Workbench to partition a cell — lock it to specific item types. A partitioned cell only accepts those items, which (a) reserves that cell for a known purpose, and (b) lets you dedicate a big cell to a single bulk resource without it filling up with junk. Pair with priority (below) for clean routing.

Storage Bus & priority

  • Storage Bus — attach to any external inventory (a chest, a barrel, a machine, a whole other mod's storage) to expose it as if it were network storage. The network can read and write it directly. Great for bulk barrels and drawer mods.
  • Priority — every storage destination (cells and storage buses) has a priority. The network writes to the highest priority that will accept the item first. Set bulk-resource destinations high so cobblestone goes to its barrel, not your general cells.
  • Partition + priority together is how you build a network that auto-sorts itself: void/overflow cells at low priority, bulk barrels at high priority, general cells in the middle.

05 — Access

Access & Terminals

A terminal is your window into the network: search, withdraw, insert, and craft. AE2 offers several flavours for different jobs, plus wireless access so you never have to walk back to a wall.

Terminal types
TerminalAddsBest for
ME TerminalSearch / store / withdrawThe basic view of the whole pool.
Crafting TerminalA built-in 3×3 crafting gridHand-crafting straight from the network.
Pattern Access TerminalRemote view of every Pattern Provider's patternsEditing/organising patterns from one place.
Wireless TerminalNetwork access from your inventoryRoaming the base without a wall terminal.

Going wireless

A Wireless Terminal connects to the network through one or more ME Wireless Access Points. Each access point has a range; you boost that range by slotting Wireless Booster cards into the access point. Cover a large base by placing several access points. The terminal itself draws AE and must be charged (in a Charger or the network).

Search syntax saves your sanity

The terminal search bar is powerful: prefix @ to search by mod name, # to search by tooltip/tag, and * as a wildcard. So @mekanism ingot finds Mekanism ingots only. Some packs also let you search by item ID. Learn these and a 5,000-item network feels small.

Wireless range, solved

This pack ships AEInfinityBooster (see §09), which provides an unlimited-range wireless booster — bind it once and your wireless terminal works anywhere, any dimension. If you have it, you can skip the access-point sprawl entirely.

06 — The payoff

Autocrafting

This is what people stay in AE2 for: tell the network "make me 64 of X" and it figures out the whole recipe tree, gathers ingredients, runs the crafts, and delivers. It is built from encoded patterns, a Crafting CPU to plan and track the job, and the machines/assemblers that do the work.

Patterns: the recipes the network knows

You encode a pattern in a Pattern Terminal (or Pattern Encoding Terminal). There are two kinds, and the difference matters:

crafting

Crafting Pattern

A 3×3 recipe the network can make in a Molecular Assembler — anything that fits a crafting grid. The assembler auto-builds it on demand.

processing

Processing Pattern

"Put these items in, get these items out" for an external machine (a furnace, a Mekanism machine, an Inscriber). The network pushes inputs and waits for outputs.

Where patterns live: Pattern Providers

Encoded patterns go into a Pattern Provider (the rewrite-era replacement/companion to the old Interface for crafting). The provider pushes ingredients to whatever it's attached to:

  • Stick a Pattern Provider against a Molecular Assembler for crafting-grid recipes (a "1-up" Assembler with a provider face is the classic auto-craft block).
  • Stick one against a machine (or a chest feeding it) for processing patterns — it pushes the inputs in and the machine's output returns to the network.
  • An ME Interface can also hold patterns and import results; in the rewrite the Pattern Provider handles the pushing and the Interface handles import/stocking.

The Crafting CPU multiblock

The brain of a craft is the Crafting CPU, assembled from a hollow shell of these blocks:

Crafting CPU components
BlockRole
Crafting Storage (1k–64k)Holds the in-progress items for the job. More/bigger storage = the CPU can handle bigger, deeper craft trees.
Co-Processing UnitLets the CPU run multiple craft steps in parallel. More co-processors = faster jobs.
Crafting MonitorShows what the CPU is currently making (text on the block face). Optional but handy.
Crafting UnitFiller/structure block; the base of the multiblock.

A network can hold several CPUs; each handles one crafting job at a time. Build a few small CPUs plus one big one so several requests can run at once and a giant order doesn't block a tiny one.

Sub-crafting just works

The CPU recursively crafts missing intermediates. Ask for a block that needs processors that need printed circuits that need silicon, and as long as patterns exist for each step and raw materials are in the network, it builds the whole chain in order. This is why automating your processor line (§01) pays off so hard.

Stock-keeping needs a Level/Crafting Card or the Requester

By default autocrafting is on-demand. To keep a buffer of an item permanently in stock, use an Interface set to stock a level, an Export Bus + Crafting Card, or — far more flexibly — the ME Requester addon in this pack (§09), which maintains target stock levels of many items at once.

07 — Moving items

Automation & IO

Buses and planes connect the digital network to the physical world: pulling outputs out of machines, pushing inputs in, and even vacuuming items and blocks straight from the world. Each bus uses a channel and accepts upgrade cards to tune its behaviour.

in

Import Bus

Pulls items from an attached inventory into the network. Put one on a furnace output, a farm chest, anything.

out

Export Bus

Pushes filtered items from the network into an attached inventory or machine. The workhorse of feeding processes.

interface

ME Interface / Pattern Provider

The flexible IO/stocking block: import results, stock items, and (provider) push craft ingredients.

world

Annihilation / Formation Plane

Annihilation: breaks blocks/picks up items in front of it into the network. Formation: places blocks/drops items from the network into the world.

Upgrade cards

Slot these into a bus's upgrade slots to change how it works:

Common bus upgrade cards
CardEffect
Capacity CardAdds filter slots — lets a bus handle more item types at once.
Acceleration CardMoves more items per operation (faster throughput). Stackable.
Redstone CardGates the bus on a redstone signal (active high/low, or pulse).
Fuzzy CardMatches by damage/NBT tolerance — useful for tools or near-identical items.
Crafting CardOn an Export Bus, lets it request a craft if the item isn't in stock — the basis of keep-in-stock automation.
Keep-in-stock recipe

Export Bus + Crafting Card + Acceleration Cards, pointed at a chest in front of a machine, will keep that machine fed and auto-craft the input if it runs out. For per-item target levels across the whole base, the ME Requester addon is cleaner (§09).

08 — The deep end

Spatial IO & Matter Tools

The exotic corner of AE2: storing entire regions of the world as data, and a set of charged tools and toys that fall out of the same fluix tech.

Spatial IO

Spatial IO lets you capture a whole 3D region — blocks, machines and all — into a Spatial Storage Cell, then paste it back elsewhere. The capture is performed by a Spatial IO Port sitting inside a frame of Spatial Pylons: you build pylon lines along the three axes to define the bounding box, power it heavily, and the port swaps the contained region into/out of a pocket dimension stored on the cell.

  • Cell tiers (2³, 16³, 128³) cap the region size you can store.
  • The operation costs a large burst of AE — keep Dense Energy Cells nearby.
  • Classic uses: a portable, relocatable base; or a self-contained "machine in a box" you can move whole.

Charged & matter gadgets

tool

Entropy Manipulator

A charged tool that heats or cools blocks — smelt-on-break, instant-mine certain blocks, melt snow/ice, light fires.

weapon

Matter Cannon

Fires loaded ammo (metal nuggets, or Paint Balls) at high speed. Denser ammo hits harder; Paint Balls colour blocks and cables.

tool

Color Applicator

Recolours cables and other blocks straight from network-stored dyes/paint balls. Essential for tidy channel-colour routing.

tool

Charged Staff

A melee charged tool that deals bonus damage and can break certain blocks quickly. Runs on AE charge.

mobile

Portable Cell

A handheld storage cell with its own terminal view — a pocket ME store you can carry and access anywhere, no network needed.

Paint your channels

Cables come in 16 colours. A coloured cable only connects to same-colour (or "fluix"/uncoloured) cable, which lets you run independent channel lines through the same physical space without them merging. The Color Applicator recolours runs in seconds.

10 — Field notes

Tips & Gotchas

Hard-won habits that separate a network that "keeps breaking" from one that quietly runs your whole base.

Channel budgeting

  • Plan trunk lines with dense cable + P2P. Run one dense backbone, then P2P-tunnel 32 channels per drop to where you need them. Don't daisy-chain glass cable across the base and wonder why drive #9 is offline.
  • Use Smart Cable while building. The animated channel dots tell you exactly which segment is saturated.
  • Subnet heavy automation. A bank of import/export buses on its own tiny network (joined to the main grid by a single Storage Bus or Quartz Fiber) costs the main network only the channels you choose to expose — not one per bus.

Power & storage

  • Keep a Dense Energy Cell as a buffer. Crafting jobs spike demand; idle figures lie to you.
  • Partition cells with the Cell Workbench so bulk resources don't eat your general-purpose storage, and so a cell's 63 type-slots aren't wasted.
  • Priority routes items. Set bulk barrels (via Storage Bus) high, void/overflow cells low, general cells in between, and the network self-sorts.
  • One item per big cell for bulk — a 64k cell holding a single resource is far more byte-efficient than one juggling 63 types.

Autocrafting

  • Multiple CPUs, mixed sizes. A small order shouldn't wait behind a 10k craft — give it its own small CPU.
  • Automate processors first. Encode patterns for every printed part and processor; sub-crafting then makes the rest of the mod effortless.
  • Add Co-Processing Units to make deep craft trees finish in parallel rather than one step at a time.
The classic failure mode

"My terminal went blank / my drives disappeared." Nine times out of ten this is a channel running out (a cable segment hit its limit) or the network running out of power (a generator stalled and the Energy Cells drained). Check Smart Cable channel load and your AE buffer first — it is almost never a bug.

When in doubt, ask the Guidebook

Every block in AE2 has a Guidebook entry with a diagram and recipe. The Controller and Crafting CPU pages in particular show valid shapes far better than any text can. The in-game book + JEI are the ground truth; this guide is the orientation.
