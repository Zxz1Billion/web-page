The Homestead

Four dirt walls kept you alive on Night One. Now turn that box into a home that keeps food from rotting, a workshop that smiths metal, and a base that shrugs off a temporal storm. This guide covers the materials, a real starter house, the exact mechanics of a high-scoring food cellar, and the kiln, charcoal pit and smithy that power the rest of your tech tree. Each comes as an interactive, layer-by-layer plan.

00 — Materials

Building Basics

Every structure on this page uses a handful of building blocks. Pick by what you can reach: cheap cob for the first real walls, fired brick and cut stone for permanence, planks for floors and furniture, and the chisel for detail.

cheapest

Cob

Mix soil and dry grass by hand to get cob, the cheapest sturdy wall in the game. Use it for the first proper house and for sealing a cellar early. It also insulates reasonably for winter warmth.

fired

Clay bricks

Form clay into bricks, dry them, then fire them (pit kiln, later an oven). Fired brick is solid and durable, the backbone of a permanent home and a tidy cellar.

stone

Stone bricks

Knock stone into cobblestone, then craft stone bricks for the most durable, fireproof walls. Use them around a forge or a deep, cool cellar.

wood

Logs & planks

Fell trees for logs, then cut them into planks with a saw (a tool you craft). Planks become floors, doors, ladders, shelves and most furniture.

Chiseling for detail

A chisel (plus a hammer in hand) carves any placed block into a custom voxel shape: slopes, arches, beams, trims, slabs. This is how Vintage Story builds look hand-made rather than blocky. Chiseling is purely cosmetic for the builds here, but it turns a plain cob box into something you want to live in.

Doors, windows & weatherproofing

  • Doors (planks) seal a doorway against rain and let you in and out without leaving a gap drifters can use. A room needs a real door to count as an enclosed cellar.
  • Windows. Smelt quartz chunks into glass (in a bloomery — not from sand), then craft window panes for light. Glass lets in skylight, which hurts a cellar's score. Keep glass in the house, not the larder.
  • Walls & roofs keep out rain (which sputters your fire and chills you) and keep drifters from wandering or spawning inside. Aim for a solid, gap-free shell everywhere on this page.
Insulation matters in winter

As seasons turn cold, body temperature becomes a real threat. A fully enclosed room with a heat source (fire pit, forge, oven) stays warm, and thick walls like cob and stone hold that warmth. The same enclosure that makes a good cellar in summer makes a survivable bedroom in winter.

01 — From box to home

The Starter House

Once you have cob or planks, replace the dirt hole with a real house: a footprint big enough for a hearth, a bed and storage, with a door and one window for light. The plan below is a cosy 7×7. Step through floor → walls → fittings → roof.

  1. Lay the floor. Level a 7×7 footprint and floor it (planks or stone brick). A clean floor stops things spawning or burrowing under you.
  2. Raise the walls. Ring the footprint two-to-three blocks high in cob or brick. Leave a 1-wide doorway on one side and a 1-block window gap on another for daylight.
  3. Fit it out. Put an indoor fire pit/hearth against a wall for warmth and cooking, a bed in a corner (it sets your spawn point), and a chest or two for storage.
  4. Glaze & door it. Drop a glass pane in the window gap and hang a plank door in the doorway. Now you're sealed against rain and drifters but still lit.
  5. Roof it. Cap the top completely. Chisel the edges into a pitched roof later for the look. Flat is fine to start.
Keep the hearth away from the bed

Fire spreads to flammable blocks. Set the fire pit on a stone or brick tile and keep planks and thatch a block clear of it. A small stone hearth corner inside an otherwise wooden house is the safe pattern.

02 — Where everything goes

Storage

You'll outgrow your inventory fast. Vintage Story has distinct containers, and the right one matters for food: some slow spoilage, some don't. Match the container to the job.

Containers and their best use (v1.22.3)

Food rule of thumb

Loose food in a chest spoils at the normal rate. Move it into storage vessels or sealed crocks, and put those inside a cellar. The benefits stack multiplicatively. That's the job of the next section.

The numbers that matter

Containers are not interchangeable for food. The storage vessel and the sealed crock each carry a spoilage multiplier that stacks on top of the cellar's, and they hold different amounts. Build to these:

Seal a crock by crafting it together with a piece of fat or beeswax. A sealed crock makes a cooked meal perish ten times slower on its own; drop that crock into a good cellar and the two multipliers compound. A storage vessel turns grain into a near-archival staple: vessel ×0.50 times a strong cellar's ×0.257 keeps grains good for over a decade of in-game time, and vegetables for well over a year.

03 — The star of the homestead

The Food Cellar

Food rots in real time. A well-built cellar slows that rot by giving the game an enclosed, dark, cool room to recognize. Build one early. It's a major quality-of-life upgrade after fire and walls.

This plan is a compact, fully enclosed 5×5 cob or stone room: solid roof, no skylight, one door, walls lined with storage vessels and sealed crocks. Step through it.

First, the room must seal

A cellar is just a room the game scored as cool and dark. Before anything is scored, the space has to register as a room at all. Two hard requirements:

  • Interior 14×14×14 or smaller. The room detector only encloses spaces up to 14 blocks in each interior dimension. A hall bigger than that never becomes a room, so it can never become a cellar. Keep larders small.
  • Every side solid, no gaps. All six faces must be sealed. Counts as solid: full blocks, leaded glass panes, a non-crude/non-ruined door, and a solid (not grated) trapdoor.
What breaks the seal

The usual reasons a "cellar" silently does nothing: a crude or ruined door, a grated trapdoor, or any fence / fence gate in a wall. None of these seal a room. Replace them with a proper door or a solid block. One gap and the whole room fails to register.

How the cellar score works (be precise)

Once the room seals, it scores how cellar-like it is. The room starts at 100% effectiveness and loses points two ways, then is clamped to a 10% floor at worst:

  • Skylight score — up to −40%. Counts the share of interior blocks getting as much sunlight as the open air, over the total interior volume. Any glass, open hole or non-solid roof raises it. A solid, opaque roof with zero sky exposure keeps this penalty at 0.
  • Cooling score — up to −50%. Counts how many of the enclosing blocks are cooling-class — stone, soil, ceramic or ore — versus non-cooling (wood, etc.). More cooling blocks, smaller penalty. Notably, packed soil and stone count equally, so a dirt hole cools exactly as well as a stone-brick vault.

The temperature & perish formulas

The score does not store food directly; it sets the room's temperature, and temperature sets the perish rate. When the outside is warmer than 5°C:

cellar_temp = 5 + ( (outside_temp − 5) × (1 − cellar_score) ) e.g. an 80% cellar in 30°C weather: 5 + ( (30 − 5) × (1 − 0.80) ) = 10°C perish_rate = max( 0.1 , min( 2.4 , 3^(temp/19 − 1.2) ) − 0.1 ) best possible cellar multiplier ≈ 0.257× (about a quarter of open-air rot)

A perfect cellar (fully roofed, no skylight, all cooling blocks) pulls the room to the 5°C floor in warm weather and keeps close to the full 100%. A leaky, sun-lit, wood-lined room drops toward the 10% floor and barely beats open air. Because the cellar caps at 5°C, it only helps when the outside is warmer than 5°C. In deep winter the open air is already colder than that, so the cellar earns its keep most in spring, summer and autumn. Drive the ambient to 0°C or below (cold biome or winter) and the perish rate bottoms out at its 0.1 floor — that is the "ice cellar" effect, no special blocks required.

Vessels multiply the benefit

The room score is only half of it. Putting food inside storage vessels and sealed crocks applies its own spoilage multiplier on top of the cellar's. A cold, dark cellar lined with sealed crocks beats the same cellar with food sitting loose on shelves.

High-scoring cellar checklist

✓ Keep it small — a 3×3 to 5×5 room scores and cools better than a hall.
✓ Fully enclose it: solid walls, solid floor, solid opaque roof, no skylight (and no glass/window).
✓ Exactly one door for access — a real plank/solid door, kept shut.
✓ Build it underground or deeply shaded, away from the forge, oven and direct sun, so it stays cool.
✓ Line the walls with storage vessels and sealed crocks, and keep your food inside them.

Ground cellar vs above-ground

The single most effective cellar is the cheapest: a hole in the ground capped with soil. Every surrounding block is cooling-class (max cooling score) and there is zero sky exposure (no skylight penalty), so a buried room scores near 100% with no special materials. An above-ground cellar can match it, but only if you build the whole shell from cooling-class blocks (stone, soil, ceramic, ore), cap it with a solid opaque roof, and avoid every window. Add wood or glass and the score drops.

Dirt is as cool as stone

Soil, packed dirt and stone all sit in the cooling class and contribute equally to the cooling score. Upgrading a dirt cellar to stone brick buys you durability and looks, not a colder room. Spend the stone on the smithy; let the larder be a dirt hole if you like.

Root / stone cellar

The standard build above: a cool, dark, enclosed cob, soil or stone room. Holds down to 5°C in warm weather and gives a reliable spoilage cut for fruit, veg, grain and prepared meals year-round.

Ice cellar (no special blocks)

There is no ice-lined container in vanilla. The "ice cellar" is simply a sealed cooling room where the ambient is already at or below 0°C — dug into cold ground or used through winter in a cold biome. At freezing the perish rate bottoms out at its 0.1 floor: the best preservation in the game, for free.

04 — Make it last

Food Preservation

The cellar slows spoilage. Preservation changes the food so it keeps far longer to begin with. Pair the two. The full nutrition and cooking system lives in The Larder. This section covers the structural side.

Salting / curing (barrel)

Cure meat by sealing it in a barrel with salt at the fixed ratio of 1 meat to 2 salt (so 32 meat needs 64 salt). The barrel must stay sealed for 20 days (480 hours); it then yields cured meat, the longest-lasting meat in the game — good for up to roughly 3.4 years. Cured meat gives about 75% of the satiety of plain cooked meat when eaten straight, the price you pay for the shelf life. Salt comes from saltpeter deposits or by evaporating saltwater (boil it down).

Pickling (barrel)

Brine preserves vegetables and legumes. Mix water + salt in a barrel to make brine, then add vegetables — each vegetable consumes 1 unit of brine. Seal the barrel for 336 hours (14 days). Pickled veg keeps especially well inside a sealed crock (×0.25) in a cellar.

Drying racks

Hang bushmeat, poultry or redmeat on a meat/drying rack to dry-age it for 2 days. Raw meat is only fresh for about 1.5 days, so build the rack inside or beside a cellar — otherwise the meat rots before the age completes. Cheap, early, and good for surplus.

Cheese (the slow one)

Milk becomes cheese through a barrel chain, then must ripen. Ripening only happens in a spot whose perish multiplier is 0.5× or lower — the block-info HUD labels it "Suitable spot for food ripening." A cellar that holds the room at or below 5°C qualifies. The longest-keeping dairy is waxed cheddar.

Crocks & vessels in the cold

Sealed crocks (×0.10 meals) and storage vessels (×0.50 grains) in a cold cellar give the longest shelf life. Preservation method, container multiplier and cellar score all multiply together.

The layers multiply. A cooked meal in a sealed crock (×0.10) inside a strong cellar (×0.257) decays at roughly ×0.026 — about forty times slower than the same meal sitting loose in the open. 05 — The workshop

The Smithy

Metalworking needs a dedicated, fireproof space. Lay it out so heat, anvil and quench form a tight working triangle: pull hot metal from the forge, hammer it on the anvil, quench it in the barrel, all within a step. The 6×6 floor plan below shows it.

  • Forge. Heats metal to working temperature. Keep it on stone brick. Flames spread to planks.
  • Anvil. Where you smith hot ingots into tool heads and parts. Place it within a step of the forge.
  • Quench barrel (1.22). A water barrel for quenching and tempering finished pieces, right beside the anvil.
  • Tool & ingot rack. Keep hammers, tongs, chisels and ingots within reach.
  • Ore & fuel bins. Store charcoal, coal and ore by the forge so you never walk for fuel.
Keep the bloomery & charcoal pit close

Smelting bog iron in a bloomery eats charcoal, so site your charcoal pit and bloomery just outside the smithy door. The full smithing and casting workflow is in The Forge & The Bloom.

06 — Fire & fuel

Kiln & Charcoal Pit

Two ground-built "structures" power your early tech. The pit kiln fires your pottery. The charcoal pit turns logs into the high-heat fuel your forge and bloomery need.

The pit kiln

To fire clay pots, bowls, crucibles and bricks: place the dry clay items on the ground, pack dry grass and sticks around and over them, add a layer of fuel (firewood, peat or coal), and light it. It smoulders for roughly 10–20 in-game hours depending on fuel, and leaves your fired pottery behind. Build it on bare dirt, away from anything flammable.

  • Fuel: 4–8 pieces. Most pottery needs 4 fuel; a storage vessel needs 8 on top. The fuel and time are the same whether you fire one item or four — so always fill the kiln.
  • Clay costs, exact: a bowl is 1 clay, a crock 2, a planter 18, a storage vessel 35. Shingles for clay roofing fire the same way.
  • Bulk firing: a beehive kiln (a multiblock you build later) fires many items at once and unlocks up to four ceramic colours per clay.

The charcoal pit

Charcoal comes from smouldering wood in a low-oxygen mound. Stack firewood logs, cover the whole pile completely with soil, then ignite it. Starved of air, the wood chars into charcoal instead of burning to ash. The plan below shows the three stages: ground, log core, soil cap.

Cover it completely

Any gap in the soil cap lets in air and the wood burns away. Seal every side and the top before lighting. The pottery and casting workflows that consume this output are in The Four Crafts and The Forge & The Bloom.

07 — Cold-climate farming

Greenhouse

Crops stop growing when it's too cold. A greenhouse is a glass-roofed, glass-walled structure over tilled farmland. It lets sunlight in while sheltering the soil, extending the growing season into the cold months and letting you farm in cold biomes. The plan below is a small glass box over a tilled bed.

  1. Till the bed. Hoe a patch of soil into farmland and plant your crops.
  2. Wall it in glass. Ring the bed with glass blocks/panes so light reaches the crops from the sides.
  3. Glaze the roof. Cap it with glass too. Light from above keeps growth ticking even when it's cold outside.
  4. Leave a door. One glass-flanked doorway to tend the crops.
Light + shelter = growth

The greenhouse flips the cellar's logic: here you want all the daylight you can get. The full farming, soil-fertility and climate detail lives in The Larder and The Living World.

08 — When the rust comes

Storm-Proofing

During a temporal storm, drifters spawn everywhere, even in daylight and on the surface. Use your homestead as shelter. A sealed, lit, fully walled base with controllable chokepoints lets you wait the storm out instead of fighting in the open.

  • Seal it. No gaps in walls, floor or roof. The same gap-free shell the cellar wants keeps drifters from wandering in.
  • Light it. Keep the interior bright (lanterns, fire) so nothing spawns inside with you.
  • Chokepoints. One door you control beats four open archways. Fight from a doorway, not the middle of a room.
Know the schedule

Storms are periodic and escalate over the game. The full temporal-stability system (warning signs, gear, and the rewards for surviving) is covered in The Rust & The Gear. Build the walls before you need them.
