Ruins & Wayfarers

Once your base is steady, the world starts pulling you outward. It's dotted with crumbling ruins, criss-crossed by a network of broken translocators, walked by wandering trader caravans, and — new in 1.22 — pierced by a fully dynamic dungeon. This is where the rare loot lives: gears, tools, hard-to-find seeds, and the slow-burning threads of the temporal mystery. Here's what's out there and how to come home with it.

00 — The lay of the land

Overview

Vintage Story's wilderness isn't empty scenery. Scattered across it are four kinds of place worth travelling to — and almost everything rare or strange you'll ever own comes from one of them.

structures

Ruins

Crumbling stone and cobble structures, on the surface and buried underground. Inside: loot containers, cracked clay vessels, gears, tools and rare seeds — and, deeper down, drifters.

travel

Translocators

Broken teleporter devices found in ruins. Repaired and powered, the static ones link a fixed pair of distant points — your main tool for crossing the map and reaching story-gated areas.

people

Traders

Wandering caravans and tents that spawn on the surface. Each specialises — seeds, tools, furniture, treasure — and they buy and sell for gears and coins.

new in 1.22

The dynamic dungeon

A procedurally assembled, multi-room dungeon that now generates in survival worlds — a fresh, varied exploration target with its own loot and threats.

Why bother leaving home?

Because some things simply aren't craftable. Temporal gears, certain seeds (cabbage is the classic example), translocator access to far regions, and the story itself all live out in the world. Exploring is the mid-game.

01 — Loot in the rubble

Ruins, Surface & Deep

Ruins are crumbling stone, cobble and brick structures left by people who came before. Some sit out in the open — a few broken walls, a collapsed tower, an overgrown foundation. Others are buried, reached only by digging into a hillside or finding an entrance underground. Both reward a careful sweep.

Crack every clay vessel

The single most valuable thing in most ruins is the humble cracked clay vessel — a sealed storage pot. Break it (it won't open like a normal container) and it spills its contents: tools, the occasional gear, and crucially rare seeds. Some crops are almost impossible to get any other way — cabbage seeds, for instance, come essentially only from cracked vessels and from traders. Smash them all; the loot is randomised per vessel.

The Malefactor's home turf

If you rolled the Malefactor class, ruins are your specialty — they're stealthier and better at finding and looting these structures. Any class can raid ruins, but a Malefactor squeezes more out of them.

Ruin types and what they typically hold
Ruin typeTypical loot
Surface ruins (walls, towers, foundations)Cracked clay vessels (seeds, tools, gears), loose loot, building blocks to salvage

Deeper means richer — and more dangerous

Underground ruins hide the best caches, but they also sit where temporal stability is lower and drifters roam. Bring light and a weapon and don't get cornered — the bestiary and how to fight them is in The Bestiary. The rare seeds you pull out of vessels feed straight into your farm — see The Larder.

02 — Crossing the map

Translocators

Sooner or later you'll find a translocator in a ruin — a tall, broken teleporter frame, dark and inert. Many are static translocators: each one is permanently wired to a partner translocator somewhere far away on the map. Repair and power a translocator, step through, and you're instantly carried to wherever its partner stands. This is the game's primary long-distance travel system, and the way you reach far or otherwise-locked regions tied to the story.

Bringing one back to life

  1. Find one and read it. A broken translocator usually needs temporal gears to repair and energise. Gears are the universal "this is temporal tech" currency — pulled from ruins, traded, or found in the world. The gear system is covered in The Rust & The Gear.
  2. Power it and step through. Once active it sends you to its fixed partner. The destination is set by the world, not chosen — so the first trip is a leap into the unknown.
  3. Note where you land. Open the map (M) and mark the exit point the moment you arrive, so you can find this translocator again from the far side.
  4. Build a safe outpost at both ends. A walled, lit little base bracketing each translocator turns a one-way gamble into a reliable two-way route — and a refuge if you arrive somewhere hostile.
Think of them as fixed train lines

You don't aim a translocator — each is a pre-laid line between two stations. Chaining them (one ruin's translocator leads to a region with another, and so on) is how you stitch together a fast-travel network across an enormous world, and how the story quietly pulls you toward the places it wants you to see.

03 — Coins & gears

Wandering Traders

Out on the surface you'll come across trader caravans and tents that spawn in the world. Each trader specialises, and you deal with them using gears (rusty and temporal) and coins as currency. They have limited stock that restocks over time, and prices drift with supply and demand — buy out something rare and the price for the rest climbs.

Best uses of a trader visit

Buy what you can't make: rare seeds (cabbage from an Agriculture trader), good tools, and especially temporal gears for translocators and temporal tech. Sell your surplus — extra ore, food, crafted goods — to fund it. Because stock is limited and prices shift, spread big purchases across several traders and several visits.

Traders don't wait forever

A wandering trader you find today may have wandered on tomorrow. If you spot one with stock you want, note its location on your map (M) and deal while it's there — or come back once it has had time to restock.

04 — New in 1.22

The Dynamic Dungeon

Version 1.22 added a brand-new exploration target: a fully dynamic dungeon that now generates in survival worlds. Unlike the older ruins — which are pre-built structures dropped into the terrain — this dungeon is procedurally assembled from many parts into a multi-room layout, so it's different every time and far larger and more varied than a single static ruin.

What's new about it

A varied, procedural layout rather than a fixed floor plan — corridors, rooms and connections assembled on generation. That makes it a fresh, replayable place to explore even once you've cleared every ruin in your region.

What's inside

Its own loot and its own threats, spread through the rooms. Treat it as a serious dungeon delve, not a quick ruin sweep — expect to fight your way deeper for the better rewards.

Don't wander in light-handed

Go in with armour, plenty of light (torches and a held lantern), and food for the trip. Place torches as you go so you can find your way out, and watch your stability deep inside. The creatures you'll meet are catalogued in The Bestiary.

05 — The thread in the world

Story Locations

Vintage Story has a quiet, authored story woven through its world, tied to the temporal mystery — why reality frays, where the gears and drifters come from, who built the translocators. You don't get it handed to you in cutscenes; you uncover it by exploring and paying attention. Here's how to follow the thread, kept deliberately spoiler-light.

  • Read your Diary (J). Your character keeps a personal diary that fills in with notes and entries as you progress. It's the main place the story speaks to you directly — check it whenever something significant happens.
  • Follow the translocators. Translocator chains don't just move you around; they steer you toward the regions and sites the story cares about. Repairing and stepping through them is how the world opens up its more remote, authored places.
  • Collect clues from special ruins. Certain authored sites — the kind of archive, lab and devastated locations the lore hints at — hold story items and context, not just loot. Read what you find; the pieces add up.
Let it unfold at its own pace

There's no penalty for taking the story slowly. Keep building, keep exploring, read the diary, and the larger picture — the gears, the rust, the storms — assembles itself. The mechanics behind the temporal side of it all are in The Rust & The Gear.

★ — Pack the bag

An Explorer's Kit

A ruin two hundred metres away is a quick raid; a translocator hop or a dungeon delve can strand you somewhere unfamiliar with night coming. Before any long expedition, pack like you might not get straight home.

  1. Torches & light. A stack of torches plus a held lantern. Dark is where drifters spawn — in ruins, dungeons and underground, light is survival, and a trail of torches is your way back out.
  2. Food + a bedroll. Enough varied food to keep your nutrition bars up, and a bedroll so you can set a new respawn point and sleep through dangerous nights far from base.
  3. Armour & a weapon. Wear the best armour you can, and carry a spear or bow for the drifters and predators you'll meet in deeper ruins and the dynamic dungeon.
  4. A prospecting pick. While you're out, sample the ground — exploration trips double as ore-scouting, and a prospecting pick tells you what's worth coming back to mine.
  5. Building blocks. A stack of cobble or dirt for emergency walls — to seal yourself in for the night, wall off a drifter, or bracket a translocator with a quick outpost.
  6. A few temporal gears. For temporal stability in low-stability depths and for repairing translocators you find in the field — don't get stranded one gear short of activating your ride home.
Build before you delve

For the outpost and shelter blocks worth carrying, see Walls & Roofs; for reading the terrain and what each region holds, The World; and for everything gear-and-stability related, The Rust & The Gear.
