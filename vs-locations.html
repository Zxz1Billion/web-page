Ruins & Wayfarers

Once your base is steady, head outward. The world is dotted with crumbling ruins, criss-crossed by broken translocators, walked by wandering trader caravans, and, new in 1.22, pierced by a fully dynamic dungeon. The rare loot lives out here: gears, tools, hard-to-find seeds, and the threads of the temporal mystery. Here's what's out there and how to come home with it.

00 — The lay of the land

Overview

Vintage Story's wilderness isn't empty. It holds four kinds of place worth travelling to, and almost everything rare you'll own comes from one of them.

structures

Ruins

Crumbling stone and cobble structures, on the surface and buried underground. Inside: loot containers, cracked clay vessels, gears, tools and rare seeds. Deeper down, drifters.

travel

Translocators

Broken teleporter devices found in ruins. Repaired and powered, the static ones link a fixed pair of distant points. Your main tool for crossing the map and reaching story-gated areas.

people

Traders

Wandering caravans and tents that spawn on the surface. Each specialises (seeds, tools, furniture, treasure) and buys and sells for gears and coins.

new in 1.22

The dynamic dungeon

A procedurally assembled, multi-room dungeon that now generates in survival worlds. A fresh, varied target with its own loot and threats.

Why bother leaving home?

Some things aren't craftable. Temporal gears, certain seeds (cabbage is the classic example), translocator access to far regions, and the story itself all live out in the world. Exploring is the mid-game.

01 — Loot in the rubble

Ruins, Surface & Deep

Ruins are crumbling stone, cobble and brick structures left by people who came before. Some sit out in the open: a few broken walls, a collapsed tower, an overgrown foundation. Others are buried, reached by digging into a hillside or finding an entrance underground. Both reward a careful sweep.

Crack every clay vessel

The best loot in most ruins is the cracked clay vessel, a sealed storage pot. Break it (it won't open like a normal container; an empty hand or any tool works) and it spills tools, the occasional gear, and rare seeds. Some crops are almost impossible to get any other way. Cabbage seeds, for instance, come essentially only from cracked vessels and traders. Smash them all. The loot is randomised per vessel, and each vessel is one of a few types with its own table.

Ruins often hold more than one vessel: one at ground level and another on a former upper floor or in a tower wall. Sweep the whole footprint, including the rubble up high.

Underground ruins add what you can't craft at all: aged planks and furniture, paintings and decorative blocks. Many sit beside bony soil or cobbled skull blocks, your cue you've found one.

The Malefactor's home turf

If you rolled the Malefactor class, ruins are your specialty. Malefactors are stealthier and better at finding and looting these structures. Any class can raid ruins, but a Malefactor gets more from them.

Deeper means richer, and more dangerous

Underground ruins hide the best caches, but they sit where temporal stability is lower and drifters roam. Bring light and a weapon, and don't get cornered. The bestiary and how to fight them is in The Bestiary. The rare seeds from vessels feed straight into your farm. See The Larder.

02 — Crossing the map

Translocators

Sooner or later you'll find a translocator in a ruin: a tall, broken teleporter frame, dark and inert. Many are static translocators, each permanently wired to a partner translocator far away on the map. Repair and power one, step through, and you're carried to wherever its partner stands. This is the game's primary long-distance travel system, and the way you reach far or story-locked regions.

Bringing one back to life

  1. Find one and read it. A broken translocator usually needs temporal gears to repair and energise. Gears are the universal temporal-tech currency, pulled from ruins, traded, or found in the world. The gear system is in The Rust & The Gear.
  2. Power it and step through. Once active, it sends you to its fixed partner. The world sets the destination, not you, so the first trip is a leap into the unknown.
  3. Note where you land. Open the map (M) and mark the exit point the moment you arrive, so you can find this translocator again from the far side.
  4. Build a safe outpost at both ends. A walled, lit base at each translocator turns a one-way gamble into a reliable two-way route, and a refuge if you arrive somewhere hostile.
Think of them as fixed train lines

You don't aim a translocator. Each is a pre-laid line between two stations. Chain them (one ruin's translocator leads to a region with another, and so on) to stitch together a fast-travel network across the world. The story uses these chains to pull you toward the places it wants you to see.

03 — Coins & gears

Wandering Traders

Out on the surface you'll find trader caravans and tents. Each trader specialises, and you deal with them using gears (rusty and temporal) and coins. They carry limited stock that restocks over time, and prices drift with supply and demand. Buy out something rare and the price for the rest climbs.

Best uses of a trader visit

Buy what you can't make: rare seeds (cabbage from an Agriculture trader), good tools, and especially temporal gears for translocators and temporal tech. Sell your surplus (extra ore, food, crafted goods) to fund it. Stock is limited and prices shift, so spread big purchases across several traders and visits.

Traders don't wait forever

A wandering trader you find today may have moved on tomorrow. Spot one with stock you want, note its location on your map (M), and deal while it's there. Or come back once it has restocked.

04 — New in 1.22

The Dynamic Dungeon

Version 1.22 added a new exploration target: a fully dynamic dungeon that now generates in survival worlds. Older ruins are pre-built structures dropped into the terrain. This dungeon is procedurally assembled from many parts into a multi-room layout, so it's different every time and far larger than a single static ruin.

What's new about it

A varied, procedural layout rather than a fixed floor plan. Corridors, rooms and connections are assembled on generation. It stays a fresh, replayable place even once you've cleared every ruin in your region.

What's inside

Its own loot and threats, spread through the rooms. Treat it as a serious dungeon delve, not a quick ruin sweep. Expect to fight your way deeper for the better rewards.

Don't wander in light-handed

Go in with armour, plenty of light (torches and a held lantern), and food for the trip. Place torches as you go so you can find your way out, and watch your stability deep inside. The creatures you'll meet are in The Bestiary.

05 — The thread in the world

Story Locations

Vintage Story has a quiet, authored story woven through its world, tied to the temporal mystery: why reality frays, where the gears and drifters come from, who built the translocators. No cutscenes hand it to you. You uncover it by exploring and paying attention. Here's how to follow the thread, kept spoiler-light.

  • Read your Diary (J). Your character keeps a personal diary that fills in with notes as you progress. It's the main place the story speaks to you directly. Check it whenever something significant happens.
  • Follow the translocators. Translocator chains steer you toward the regions and sites the story cares about. Repairing and stepping through them opens the world's more remote, authored places.
  • Collect clues from special ruins. Certain authored sites (archives, labs and devastated locations the lore hints at) hold story items and context, not just loot. Read what you find. The pieces add up.
Let it unfold at its own pace

There's no penalty for taking the story slowly. Keep building, keep exploring, read the diary, and the larger picture (the gears, the rust, the storms) assembles itself. The mechanics behind the temporal side are in The Rust & The Gear.

★ — Pack the bag

An Explorer's Kit

A ruin two hundred metres away is a quick raid. A translocator hop or a dungeon delve can strand you somewhere unfamiliar with night coming. Before any long expedition, pack like you might not get straight home.

  1. Torches & light. A stack of torches plus a held lantern. Drifters spawn in the dark. In ruins, dungeons and underground, light keeps you alive, and a trail of torches is your way back out.
  2. Food + a bedroll. Enough varied food to keep your nutrition bars up, and a bedroll to set a new respawn point and sleep through dangerous nights far from base.
  3. Armour & a weapon. Wear the best armour you can, and carry a spear or bow for the drifters and predators in deeper ruins and the dynamic dungeon.
  4. A prospecting pick. Sample the ground while you're out. Exploration trips double as ore-scouting, and a prospecting pick tells you what's worth coming back to mine.
  5. Building blocks. A stack of cobble or dirt for emergency walls: seal yourself in for the night, wall off a drifter, or bracket a translocator with a quick outpost.
  6. A few temporal gears. For temporal stability in low-stability depths and for repairing translocators in the field. Don't get stranded one gear short of activating your ride home.
