The Turning World

Grinding grain by hand is fine for Day Three. By the time you are smithing steel you want the world to turn the crank for you. Mechanical power is a chain — a generator catches wind or water, axles and gears carry that rotation across your base, and a machine at the far end does the work. Master four parts and one rule, and a single windmill can grind your flour, sharpen your tools and hammer your steel while you sleep.

00 — The principle

How A Network Works

A mechanical network is a single chain of three roles, and every connected block spins at one shared speed. Get the picture of that chain in your head and everything else is just plumbing.

source

Generators

Turn an outside force into rotation: a windmill catching wind, a waterwheel in flowing water, or your own arm on a hand crank. They supply torque.

carry

Transmission

Axles run rotation in a straight line; angled gears turn it 90° around corners; gearboxes, clutches and the small 4-way hub split it, change ratios or toggle a branch.

work

Consumers

Machines that spend the power doing a job — the quern, grindstone, helve hammer and pulverizer. Each adds resistance.

The whole thing balances like a tug of war. Generators provide torque; every machine downstream adds resistance. If the torque available is greater than the total resistance, the network turns and does work. If the machines' combined resistance exceeds the torque, nothing moves at all — not just the overloaded machine, the entire shared network stalls.

network turns ⟺ torque available ≥ Σ resistance of all engaged machines stalls ⟺ too many / too heavy machines for the wind you have

You connect the parts with axles — wooden first, metal later for longer, stronger runs — and you change direction with angled gears, which take a turning axle and hand the rotation off at 90° around a corner or up a wall.

One speed, shared

Because the network shares a single rotational speed, adding a slow heavy machine slows everything, and switching one off (with a clutch/toggle) speeds the rest back up. This is why on/off branches matter — you rarely want every machine engaged at once.

01 — The source

Generators

A generator is whatever first puts torque into the chain. You have three, in roughly ascending order of effort and output.

Windmill — wind into rotation

The windmill is the workhorse. It is a multiblock: a horizontal axle on a bearing, a rotor mounted on it, and one to several sets of sails — broad cloth blades made from sailcloth / fabric — arranged as a cross on the rotor. Wind pushes the sails, the rotor turns, and that rotation is fed into your axle network.

  • More sail sets = more power. Each set you add increases the torque the windmill can deliver.
  • Higher is windier. Wind strength rises with altitude, so a windmill on a tower or hilltop produces far more than one at sea level. Weather matters too — a storm is worth several calm days.

See schematic (C) below for how the sails sit as a + cross on the rotor.

Waterwheel — flowing water into rotation new in 1.22

New in 1.22, the waterwheel is the steady alternative to wind. It is driven by rapidly flowing water — the fast-flow water blocks introduced alongside it — and it arrived together with the small 4-way hub. Power is taken off at the wheel's centre through that hub, which you then wire into the rest of your network. Where a windmill's output rises and falls with the weather, a wheel in a good flow gives you a dependable, near-constant turn.

The hand crank — zero tech

Before any of that, the humble hand crank on a quern is your generator: you are the engine. It costs no resources and powers exactly one quern, slowly, for as long as you hold the button. Fine for your first sacks of flour — but the moment you are grinding ore in bulk, build a windmill.

Wind vs water, at a glance

Windmill: easy to site anywhere with sky above; output swings with weather and altitude. Waterwheel: needs rapidly flowing water nearby; output is steady and reliable. Many bases run a windmill for headline power and keep a small wheel for a constant baseline.

02 — Carrying the power

Transmission

Transmission is how rotation gets from the generator to the machine. The pieces are simple and they snap together along the grid.

The transmission kit (wooden parts first; metal versions go further and faster)
PartWhat it doesUse it to…
AxleCarries rotation in a straight line along its lengthRun power across, down or along — the spine of every network
Angled gearTransfers rotation through a 90° cornerTurn a corner, climb a wall, or feed a machine sideways
Gearbox / clutchChanges the gear ratio, or toggles a branch on/offTrade speed for torque (or back), and disengage idle machines
Small 4-way hub 1.22Splits one input into multiple outputs at a junctionDrive several machines from one source; take power off a waterwheel's centre

Two practical rules keep a network healthy:

  • Support your axle runs. Long unsupported spans want a block beneath or beside them at intervals; keep runs neat and bridged so they don't float awkwardly through your base.
  • Toggle what you aren't using. A clutch on a branch lets you cut a heavy machine out of the shared network so the rest can spin up to full speed — essential once you have several consumers on one line.

03 — Spending the power

Consumers

These are the machines worth automating. Each one can be hand-driven in a pinch, but mechanical power is what turns them from chores into a standing workshop.

grind

Quern

The first machine you'll ever power. Grinds grain into flour and ores / rock into powders. Hand-crank it early, mechanize it the moment you're milling in volume. Flour feeds straight into the kitchen.

sharpen

Grindstone

Sharpens and repairs tool heads, restoring an edge so a good tool lasts far longer before it's spent. A light load — easy to add to an existing line.

forge

Helve hammer

Automates the anvil's worst grind: hammering blooms, ingots and blister steel flat. Enormous for iron and steel work — see the forge. It is also the heaviest common consumer.

crush

Pulverizer

Crushes ore to boost yield and is a key link in the steel pipeline. Pair it with the helve hammer and you've mechanized the back half of metalworking.

The smithy pair

The two machines that justify a windmill on their own are the helve hammer and the pulverizer: together they pull the grind out of making iron and steel. Put both near your forge and run a single line to them. See the worked example below.

Splitting & smoothing the network

A large wooden gear trades speed ↔ torque (and merges several sources or splits one to many). Spur gears 1.22 split a power train at a flat 1:1 ratio without a big gear. A clutch beside a transmission toggles a branch on/off, so you can leave the helve hammer disengaged until you need it. Pushing speed far past what your torque supports just wastes power to resistance.

Why 1.22 networks behave

The helve hammer used to charge an exponential power cost as its speed rose — push the speed up and the cost ballooned, so a network would surge, overload, stall, recover, and surge again. 1.22 removed that exponential cost, so the hammer's draw is steady. Networks no longer oscillate; once you have enough sails to start it, it simply runs. Build to the table above and you're done.

05 — In practice

Build It

Windmill → axle → angled gear → machine

The classic first power line. Build the generator high, drop the power down, turn it into your workshop. Schematic (A) is this exact run, top-down.

  1. Place the bearing high, in open sky. Mount a windmill bearing / support on a tower or hilltop where the wind is strong and nothing blocks the sails. Higher is always more power.
  2. Attach 1–3 sail sets. Build the rotor on the bearing's horizontal axle and add sail sets — start with what you can afford, plan for ~3 if you'll run a helve hammer (see schematic C for the sail cross).
  3. Run an axle down. Connect the windmill's output through axles down the tower toward your workshop, supporting the run as it goes.
  4. Turn the corner with an angled gear. Where the vertical drop meets your floor, place an angled gear to redirect the rotation 90° into a horizontal axle run.
  5. Feed the machine. Carry that horizontal axle into the back of your quern / helve hammer / pulverizer. It spins; the machine works.

Waterwheel → hub → network

The steady option. Find moving water, drop the wheel in, take the power off the middle. Schematic (B) shows the wheel beside its flow with the central hub.

  1. Find or make rapidly flowing water. You need the fast-flow water blocks — a steep natural stream, or build a channel and let water run rapidly down a drop beside where the wheel will sit.
  2. Place the waterwheel in the flow. Set the wheel so the rapidly flowing water drives its blades. A faster, deeper flow turns it harder.
  3. Take power off the centre via the hub. The wheel outputs through the small 4-way hub at its centre — connect an axle there.
  4. Wire into your network. Run that axle (turning corners with angled gears as needed) to your machines, exactly as with a windmill. The hub can also split to several outputs.

06 — Putting it together

Worked Example — Automate The Smithy

The pay-off build. One windmill, one main line, and the two grindiest jobs in the game run themselves — with a side branch you can switch on only when you need it.

  1. Generator. A windmill on a tower beside the forge, with ~3 sail sets (per the power budget) so it can carry the heavy helve hammer in a medium breeze.
  2. Main line → helve hammer. Axle down the tower, an angled gear at the floor, then a horizontal run straight into the helve hammer by the anvil — this is your always-on steel-and-iron machine.
  3. Toggled branch → quern + pulverizer. Split off the main line with the small 4-way hub and put a clutch/gearbox on the branch. On it, run a quern (for flour) and a pulverizer (for ore yield). Toggle the branch off while the hammer is doing heavy work, on when you need to grind.
windmill (≈3 sails, high) │ axle down ▼ angled gear ──► HELVE HAMMER (main line · always on) │ └─ 4-way hub ──[ clutch ]──► QUERN + PULVERIZER (branch · toggle)

This single network ties three of your guides together: the pulverizer + helve hammer are the back half of the steel pipeline, the quern mills the flour for your kitchen, and the windmill itself is the first big mechanical build after you've found your feet in the early game.

Sequence the load

Because the network shares one speed, don't engage everything at once on a marginal windmill. Keep the helve hammer on the main line and switch the quern/pulverizer branch in only when the hammer is idle. With 1.22's flattened power cost, swapping branches no longer makes the network surge — it just settles to the new speed.
