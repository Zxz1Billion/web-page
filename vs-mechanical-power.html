00 — The principle

How A Network Works

A mechanical network is a single chain of three roles. Every connected block spins at one shared speed. The rest is plumbing.

source

Generators

Turn an outside force into rotation: a windmill in wind, a waterwheel in flowing water, or your arm on a hand crank. They supply torque.

carry

Transmission

Axles run rotation in a straight line; angled gears turn it 90° around corners; gearboxes, clutches and the small 4-way hub split it, change ratios or toggle a branch.

work

Consumers

Machines that spend power on a job: the quern, grindstone, helve hammer, and pulverizer. Each adds resistance.

The network balances like a tug of war on three quantities:

  • Torque — rotational force, measured in kN. Generators supply it. It directly counteracts resistance.
  • Resistance — how much rotational energy the network needs just to keep turning. Every component has some; machines have a lot.
  • Speed — the rate the network turns, and thus how fast machines do their jobs. You only get speed if torque is left over after covering resistance.

If torque exceeds total resistance, the network turns and does work, and the surplus becomes speed. If combined resistance exceeds torque, the whole train loses speed or halts completely — not just the overloaded machine, the entire shared network. Pure transmission (axles, gears) has very low resistance; the wiki notes it takes hundreds of axles to equal the resistance of a single helve hammer. The machines are what cost you.

network turns ⟺ torque available ≥ Σ resistance of all engaged machines spare torque ⟹ higher speed (faster work), up to what the train can bear stalls ⟺ too many / too heavy machines for the wind you have
Don't over-drive the line 1.22

1.22 added a real penalty for forcing too much torque or speed through a train: the over-stressed blocks smoke and then catch fire. Overloading is no longer a free mistake — size the network instead of brute-forcing it.

Connect the parts with axles, wooden first and metal later for longer, stronger runs. Change direction with angled gears, which hand a turning axle's rotation off at 90° around a corner or up a wall.

One speed, shared

The network shares a single rotational speed, so adding a slow heavy machine slows everything, and switching one off (with a clutch/toggle) speeds the rest back up. On/off branches matter; you rarely want every machine engaged at once.

01 — The source

Generators

A generator first puts torque into the chain. You have three, in roughly ascending order of effort and output.

Windmill — wind into rotation

The windmill is the workhorse. It is a multiblock: a horizontal axle on a bearing, a rotor mounted on it, and one to several sets of sails arranged as a cross on the rotor. Sails are broad cloth blades made from sailcloth or fabric. Wind pushes the sails, the rotor turns, and that rotation feeds your axle network.

  • 4 sails = 1 set; 5 sets max. Each set adds 20% output. One set at full wind makes 20 kN, so a maxed five-set rotor reaches about 100 kN at 100% wind, scaling linearly with the live wind percentage.
  • Higher is windier. Wind speed rises +1% per block above ~10 over sea level, capping at +50% around 60 blocks up. A full rotor in full wind at altitude can therefore push toward ~150 kN. Weather adds on top; a storm is worth several calm days.
  • Give the sails room. Each set wants one block of clearance, so a full 5-set rotor needs at least 5 blocks clear of the ground or any solid block. Surrounding walls do not sap wind — a windmill boxed in solid blocks still spins; only altitude and turbulence matter.

Waterwheel — flowing water into rotation new in 1.22

New in 1.22, the waterwheel is the steady alternative to wind. It spins only while its paddles touch one or more rapidly-flowing water blocks — the fast-flow water added in 1.22. The wheel is built from two small 4-way hubs fitted to an axle; take power off at its centre and wire that into your network. A windmill's output rises and falls with the weather; a wheel in a good flow gives a dependable, near-constant turn, and it cares nothing for altitude or storms.

  • Where to find the flow. Rapidly-flowing water generates only rarely, in stream channels at higher altitudes, and only in terrain generated by 1.22 or later. An old world won't have it near old chunks; explore fresh land or build a channel with a steep drop.
  • It's modular. The blade configuration is tunable to the local current, so you can match the wheel to a weak or strong flow.

The hand crank — zero tech

Before any of that, the hand crank on a quern is your generator. You are the engine. It costs no resources and powers one quern, slowly, for as long as you hold the button. Fine for your first sacks of flour. Once you're grinding ore in bulk, build a windmill.

Wind vs water, at a glance

Windmill: sites anywhere with sky above; output swings with weather and altitude. Waterwheel: needs rapidly flowing water nearby; output is steady. Many bases run a windmill for headline power and keep a small wheel for a constant baseline.

02 — Carrying the power

Transmission

Transmission carries rotation from the generator to the machine. The pieces snap together along the grid.

The transmission kit (wooden parts first; metal versions go further and faster)
PartWhat it doesUse it to…
AxleCarries rotation in a straight line along its lengthRun power across, down, or along; the spine of every network
Angled gearTransfers rotation through a 90° corner (horizontal axle ↔ vertical axle)Turn a corner, climb a wall, or feed a machine sideways
Large gearExchanges speed ↔ torque, like changing gears on a bicycle; can also sum the torque of several sources into one outputTrade speed for torque (or back); merge two windmills, or split one source to many
Spur gear 1.22Routes power through a wall (with in-wall axles) at a flat 1:1, no exposed shaftCarry a line into the next room cleanly without a visible axle
Transmission + clutchThe transmission separates two parts of a train; the clutch beside it toggles the link on/offDisengage an idle machine so the rest of the network speeds up
BrakeCompletely stops a power trainHalt everything to work on a machine safely
Small 4-way hub 1.22Splits one input into multiple outputs at a junctionDrive several machines from one source; take power off a waterwheel's centre

Angled gears, in practice

The angled gear is the piece you place most. It hands a turning axle off at a right angle — connecting a horizontal axle to a vertical one and back. A common snag: it refuses to attach to an unsupported axle. The fix is to place a temporary support block under the axle, snap the gear on, then remove the block.

Two rules keep a network healthy:

  • Support your axle runs. Long unsupported spans need a block beneath or beside them at intervals. Keep runs neat and bridged.
  • Toggle what you aren't using. A transmission + clutch on a branch cuts a heavy machine out of the shared network so the rest can spin up to full speed. Essential once you have several consumers on one line.
Merging & ratios with the large gear

To run a heavy machine you sometimes need torque, not speed. A large gear trades one for the other. The classic setup stacks two: a lower large gear sums the torque coming in from several windmills, then an upper gear converts that pooled torque into more rotational speed (at the usual cost). It is also how you split one strong source to feed many machines.

03 — Spending the power

Consumers

These machines are worth automating. Each can be hand-driven in a pinch, but mechanical power turns them from chores into a standing workshop.

grind · 0.1

Quern

The first machine you'll power. Grinds grain into flour and ores and rock into powders. Lowest resistance of the set (default 0.1); a single sail set runs it slowly in a strong breeze. Drive it through the axle on its top or bottom (bottom is the efficient way). Flour feeds straight into the kitchen.

sharpen

Grinding wheel

The mechanical sharpener (distinct from the hand grindstone, which uses no power). Wire it to the network and it sharpens tool heads with a temporary crit-chance bonus. A light load, easy to add to a line.

forge · heavy

Helve hammer

Automates the anvil's worst grind: hammering blooms, ingots, and blister steel flat. Its resistance is dynamic, rising with speed (≈0.1585 at a standstill, higher as it spins). Major for iron and steel work; see the forge. The heaviest common consumer by far.

crush · power-only

Pulverizer

Crushes ore to boost yield and is a key link in the steel pipeline. Cannot be hand-powered — it needs the network. Far cheaper than the helve hammer; one sail set turns it slowly. Pair it with the hammer to mechanize the back half of metalworking.

Grindstone vs grinding wheel

Easy to confuse. The grindstone is a hand block you crank yourself to sharpen tools — no mechanical power. The grinding wheel is the powered version that must sit on a network. Only the wheel belongs on this page.

The smithy pair

The helve hammer and the pulverizer justify a windmill on their own. Together they pull the grind out of making iron and steel. Put both near your forge and run a single line to them. See the worked example below.

Splitting & smoothing the network

A large wooden gear trades speed for torque, and merges several sources or splits one to many. Spur gears 1.22 split a power train at a flat 1:1 ratio without a big gear. A clutch beside a transmission toggles a branch on or off, so you can leave the helve hammer disengaged until you need it. Pushing speed past what your torque supports wastes power to resistance.

Why 1.22 networks behave

The helve hammer used to charge an exponential power cost as its speed rose. Push the speed up and the cost ballooned, so a network would surge, overload, stall, recover, and surge again. 1.22 removed that exponential cost, so the hammer's draw is steady. Networks no longer oscillate. Once you have enough sails to start it, it runs. Build to the table above and you're done.

05 — In practice

Build It

Windmill → axle → angled gear → machine

The classic first power line. Build the generator high, drop the power down, and turn it into your workshop. Schematic (A) is this run, top-down.

  1. Place the bearing high, in open sky. Mount a windmill bearing/support on a tower or hilltop where the wind is strong and nothing blocks the sails. Higher is always more power.
  2. Attach 1–3 sail sets. Build the rotor on the bearing's horizontal axle and add sail sets. Start with what you can afford; plan for ~3 if you'll run a helve hammer (see schematic C for the sail cross).
  3. Run an axle down. Connect the windmill's output through axles down the tower toward your workshop, supporting the run as it goes.
  4. Turn the corner with an angled gear. Where the vertical drop meets your floor, place an angled gear to redirect the rotation 90° into a horizontal axle run.
  5. Feed the machine. Carry that horizontal axle into the back of your quern, helve hammer, or pulverizer. It spins; the machine works.

Waterwheel → hub → network

The steady option. Find moving water, drop the wheel in, and take the power off the middle. Schematic (B) shows the wheel beside its flow with the central hub.

  1. Find or make rapidly flowing water. You need the fast-flow water blocks: a steep natural stream, or a channel where water runs rapidly down a drop beside where the wheel will sit.
  2. Place the waterwheel in the flow. Set the wheel so the rapidly flowing water drives its blades. A faster, deeper flow turns it harder.
  3. Take power off the centre via the hub. The wheel outputs through the small 4-way hub at its centre. Connect an axle there.
  4. Wire into your network. Run that axle to your machines, turning corners with angled gears as needed, exactly as with a windmill. The hub can also split to several outputs.

The sail cross

The sails mount as a + cross on the rotor. Each arm is one sail set, viewed face-on.

06 — Putting it together

Worked Example — Automate The Smithy

The pay-off build. One windmill, one main line, and the two grindiest jobs in the game run themselves, with a side branch you switch on only when needed.

  1. Generator. A windmill on a tower beside the forge, with ~3 sail sets (per the power budget) so it can carry the heavy helve hammer in a medium breeze.
  2. Main line → helve hammer. Axle down the tower, an angled gear at the floor, then a horizontal run straight into the helve hammer by the anvil. This is your always-on steel-and-iron machine.
  3. Toggled branch → quern + pulverizer. Split off the main line with the small 4-way hub and put a clutch/gearbox on the branch. On it, run a quern (for flour) and a pulverizer (for ore yield). Toggle the branch off while the hammer is doing heavy work, on when you need to grind.
windmill (≈3 sails, high) │ axle down ▼ angled gear ──► HELVE HAMMER (main line · always on) │ └─ 4-way hub ──[ clutch ]──► QUERN + PULVERIZER (branch · toggle)

This network ties three guides together. The pulverizer + helve hammer are the back half of the steel pipeline. The quern mills flour for your kitchen. The windmill is the first big mechanical build after you've found your feet in the early game.

Sequence the load

The network shares one speed, so don't engage everything at once on a marginal windmill. Keep the helve hammer on the main line and switch the quern/pulverizer branch in only when the hammer is idle. With 1.22's flattened power cost, swapping branches no longer makes the network surge; it settles to the new speed.

Sizing it by the numbers

Work backwards from the heaviest machine you want running at once. A medium-breeze helve hammer wants roughly three sail sets to start; the pulverizer and quern each want about one. Engaging all three at once therefore needs a comfortable margin, which is exactly why the branch is toggled. Two paths to that margin:

  • Build high. A 3-set rotor at 60 blocks up gets the +50% wind bonus — the same sails that make 60 kN at sea level make ~90 kN up there. Altitude is the cheapest power in the game.
  • Add a second source. Sum two windmills (or a windmill and a waterwheel) through a large gear for more torque on the same line, then trade some back for speed.

07 — When it won't turn

Troubleshooting A Stalled Network

A dead or crawling network almost always comes down to too little torque, a broken connection, or a placement gotcha. Work the list top to bottom.

Symptom → cause → fix
SymptomLikely causeFix
Whole network frozen, machine engagedResistance > torque — too heavy a load for the windToggle off other machines, add sail sets, build higher, or sum a second source with a large gear
Turns, but agonisingly slowlyMarginal torque; little spare to become speedMore wind (altitude), fewer engaged machines, or merge sources for more torque
Windmill barely spins in open airToo low — insufficient wind near the groundRaise it; wind only starts climbing ~10 blocks above sea level
Two nearby windmills underperformTurbulence — they're inside 1.5× sail diameter of each otherSpace them ≥15 blocks apart, or stagger their heights
Angled gear won't attachAxle is unsupported at that spotPlace a temporary support block under the axle, snap the gear on, remove the block
Waterwheel won't turnPaddles aren't touching rapidly-flowing water (or world predates 1.22)Confirm fast-flow blocks reach the blades; build a channel with a drop, or move to 1.22+ terrain
Blocks smoking / on fire 1.22Over-driving the train — too much torque/speed forced throughReduce input or gear it down; don't brute-force past what the line can bear
One branch dead, rest fineA transmission/clutch on that branch is disengaged, or a brake is setToggle the clutch back on; release the brake
First three checks, every time

Before anything else: (1) is the wind actually blowing and is the mill high enough; (2) is every axle, gear and hub physically connected with no gap; (3) is a clutch or brake quietly cutting the line. Most "broken" networks are one of these three, not a torque shortfall.
