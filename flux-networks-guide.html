A working field guide for Flux Networks — the mod that lets you delete the spaghetti of power cables and move Forge Energy wirelessly between where you make it and where you spend it, even across dimensions. Build one named, colour-coded network per base: Plugs drink power from your generators, Points pour it into your machines, a Controller ties it together, and Storage banks the surplus.

00 — Orientation

Start Here

Flux Networks solves one problem and solves it cleanly: getting power from A to B without wires. Instead of running cable from a reactor to every machine, you place a Flux Plug on the energy source and a Flux Point on the consumer, tune both to the same network, and the energy teleports across — anywhere in the world, in any dimension, no cable between them.

A Flux Network is an invisible, named, colour-coded power grid that exists per player. Every connected device is either an input (it adds energy to the network) or an output (it draws energy from the network). The network balances supply against demand each tick. There are no channels, no cable routing, and no line-of-sight requirement — only the network you tuned the block to.

input

Flux Plug

Sits on a generator or battery and pulls FE into the network as surplus to share.

output

Flux Point

Sits on a machine or charger and pushes FE out of the network into it.

core

Flux Controller

One per network. Raises network capacity and unlocks wireless charging.

buffer

Flux Storage

A wireless battery block (three tiers) that acts as the network's central bank.

The one idea to keep

A Flux Network has no physical extent. A Plug in the Nether and a Point in the Overworld on the same network are connected as if they were touching. Energy enters through Plugs, leaves through Points, and the network is just bookkeeping in between — so think in terms of inputs vs outputs, not pipes.

Because it speaks plain Forge Energy (FE/RF), Flux Networks slots in beside whatever else your pack runs. Pair it with the pack's power hub — Mekanism, IC2, Create, or Thermal — and let those mods make the energy while Flux Networks handles the delivery. See the Automation & Power hub for how it fits the wider grid.

The shape of the climb

  1. Make the tools. Craft a Flux Configurator (your in-hand network remote) and the Flux Block base used in most recipes.
  2. Create a network. Open the Configurator, make a new network, give it a name and colour, and set its security.
  3. Place a Plug. Put a Flux Plug against your generator/battery and connect it to the network — power now flows in.
  4. Place a Point. Put a Flux Point against a machine and connect it — power now flows out.
  5. Add a Controller. One Controller raises capacity and turns on wireless inventory charging within range.
  6. Bank the surplus. Drop in a Flux Storage block as the network's battery so spikes and lulls even out.

01 — The hardware

Core Blocks

Flux Networks is a small, focused mod — a handful of blocks and one tool do everything. Learn what each one is for (input, output, control, or storage) and the rest is configuration.

The Flux Networks block roster
BlockDirectionWhat it does
Flux PlugInputPulls FE out of a touching generator, battery, or energy block and feeds it into the network as surplus.
Flux PointOutputDraws FE from the network and pushes it into a touching machine, charger, or storage block.
Flux ControllerCoreOne per network. Raises network capacity, enables wireless inventory charging, and is the network's heart.
Flux Storage (Basic)BufferA wireless battery block — buffers network energy and can act as both an input and an output point.
Flux Storage (Herculean)BufferThe mid tier — much larger internal capacity for a central bank.
Flux Storage (Gargantuan)BufferThe top tier — enormous capacity for soaking reactor-scale output.

The Flux Plug — your inputs

A Flux Plug is placed against an energy source: a generator, an energy cube, an Induction Matrix port, a Create-style adapter, anything that outputs FE. It continuously pulls that energy into the network, making it available to every Point. One Plug per source face; you can plug many sources into the same network and they all pool together.

The Flux Point — your outputs

A Flux Point is placed against an energy consumer: a machine, a charger, a furnace upgrade, a storage block. It pulls energy down out of the network and pushes it into whatever it touches, up to that connection's transfer limit. Most of your placements will be Points — one per machine cluster you want fed.

The Flux Controller — the heart

The Flux Controller is optional but transformative. A network runs fine with just Plugs and Points, but a Controller does two big things: it raises the network's total energy capacity (a network without one holds comparatively little buffer), and it switches on wireless charging so items in your inventory top up while you're within range. You may have only one Controller per network.

The Flux Storage blocks — wireless batteries

The three Flux Storage tiers — Basic → Herculean → Gargantuan — are battery blocks that live on the network. Unlike a normal energy cell, a Flux Storage block doesn't need a cable: connect it to the network and its stored charge is network charge. Use one as the central bank that smooths the gap between when you generate and when you spend.

The Flux Configurator

The Flux Configurator is the handheld tool that ties it all together. Right-click it (or open its GUI) to create, name, colour, and join networks, browse all connected devices, read live throughput, and edit per-connection settings. Sneak-right-click a Flux block to bind or read it. It is effectively a wireless network remote — keep it on your hotbar while you build.

02 — First setup

Creating a Network

A network is created once and then everything tunes to it. The whole flow happens in the Flux Configurator (or the GUI of any Flux block), so make one before you place anything.

Make and name it

  1. Open the Configurator. The network list shows every network you own or can access.
  2. Create a network. Give it a clear name (e.g. "Main Base") so you don't confuse it later.
  3. Pick a colour. The colour is purely cosmetic but it labels the network everywhere — pick a distinct one per network.
  4. Set security. Choose Public, Private, or Encrypted (see below) before others can stumble onto it.
  5. Connect devices. With a Flux block placed, open it and select the network from the list to bind it.

Security modes

Who can connect to and use the network
ModeWho can joinUse it for
PublicAnyone — any player can connect their Flux blocks.Shared community grids; co-op bases where everyone contributes.
PrivateOnly the owner and players on the access list.The default for a personal base — keeps strangers out.
EncryptedOwner / authorised players, with an extra layer guarding the network from access without authorisation.Servers where you want the strongest protection against snooping or cross-feeding.

On Private and Encrypted networks you manage an access list: add specific players and grant them a permission level (use, edit, or full). That lets a teammate connect their own machines to your grid without being able to delete it.

Connecting a device

Every Flux block — Plug, Point, Controller, Storage — has the same network selector in its GUI. Place the block, open it, choose the network, and it joins instantly. To move a device to a different network, just reopen it and pick another. The Configurator can also bind blocks in bulk as you place them.

03 — Flow control

Plugs, Points & Limits

Once power is flowing, the interesting controls are transfer limit and priority — they're how you stop one greedy machine from starving everything else, and how you decide what gets fed first when supply is tight.

Per-connection transfer limit

Every Plug and Point has its own transfer limit in FE/t — the maximum it will move per tick. Set a Point's limit to cap how fast a machine drains the grid; set a Plug's limit to throttle how aggressively it drains a source (handy when the source dislikes sudden full-rate pulls). Leave it high for trunk connections, low for anything you want to keep gentle.

Priority

Each connection also has a priority. When the network can't satisfy everything at once:

  • Higher-priority Plugs are drawn from first — so your cheap/free generators feed in before you start burning fuel.
  • Higher-priority Points are fed first — so critical machines (a reactor's own coolant pumps, a chunk-loader) get power before luxuries.

This turns the network into a simple load-balancer: set priorities once and the grid sorts itself out as conditions change.

How balancing works

Plugs pool their available energy into the network each tick; Points then distribute that pool to consumers, in priority order, each capped by its own transfer limit. If demand exceeds supply, low-priority Points get less (or nothing) that tick. If supply exceeds demand, the surplus flows into any Flux Storage on the network. It's all per-tick, so the network responds immediately as machines turn on and off.

Network total capacity

The network's overall energy capacity (how much it can hold as buffer) and its total throughput grow with what you connect. A bare Plug-and-Point network holds little; adding a Flux Controller sharply raises the capacity, and adding Flux Storage blocks raises it further. If a network feels like it "runs dry" between bursts, it usually wants more buffer — a Controller, a Storage block, or both.

Limit, don't overcommit

If you connect ten machines and one of them can swallow the network whole, the others stall. Set sensible per-Point transfer limits so no single consumer can drain the grid, and use priority to protect the machines that must never stop. Tuning these two numbers is most of running a Flux Network well.

04 — Quality of life

Wireless Charging

The headline convenience, unlocked by the Flux Controller. Once a Controller is on the network, the network can wirelessly charge FE-accepting items in players' inventories while they're within the Controller's range — armour, tools, jetpacks, a MekaSuit, drills, anything that takes FE tops up automatically as you move around.

This removes the chargepad shuffle entirely: walk into your base and your gear refills from the same grid that runs your machines. The Controller decides which players to charge based on the network's security/access settings, so on a private network only authorised players benefit.

Chunk-loading behaviour

Flux Networks keeps the chunks containing its connected blocks loaded, so a network spanning generators in one place and machines in another keeps both ends ticking even when no player is nearby. That makes Flux blocks a convenient chunk-loader as a side effect — but it's also a responsibility: every Plug, Point, and Storage you place is a chunk you're keeping awake.

Loaded chunks cost performance

Chunk-loading is great for remote reactors and far-flung mining outposts, but on a busy server it adds up. Keep your Flux block count reasonable, consolidate where you can, and remember that tearing out a network also releases the chunks it was holding. Some packs gate or configure this behaviour — check your server's rules.

Range is finite

Wireless charging works within range of the Controller, not infinitely. Place the Controller central to where you and your gear spend time — the main base, the crafting room — so you're topped up where it matters. The cross-dimensional magic is for power transfer between blocks; charging you is range-limited.

05 — Banking power

Storage Tiers

A Flux Storage block is a wireless battery: connect it to the network and its charge becomes network charge. It both soaks surplus when generation outpaces demand and releases it back when demand spikes — the buffer that keeps the lights on between bursts.

The three storage tiers (capacity rises steeply per tier — read the tooltip for exact FE)
TierRelative capacityRole
Flux Storage — Basic×1A starter buffer; smooths small networks and early generators.
Flux Storage — Herculean×bigThe workhorse central bank for an established base.
Flux Storage — Gargantuan×hugeReactor-scale soak; holds enough to ride out long lulls.

Using one as the central bank

The clean pattern is a single Flux Storage block as the network's battery. Your Plugs fill it (via the network) when generators are ahead; your Points drain it when machines surge ahead. Because it's wireless, you can tuck it anywhere — it doesn't need to sit next to anything. One good Storage block usually beats scattering many small ones.

Integrating with your power source

Flux Storage is a buffer, not a generator — it only holds what your sources put in. The right setup is a real power plant feeding the network through Plugs:

  • Mekanism Induction Matrix — put a Flux Plug on a Matrix port and the Matrix's huge buffer becomes the network's source; the Flux Storage then handles the last-mile smoothing.
  • Reactors & generators — plug a Fission/Fusion turbine output, a bank of Gas-Burning Generators, or any FE generator straight in.
  • Other-mod power — IC2, Create, Thermal, or Powah generators all output FE-compatible energy that a Plug will happily drink.
Storage + Controller = a tidy grid

A network with one Controller (for capacity + charging) and one Gargantuan Storage (for buffer) behind a real generator is about as clean as base power gets: generate anywhere, store wirelessly, spend anywhere, and walk around charged.

06 — Sharing safely

Multiplayer & Security

Networks are per-player: each network has an owner, and the security mode plus access list decide who else can touch it. On a server this is what stops your power grid from quietly feeding — or being drained by — someone else's base.

Sharing access

On a Private or Encrypted network the owner maintains an access list. Add a player and choose how much they can do — connect their own devices and use power, edit settings, or have full control. This lets a team run one shared grid where everyone plugs in their generators and machines, without anyone being able to nuke the network.

Picking a security mode for multiplayer
SituationModeWhy
Solo basePrivateOnly you connect; nothing to leak, nothing to grief.
Trusted teamPrivate + access listTeammates plug in their own devices; you keep ownership.
Public community gridPublicAnyone may contribute and draw — a shared utility.
Hostile / large serverEncryptedThe strongest guard against unauthorised access.

Avoiding accidental cross-feeding

The classic mistake is two networks bleeding into each other — usually because a block got bound to the wrong network, or a Public network is collecting power you meant to keep. Avoid it by:

  • One colour per network. The Configurator shows each block's network colour, so a wrong binding is visible at a glance.
  • Keeping personal grids Private. Public networks will accept anyone's Plugs and feed anyone's Points — only use Public on purpose.
  • Naming clearly. "Main Base" and "Mob Farm" beat two networks both called "Network".
  • Auditing the device list. The Configurator lists every connected block; if a stranger's Point appears on your grid, you'll see it.
Public means public

A Public network is an open utility: any player can attach a Point and start draining it, or a Plug and start filling it. That's a feature for community grids and a trap for a personal base. If in doubt, leave new networks Private and add trusted players to the access list deliberately.

07 — Putting it together

Patterns & Tips

None of the parts are complicated; the wins come from arranging them well. Here's the layout most bases settle into and the habits that keep it healthy.

The standard base layout

  • 1 network per base
  • Plugs at your generators
  • Points at your machines
  • 1 Controller
  • 1 Storage buffer
  1. One network per base. Resist the urge to make many small networks; one well-named grid is far easier to reason about.
  2. Plug your generators. A Flux Plug on each power source (or on your central battery / Induction Matrix) feeds everything in.
  3. Point your machines. A Flux Point per machine cluster pulls power back out — set a transfer limit so none of them can hog the grid.
  4. Add a Storage block as the buffer. One Herculean or Gargantuan Storage soaks surplus and covers spikes; size it to your biggest burst.
  5. Drop in a Controller. Raises capacity and charges your gear as you walk around — place it central to your base.
  6. Set limits and priorities. Cap Points to avoid spikes, prioritise critical machines and cheap generators, and you're done.

Habits that pay off

  • Throttle to avoid spikes. Per-connection transfer limits keep a single machine from yanking the whole buffer flat the instant it turns on.
  • Prioritise free power. Give solar/wind/passive Plugs higher priority than fuel-burning ones so you spend resources only when you must.
  • Mind your chunk count. Every Flux block keeps its chunk loaded — useful, but consolidate rather than sprinkling Points everywhere.
  • Label everything. Custom names on Plugs and Points make the Configurator's device list readable months later.
  • Keep it Private. Default new networks to Private on servers and add players deliberately.

Pair it with the pack's power

Flux Networks doesn't generate energy — it moves it. Let your tech mod do the generating and let Flux do the delivery:

  • Mekanism — Plug an Induction Matrix or a turbine output; let Flux carry it to machines and charge your MekaSuit.
  • IC2 / Create / Thermal / Powah — any FE-compatible generator feeds a Plug directly; convert at the source if a mod uses its own energy unit.
  • Remote setups — a reactor in a safe dimension, machines at home: one network bridges them with the Plug keeping the reactor chunk loaded.
When in doubt, simplify

Most "my power keeps running out" problems are too little buffer (add a Controller and a bigger Storage) or no limits (one machine eating everything). Two settings — transfer limit and priority — solve the vast majority of grid headaches. Build small, watch the live throughput in the Configurator, and scale up.

★ — The path

Progression — going wireless

Flux Networks isn't a tech tree so much as a delivery upgrade you bolt onto whatever power you already make. The path below takes you from a single hand-built tool to a secured, base-wide grid that charges your gear as you walk — one block at a time, each step earning its keep before you add the next.

  1. Craft the Flux Configurator and open a network. The Configurator is your one tool for everything Flux. Build it, then create a fresh network and name it — this is the channel every block below will tune to. Keep it to a single grid for your whole base.
  2. Plug your power source (input). Place a Flux Plug on the output of a generator — a turbine, a reactor, or the output side of a Mekanism Induction Matrix — and bind it to your network. Energy now flows in wirelessly, no cable required.
  3. Point your machines (output). Drop a Flux Point on each machine or cluster that needs power and tune it to the same network. Power teleports from the Plug to every Point — your factory is running off the grid before you've built anything central.
  4. Add a Flux Storage block as the buffer. A central Storage block soaks up surplus when generation outpaces demand and covers the spikes when a machine fires up. Size it to your biggest burst so the grid never browns out mid-craft.
  5. Drop in a Flux Controller. The Controller unlocks network-wide wireless charging — your tools and armour top up as you move through the base — and raises the grid's overall throughput and capacity. Place it central to where you spend your time.
  6. Tune limits, priorities, and security. Set per-connection transfer limits so no single Point starves the rest, assign priorities so free generators feed first and critical machines drink first, then set the network to Private and add trusted players deliberately for multiplayer.
Where this leads

A finished Flux grid becomes the backbone of your Automation hub: every machine, drone, and processing line draws from one buffered, prioritised pool instead of a tangle of cables. Pair it with a serious power source from the Mekanism guide, and it's a core rung on the climb described in The Ascent — the moment your base stops being a pile of generators and starts being a power network.
