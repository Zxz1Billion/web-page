Warmth & Wear

Two invisible bars decide whether you see spring: how warm your body is, and how well your gear stops a claw. They share the same clothing screen but pull in opposite directions. Fur keeps the cold out, plate keeps the teeth out, and a desert in summer punishes both. This page covers how heat and armor work, and how to dress for the season and the fight.

00 — Orientation

Two Systems, Same Body

Your equipment screen feeds two separate survival systems. Don't confuse them.

heat

Warmth (clothing)

Worn clothing adds warmth, which fights cold damage. Warmth keeps you alive against the climate: winter, night, altitude, rain and wind. Fur and wool warm best.

defense

Protection (armor)

Worn armor in the head/body/legs slots reduces incoming damage. Armor keeps you alive in fights with wolves, bears and drifters. Metal plate protects best.

tension

The trade-off

Heavy metal armor protects but slows you and gives little warmth. Thick furs warm you but aren't armor. Balance both for the season and the threat.

Read the tooltip — always

Hover any clothing or armor piece. The tooltip shows its warmth (in green °C) and its protection stats. The tooltips are ground truth. The numbers below are representative defaults for a Standard world.

01 — The climate is trying to kill you

Body Temperature & Cold

With true winters enabled (the default on Standard), keep your body temperature at about 37°C. Let it fall and you take mounting cold damage. Prolonged exposure can freeze you to death. Unclothed, you start taking cold damage once the surrounding temperature drops below roughly 0°C (default settings).

What pushes your temperature down

  • Season & latitude. Winter is the obvious threat. Colder regions (toward the poles, or at altitude) stay dangerous year-round.
  • Night. Temperatures fall sharply after dark, even in mild seasons.
  • Wetness. Rain and swimming chill you fast. A soaked seraph loses heat much quicker. Wear a hat or take cover in rain.
  • Wind & exposure. Open terrain bleeds heat. Walls and shelter blunt it.

What warms you back up

  • Clothing warmth. Your main portable defense (next section).
  • Heat sources. Stand near a lit fire pit, forge or hearth to raise your temperature. An enclosed, fire-lit room stays livable through a cold night.
  • Being indoors, out of wind and rain. Shelter alone buys you a lot.
You can also over-heat

Don't just stack warmth. In hot biomes or high summer, a full fur kit pushes you into heat discomfort. Keep a light linen set for warm seasons and swap into furs for the cold. Dress for the season.

02 — Dress for the cold

Clothing for Warmth

Each worn clothing piece offsets the effective temperature by its warmth value (shown in green on the tooltip), and the pieces add up. A worn-down garment gives less than a fresh one.

Worked example (from the game's own numbers)

A full set of fur clothes in Good condition might read +3.0, +2.0, +1.5 across pieces, a combined +6.5°C of warmth. That lets you stand outside temperatures as cold as about −6.5°C before cold damage begins. Push your total warmth toward ~10 and your body holds near 37.8°C until the ambient temperature drops below roughly 10°C.

How to dress

  1. Fill every clothing slot. There are many slots (head, face, neck, upper/lower body, arms, hands, legs, feet). Each empty slot is wasted warmth. Even cheap linen on every slot beats a fur coat with bare legs.
  2. Match the season. Wear a fur set for winter and cold biomes, and a light linen set for summer and deserts. Carry both once you can.
  3. Keep a hat on in rain. Staying dry preserves far more warmth than any single garment adds.
  4. Repair before they're ragged. Worn condition cuts warmth. Mend or replace pieces before a long cold spell.

Tailoring clothing uses linen (from flax), wool (from sheep) and fur/leather (from animals). See §05 and The Bestiary for husbandry. The Tailor class gets a head-start, but a Commoner makes everything the same way.

03 — The season that ends runs

Surviving Winter

Winter is Vintage Story's first real test of preparation. Crops stop and die, water freezes, foraging dries up, and the cold turns lethal. Prepare in autumn. You can't react your way through it in the snow.

  1. Fill the larder before the freeze. Harvest and preserve enough food to last the whole season: sealed crocks and a cold cellar, salted meat and dried fish. See The Larder and The Homestead → the cellar.
  2. Make a fur (or wool) set. Hunt for hides, tan leather, and tailor warm clothing for every slot. Aim for a warmth buffer above your coldest expected nights.
  3. Build a heated, sealed shelter. Walls, a roof and a lit fire pit or hearth keep the interior survivable. Hang a door, seal draughts, and use light to keep drifters out.
  4. Stay dry, out of the wind, and close to home. Don't start a long expedition in deep winter. Bring fire-starting and emergency walling if you must travel.
  5. Plan your spring planting. Keep seeds, and consider a greenhouse, so you can replant the moment the ground thaws.
The classic death

Players explore too far on a clear autumn day, get caught by nightfall and the season's first hard freeze with no fur and no shelter, and freeze before reaching home. Always carry a fur layer and the means to wall up and light a fire.

04 — Surviving the fight

The Armor System

Armor is separate from warmth clothing and goes in three dedicated slots: Head, Body and Legs. There are eight armor types: four non-metal and four metal. Within the metal types, the metal tier sets how much they protect.

Non-metal armor

  • Improvised. Scrap protection, better than nothing.
  • Lamellar (wood). Early laced wooden plates.
  • Leather. Light, cheap and flexible.
  • Gambeson. Quilted cloth. Light, a fine base layer, and better than it looks.

Metal armor (by tier)

  • Brigandine. Leather jerkin plus metal plates. Balanced, available from the copper age.
  • Chain. Mail. Flexible and good against cuts.
  • Scale. Overlapping scales. Solid all-round.
  • Plate. The tankiest. Heaviest, and slows you most.

How protection works

Each piece has a protection tier, a flat damage reduction and a percent protection. On a hit, the flat reduction comes off first, then the percentage is taken off what remains. The armor type sets the percentage band (improvised lowest, plate highest). For metal armor, the metal shifts everything up the ladder.

Metal armors (brigandine, chain, scale, plate) take their flat, percent and durability from the metal, climbing copper → bronze → iron → meteoric iron → steel. Iron beats black bronze, and steel is the peak. Plate adds +0.5 flat reduction at every metal tier, and a full steel plate set is listed near 97%. numbers for metal tiers are config-tunable — hover the item in-game for exact values

How the tiers interact

Combat compares the attack's damage tier with your protection tier:

  • Attack tier ≤ your armor tier: for each tier of headroom your armor gains a little, about +0.1 flat and +3% protection per tier. You take even less.
  • Attack out-tiers your armor: protection is shed per tier above, roughly −0.2 flat and −15% protection per tier. This is why a steel weapon punches through copper armor far worse than its listed % suggests.
  • High-tier damage resistance halves the out-tier penalty. Gambeson holds up well, losing only half the normal % per tier.

The three slots

Armor fills head, body and legs. A type's stats are identical in every slot. The slots differ by hit chance: body ≈ 50%, legs ≈ 30%, head ≈ 20%. The body piece does the most work and wears out fastest, so carry spare chest pieces. Head and legs last longer. Movement penalties from each piece stack (a −7% piece ×3 = −21%).

Heavier isn't always better

Plate's weight costs movement and stamina. Against fast packs (wolves, hyenas; see The Bestiary) the mobility of gambeson or chain keeps you alive better than the raw numbers of plate. Match armor to the threat, not just the tier list.

05 — Where the gear comes from

Leather, Linen & Wool

Both systems are built from the same handful of soft materials. Set up a steady supply and you stay warm and armored indefinitely.

flax → linen

Linen

Grow flax (see The Larder), rett and process it into fibre and twine, then weave linen. Linen makes light clothing and the base for gambeson.

sheep

Wool

Shear domesticated sheep for wool, which makes warm clothing. Start a flock via animal husbandry.

hides → leather

Leather

Hunt or butcher animals for hides, then tan them: soak in a barrel with tannin (e.g. from bark) over several days. Leather makes armor (jerkins) and warm wear.

fur

Fur

The warmest clothing material, from the right animals. A fur set is your winter lifeline.

Tanning takes days — start early

Hides cure in a barrel of tannin solution over in-game days. Begin tanning in autumn so your winter armor and fur trim are ready before the first freeze. Metal plates for brigandine, scale and plate come from the forge. See Fire & Metal.

06 — Keep it serviceable

Wear & Upkeep

Clothing, armor and tools all degrade with use. A degraded item gives less warmth, less protection, and less durability before it fails.

  • Clothing & armor condition drops as you take hits and time passes. Warmth and protection scale with condition, so keep your kit in good repair and carry spares for expeditions.
  • Metal tools & armor are maintained at the anvil. Re-working or adding metal restores a worn metal item. A grindstone (hand-cranked or powered, see The Turning World) sharpens and tends edges.
  • Carry backups. A spare linen layer and a backup weapon weigh little and save runs when the main kit fails far from home.
Specifics vary by version & settings

Exact repair mechanics, warmth values and armor numbers are config-tunable and shift between versions (and some packs add whetstones or repair kits). Treat this page as the shape of the systems. Your in-game tooltips and the wiki are authoritative for precise figures.
