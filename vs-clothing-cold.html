Warmth & Wear

Two invisible bars decide whether you see spring: how warm your body is, and how well your gear stops a claw. They share the same clothing screen but pull in opposite directions — fur keeps the cold out, plate keeps the teeth out, and a desert in summer punishes both. Here's how heat and armor actually work, and how to dress for the season and the fight.

00 — Orientation

Two Systems, Same Body

Your equipment screen feeds two completely different survival systems, and confusing them gets people killed in their first winter.

heat

Warmth (clothing)

Worn clothing adds warmth, which fights cold damage. This is about surviving the climate — winter, night, altitude, rain and wind. Fur and wool are king.

defense

Protection (armor)

Worn armor in the head/body/legs slots reduces incoming damage. This is about surviving fights — wolves, bears, drifters. Metal plate is king.

tension

The trade-off

Heavy metal armor protects but slows you and offers little warmth; thick furs warm you but aren't armor. You balance both for the season and the threat.

Read the tooltip — always

Hover any clothing/armor piece and the tooltip shows its warmth (in green °C) and its protection stats. Those tooltips are ground truth; the numbers below are representative defaults for a Standard world.

01 — The climate is trying to kill you

Body Temperature & Cold

With true winters enabled (the default on Standard), your body temperature must stay at about 37°C. Let it fall and you take steadily mounting cold damage — prolonged exposure can freeze you to death. Unclothed, you begin taking cold damage once the surrounding temperature drops below roughly 0°C (default settings).

What pushes your temperature down

  • Season & latitude. Winter is the obvious threat; colder regions (toward the poles, or at altitude) are dangerous year-round.
  • Night. Temperatures fall sharply after dark, even in mild seasons.
  • Wetness. Being rained on or swimming chills you fast — a soaked seraph loses heat much quicker. Wear a hat / take cover in rain.
  • Wind & exposure. Open, exposed terrain bleeds heat; walls and shelter blunt it.

What warms you back up

  • Clothing warmth — the main, portable defence (next section).
  • Heat sources — standing near a lit fire pit, forge or hearth raises your temperature; an enclosed, fire-lit room stays livable through a cold night.
  • Being indoors / out of wind & rain — shelter alone buys you a lot.
You can also over-heat

Warmth isn't a free stat to stack. In hot biomes or high summer, wearing a full fur kit can push you the other way into heat discomfort. Keep a light linen set for warm seasons and swap into furs for the cold — dressing for the season is the whole game.

02 — Dress for the cold

Clothing for Warmth

Each worn clothing piece offsets the effective temperature by its warmth value (shown in green on the tooltip), and the pieces add up. Condition matters: a worn-down garment gives less than a fresh one.

Worked example (from the game's own numbers)

A full set of fur clothes in Good condition might read +3.0, +2.0, +1.5 across pieces — a combined +6.5°C of warmth. That lets you stand outside temperatures as cold as about −6.5°C before cold damage begins. Push your total warmth toward ~10 and your body holds near 37.8°C until the ambient temperature drops below roughly 10°C.

How to dress

  1. Fill every clothing slot. There are many slots (head, face, neck, upper/lower body, arms, hands, legs, feet…); each empty slot is warmth left on the table. Even cheap linen on every slot beats a fur coat with bare legs.
  2. Match the season. A fur set for winter and cold biomes; a light linen set for summer/deserts. Carry both once you can.
  3. Keep a hat on in rain. Staying dry preserves far more warmth than any single garment adds.
  4. Repair before they're ragged. Condition decays warmth; mend or replace pieces before a long cold spell.

Tailoring clothing uses linen (from flax), wool (from sheep) and fur/leather (from animals) — see §05 and The Bestiary for husbandry. The Tailor class gets a head-start here, but a Commoner makes everything the same way.

03 — The season that ends runs

Surviving Winter

Winter is Vintage Story's first true test of preparation. Crops stop and die, water freezes, foraging dries up, and the cold itself becomes lethal. You survive it by having gotten ready in autumn — not by reacting in the snow.

  1. Fill the larder before the freeze. Harvest and preserve enough food to last the whole season — sealed crocks and a cold cellar, salted meat and dried fish. See The Larder and The Homestead → the cellar.
  2. Make a fur (or wool) set. Hunt for hides, tan leather, and tailor warm clothing for every slot. Aim for a comfortable warmth buffer above your coldest expected nights.
  3. Build a heated, sealed shelter. Walls + a roof + a lit fire pit/hearth keeps the interior survivable. A door and no draughts; light also keeps drifters out.
  4. Stay dry, out of the wind, and close to home. Don't start a long expedition in deep winter. Bring fire-starting and emergency walling if you must travel.
  5. Plan your spring planting. Keep seeds (and consider a greenhouse) so you can replant the moment the ground thaws.
The classic death

A new player explores too far on a clear autumn day, gets caught by nightfall and the season's first hard freeze with no fur and no shelter, and freezes before reaching home. Don't be them: carry a fur layer and the means to wall up and light a fire, always.

04 — Surviving the fight

The Armor System

Armor is separate from warmth clothing and goes in three dedicated slots: Head, Body and Legs. There are eight armor types — four non-metal and four metal — and within the metal types the metal tier sets how much they protect.

Non-metal armor

  • Improvised — scrap protection; better than nothing.
  • Lamellar (wood) — early laced wooden plates.
  • Leather — light, cheap, flexible.
  • Gambeson — quilted cloth; surprisingly good, light, and a fine base layer.

Metal armor (by tier)

  • Brigandine — leather jerkin + metal plates; available from the copper age. Balanced.
  • Chain — mail; good vs cuts, flexible.
  • Scale — overlapping scales; solid all-round.
  • Plate — the tankiest; heaviest and slows you most.

How protection works

The model combines flat damage reduction, percent protection, and a tier comparison: a higher-tier metal gives both more flat reduction and a higher percentage, and resists higher-tier weapons better. So armor smithed from a better metal protects more — and protects especially well against the powerful, high-tier attacks of late-game foes.

Heavier isn't always better

Plate's weight carries movement/stamina costs. Against fast packs (wolves, hyenas — see The Bestiary) the mobility of gambeson or chain can keep you alive better than the raw numbers of plate. Match armor to the threat, not just the tier list.

05 — Where the gear comes from

Leather, Linen & Wool

Both systems are built from the same handful of soft materials. Set up a steady supply and you can keep yourself warm and armored indefinitely.

flax → linen

Linen

Grow flax (see The Larder), rett/process it into fibre and twine, then weave linen — light clothing and the base for gambeson.

sheep

Wool

Shear domesticated sheep for wool — warm clothing. Start a flock via animal husbandry.

hides → leather

Leather

Hunt or butcher animals for hides, then tan them: soak in a barrel with tannin (e.g. from bark) over several days. Leather makes armor (jerkins) and warm wear.

fur

Fur

The warmest clothing material, from the right animals. A fur set is your winter lifeline.

Tanning takes days — start early

Leather isn't instant: hides cure in a barrel of tannin solution over in-game days. Begin tanning in autumn so your winter armor and fur trim are ready before the first freeze. Metal plates for brigandine/scale/plate come from the forge — see Fire & Metal.

06 — Keep it serviceable

Wear & Upkeep

Clothing, armor and tools all degrade with use, and a degraded item underperforms — less warmth, less protection, less durability before it fails.

  • Clothing & armor condition drops as you take hits and time passes; warmth and protection scale with condition, so keep your kit in good repair and carry spares for expeditions.
  • Metal tools & armor are maintained at the anvil — re-working/adding metal restores a worn metal item; a grindstone (hand-cranked or powered, see The Turning World) sharpens and tends edges.
  • Carry backups. A spare linen layer and a backup weapon weigh little and have saved many a run when the main kit fails far from home.
Specifics vary by version & settings

Exact repair mechanics, warmth values and armor numbers are config-tunable and shift between versions (and some packs add whetstones/repair kits). Treat this page as the shape of the systems; your in-game tooltips and the wiki are authoritative for precise figures.
