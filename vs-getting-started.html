Vintage Story · v1.22.3 · Standard · Commoner

The First Day

You wake with empty hands in a cold wilderness. There is no crafting table, you can't punch trees into planks, and the night is dangerous. This walks you from spawn to your first sunrise. Knap a knife, make fire, eat something safe, and wall yourself in before dark. Do these eight things and Day Two is yours.

Survival start Standard world Commoner class 🌙 Night is lethal — plan for it

00 — Orientation

What This Game Is

Vintage Story uses a voxel grid but plays like a slow simulation of the Stone Age working toward the Iron Age. If you expect Minecraft, three differences will catch you out on the first day.

slower

No instant tools

You can't punch a tree into planks. Knap stone into a blade, lash it to a stick, then cut and chop. The first tool is a minigame, not a recipe.

hungrier

Nutrition, not just hunger

You have a saturation bar plus separate nutrition for fruit, vegetable, protein, grain and dairy. Eating one food group leaves you frail. Eat a variety (see The Larder).

stranger

Temporal stability

Reality has integrity. Deep underground and during temporal storms, stability falls and monsters called drifters appear. The surface is mostly safe at first.

The golden rule of Day One

Don't dig a deep mine and don't wander far. Today's jobs are a knife, an axe, a fire, some food, and four walls before sunset. Everything else is tomorrow.

A fourth surprise is time itself. The default day is short — under an hour of real time — so the sun moves fast and the to-do list above is genuinely a race. Keep an eye on the sky colour and the clock; when shadows lengthen, stop foraging and start walling. It is far better to seal an ugly dirt box at dusk than to be caught in the open with a half-built mansion.

What you do not need today

No farm, no clay pottery, no metal, no big house, no exploring. Those are Day Two and beyond — the whole metal climb lives in The Long Road. Resist the urge to mine: stone tools can't even break ore veins yet, so there is nothing useful underground for you tonight, only danger.

01 — Who you are

You, the Commoner

At character creation you pick a class. This guide assumes the Commoner, the default and the best first character. In the game's own words, Commoners "are generalists from a multitude of backgrounds. While they have no defining skills or strengths to speak of, they also lack the weaknesses of those who specialize."

That neutrality is why it's recommended. Every other class trades a bonus for a penalty, and a beginner doesn't yet know which trade they want.

All six starting classes (Commoner is the safe default)
ClassGivesCosts
CommonerNo bonuses, no penalties; balanced everythingThe all-rounder (recommended)
HunterCrafts bows & arrows; strong ranged combat & efficient butcheringReduced melee damage
MalefactorBetter foraging & more loot from cracked vessels/ruins; crafts the slingFrail (less health); weak in melee
Clockmaker+damage vs mechanical creatures (locusts); repairs & dismantles machineryFrail; weaker against everything else
Blackguard+melee damage, +armor durability, less armor movement penaltyHungrier; weak ranged & poor at harvesting food
TailorCrafts unique clothing; far more efficient clothing repairIll-suited to wilderness hardship
Commoners can do everything

A Commoner can still craft, hunt, smith and tailor, just with no head-start. Nothing in this guide is class-locked except the bow (Hunter) and sling (Malefactor). A Commoner throws spears and stabs instead, which is plenty for Day One.

The Survival Handbook is searchable and context-aware. Hover almost anything and press H to jump to its page, with recipes and spawn locations. It is the ground truth behind this almanac.

Reading your status bars

Your HUD tells you everything that's about to kill you. Learn to glance at it before it's an emergency.

The blue temporal-stability gear sits above your hotbar. On the surface in daylight it reads near 100% and you can ignore it today. It only becomes a problem when you dig deep or when a storm rolls in — see Surviving the Night and the full system in The Rust & The Gear.

The four ways to make things

Vintage Story has no single workbench. Different items come from different surfaces, and knowing which is which saves a lot of fumbling on Day One:

  • Grid crafting (E) — the 4-slot inventory grid. Torches, the firestarter, tool assembly (head + stick).
  • Knapping — Shift-click loose stone on the ground, chip a voxel pattern. Your first tool heads.
  • Clay forming — Day Two work: shape soft clay into pottery, then fire it.
  • Ground & fire interactions — placing the fire pit, roasting food, lighting torches. Mostly Shift + right-click.

03 — The first hour

Knap Your First Tools

Everything starts with knapping: shape a loose stone into a tool head by chipping away voxels, then lash that head to a stick. Make two tools first, a knife (harvests grass, reeds, berries and butchers animals) and an axe (fells trees for firewood).

Gather the raw materials

  • Loose stones & flint. Right-click the small stones lying on the ground. Flint (often near gravel and water) is the easiest start: it's the only material that lays a knapping surface immediately, and it knaps sharp blades. Grab a handful — plan on roughly one stone per tool head, plus spares for misclicks.
  • Sticks. Pick up loose sticks on the forest floor, or knock them from leaves with a tool later. You need one stick per finished tool as the handle.

Which stone to knap

Flint is the friendly default, but several common stones can be knapped if no flint is around. They differ in the strength (durability) of the resulting tool. By the wiki's ordering:

Knappable materials, strongest first (config-tunable)
MaterialTool strengthNotes
ObsidianBestRare; volcanic areas. Sharpest stone edge if you find it.
FlintVery goodThe recommended start — lays a surface instantly, common near gravel/water.
Other stone (basalt, chert, granite, andesite, peridotite…)LowerWorks in a pinch. With plain stone you must place it as a surface first.

How knapping works

  1. Place a knapping surface. With loose stone selected on your hotbar, hold Shift and click the top of a solid block. The stone lays out as a flat voxel grid.
  2. Chip to the pattern. Left-click the voxels you need to remove. The Survival Handbook shows the target outline for each tool. Chip everything outside the shape until the blade remains.
  3. Collect the head. When the shape is complete it pops off as a tool head (e.g. a Knife Blade or Axe Head).
  4. Assemble at the grid. Press E and combine the head + a stick in the crafting grid to finish the tool.

For the exact target patterns of every knappable tool (knife, axe, hoe, shovel, spear and arrowheads), see The Four Crafts, which shows each one as an interactive grid you can step through.

Knap the knife first, then the axe

A flint knife unlocks grass (for the firestarter and torches), cattails and reeds (for cordage), berries and butchering. A flint axe then turns trees into firewood. With those two you can make fire and food, the rest of Day One.

The starter tool set, in priority order

You can't make everything before dark, so knap in order of what unlocks the next job. The first two are non-negotiable; the rest are nice-to-have on Day One.

What each knapped tool does, and how urgent it is today
ToolWhat it's forDay-One priority
KnifeCuts grass, reeds & cattails; harvests berries; butchers animals for meat & hideFirst — everything depends on it
AxeFells trees; logs break into firewood; chops some plantsSecond — no fire without firewood
SpearStab or throw at hares, chickens & predators (hold + release right-click to throw)Useful — your only ranged option as a Commoner
ShovelDigs dirt, sand, gravel, clay; speeds up shelter wallsOptional today, essential soon

Make spares of the heads

Flint tools are fragile and wear out. If you have stone to spare, knap an extra knife and axe head while you're at it — re-knapping in the dark with a wolf nearby is a bad time. A loose head is cheap to carry; finish it with a stick the moment a tool breaks.

04 — Warmth & light

Fire & Light

Fire cooks food, lights your shelter and keeps you warm as nights get cold. You need three things: dry grass, firewood, and a firestarter.

  1. Cut dry grass with your knife from tall-grass tiles. A few handfuls will do — you'll want some for the firestarter, some for torches.
  2. Make firewood. Chop a tree with your axe. The trunk topples; break the felled logs to get firewood. One tree yields plenty.
  3. Craft a Firestarter (a bow-drill) in the crafting grid from sticks + dry grass. It has a low success chance per attempt, so keep holding right-click; it will catch after several tries.
  4. Build the fire pit. Sneak (Shift) + right-click the ground with dry grass in hand to lay the kindling, then sneak + right-click it with 4 firewood to complete the pit. Aim at the base of the pit with the firestarter and hold right-click to light it.
Torches are nearly free

A torch is a stick + dry grass (or a cattail). Make a few. They are cheap portable light. Drifters spawn in the dark, so a lit base is a safe base.

Fire does three jobs

The fire pit is the most important block you'll place today because it solves three problems at once:

  • Light. A lit pit floods its surroundings with light, and light suppresses drifter and rift spawns. Build inside your shelter and nothing spawns in with you.
  • Warmth. Standing near a fire raises your body temperature, and the warmth lingers for a while after you step away. In a cold biome this is the difference between fine and freezing — below 0 °C the cold drains health and hikes your hunger rate by roughly 30%.
  • Cooking. The pit has slots for raw food. Roasted meat is safe; raw meat can make you ill.

Fuel matters later, not tonight

Plain firewood is fine for cooking and warmth tonight. It is not hot enough to melt metal — that needs charcoal (peat is too cool), which reaches the ~1084 °C copper needs. That's a Copper-Age concern; see The Copper Age. For Day One, firewood is all you need.

The fire pit is also your first cooker. Put raw meat or foraged food in its slots to roast it. Later it becomes the base for a cooking pot and crucible. Tonight, roasted meat is dinner.

05 — Don't starve, don't poison yourself

Food on Day One

You won't farm today. Day-One calories come from foraging and a little hunting. Eat a mix so your nutrition bars don't run dry.

forage

Berries

Right-click a berry bush to harvest without destroying it (left-click breaks it, then replant). Quick fruit nutrition.

forage

Cattail & roots

Cut cattails at the water's edge with your knife for cattail roots (roast them) and tops for cordage. Reliable early veg.

forage

Mushrooms

Plentiful, but some are poisonous. Check the Handbook (H) before eating an unfamiliar one. Cook the safe ones.

hunt

Hares & chickens

Easy prey. Stab with a knife or throw a knapped spear (hold & release right-click), then harvest with the knife for raw meat to roast.

Cook it, and eat the rainbow

Raw meat can make you ill, so roast it on the fire pit. Don't binge a single food group. Your five nutrition bars (fruit/veg/protein/grain/dairy) each add a little max-health, so a varied diet makes you tougher. Full system in The Larder.

Safe vs. risky forage

Berries are the safest calories in the game — none of the wild bush berries will poison you. Mushrooms are the trap. Some are nutritious, some merely useless, and a few will drain your health or kill you, and cooking a poisonous mushroom does not make it safe. The meal still hurts you. The only reliable identifier is the Handbook.

Day-one forage at a glance
FoodSafetyHow to take it
Berries (blueberry, cranberry, currants, raspberry, blackberry, strawberry…)Always safeRight-click the bush to pick without destroying it; it regrows
Cattail rootsSafe (roast first)Knife at the water's edge; cook for veg nutrition

The mushroom rule

If you can't identify a mushroom in the Handbook, don't eat it — not even cooked. On Day One you have berries, roots and meat; there is no reason to gamble on a fungus. There are over a dozen species and the dangerous ones mimic the safe ones closely.

Water & thirst

On Standard settings thirst is light early on, but don't ignore it. Drink at fresh water (rivers, lakes, rain) and later carry water in a clay jug or bowl from the pottery stage. Being soaked also cools you, which matters more than thirst on a cold first night — see the temperature note below. If your world uses a harsher survival preset, keep water handy from the start; defer to your world settings and the Handbook.

06 — Before the sun drops

A First-Night Shelter

You don't need a house tonight. You need walls, a roof, a door and light. The fastest shelter is a small dirt or cobblestone box dug into a hillside, with the fire pit inside for light and warmth. Below is a minimal 5×5 plan. Step through the layers.

  1. Floor & walls. Clear a 5×5 footprint and ring it with dirt or cobblestone two blocks high, leaving a one-block doorway.
  2. Fire inside. Place your lit fire pit in the centre. It lights the interior so nothing spawns in with you, and warms you as the night cools.
  3. Roof it. Cap the top so nothing drops in. A solid roof also keeps rain off your fire.
  4. Close the door. Use a wooden door (planks) or a stacked block in the gap. If you have grass cordage, make a bedroll to sleep safely through the night (it doesn't set respawn — see below).
Dig in, don't build up

On Day One it's faster to carve into a hill than to stack a tower. Three dug walls plus one built wall plus a roof is a complete shelter in a couple of minutes, and the surrounding stone is free.

Pick the fastest shelter you can finish

"Best" shelter is whichever one you can seal before dark. Match the option to how much daylight is left:

Shelter options, fastest first
OptionBuild timeWhen to use it
Hillside dugoutFastestDefault. Carve into a slope; only 1–2 walls to build, rest is free stone
Dirt/cobble box (the 5×5 plan above)FastFlat ground, no hill handy
Pillar-up + capQuick & dirtyEmergency only — jump-place blocks under yourself, roof over. Drifters can't reach you, but you can't do anything either
Sealed cave mouthVariesOnly if shallow; deeper caves lower stability and invite drifters
Don't accidentally trap yourself with stone

A real wall is fine, but don't seal yourself somewhere dark with no fire — drifters can spawn inside an unlit enclosed space. Light first, then seal. One torch or the fire pit inside makes the box safe.

Cordage & a bedroll

If there's daylight to spare, make cordage. Cut cattail tops or papyrus with your knife and craft them in the grid into rope (from cattails/papyrus; flax fibre makes the separate twine). Rope binds countless later recipes, so it's never wasted. Reeds and cattails also weave into a basket and a basic bedroll.

Sleeping vs. setting spawn

A grass bed lets you sleep through most of the night to skip the dangerous hours. Note the nuance: in current versions, sleeping does not set your respawn point — your spawn is bound with a temporal gear (a rare drop/trade item) later. So tonight, the bed is for skipping time safely, not for respawn insurance. Still build it: a slept night is a safe night.

07 — Holding until dawn

Surviving the Night

The dangers after dark are drifters (temporal creatures that spawn in low light) and surface predators like wolves. Your shelter plus a fire handles drifters. Don't go out to meet wolves.

The three night rules

  • Stay lit. Drifters spawn in darkness. A fire pit and a couple of torches keep your interior safe.
  • Stay walled. If you hear a drifter, do not open the door. Wait it out or fight from a doorway you control.
  • Stay shallow. Don't tunnel deep tonight. Stability is high on the surface and drops as you go down. That's a Day-Three problem, not a Day-One one.
You have a grace period

Full temporal storms (when drifters spawn everywhere, even in daylight) don't begin immediately. The first ones are days away. Use that grace to get established. The whole system is explained in The Rust & The Gear.

Know your night-time threats

Two kinds of danger come out after dark. Light handles one; distance handles the other.

What's hunting you at night
ThreatHow it finds youYour answer
DriftersSpawn in darkness & from surface rifts in low lightLight. A lit fire/torch near a rift stops it producing drifters. Stay walled and lit
WolvesAggro within ~20 blocks; hunt in the open; tier-2 damageDon't go out. They can kill an unarmoured Commoner fast. Fight only from a doorway you control
Bears / hyenasAggro ~20 blocks; tier-2 damage; bears hit very hardAvoid entirely on Day One. Run for your shelter and seal it

The first-night routine

When the sky starts to dim, run this checklist in order:

  1. Get inside your walls and put the fire pit / a torch indoors so the interior is lit.
  2. Seal the door — hang a planked door or stack a block in the gap. Don't reopen it for noises.
  3. Eat if your food bar is low, so you regenerate health overnight rather than starve.
  4. Sleep or wait. Use a grass bed to skip the night, or just sit tight by the fire until the sky lightens.
  5. Stay put. Whatever you hear outside, the safest move is to stay lit and walled until dawn.

Survive to morning and you've cleared the hardest part of the opening. Day Two is about pottery and clay (a bowl, a cooking pot, a crucible) and scouting copper, which is where The Long Road picks up.
