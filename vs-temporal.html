Vintage Story · v1.22.3 · Standard · Temporal mechanics

The Rust & The Gear

Vintage Story makes reality itself dangerous. Temporal stability is the integrity of the world at your exact location: high on the open surface, low in the deep, and torn open by rifts and temporal storms. Let it fall and the screen warps, monsters pour in, and the rust world reaches through. This page covers how stability works, what drains it, and how you fight back.

00 — The mechanic

What It Is

Temporal stability is a percentage measuring how firmly reality holds together where you stand. It runs on a 0–150% scale where 100% is neutral. Stable surface areas sit at or above 100% (up to 150%), refilling you fast. The moment it starts to drop, a spinning stability gauge (a blue gear icon above the hotbar) appears and the world begins to misbehave.

Most of the world's surface is stable. Out in daylight, far from a rift, you can live for days without the bar ever appearing. Stability falls in three situations: when you go deep underground (the deeper, the worse), when you stand near a temporal rift, and during a temporal storm. As the number drops, the symptoms escalate.

What low stability does to you — and the thresholds where each kicks in
StabilityWhat you see & feel
100%+ (surface)No gauge, no effects. Areas read up to 150%, so surface time refills you.
Below ~50%The gear gauge shows and spins (counter-clockwise means losing). Screen warping and colour shifts creep in and worsen as it falls.
≤ 25%Bowtorn force-spawn around you regardless of light level. Distortion is heavy — leave now.
≤ 12%You take unavoidable damage every 4s, starting ~0.02 HP and rising toward ~0.15 HP per tick near 0%.
0%You're pulled into the Rust World and take continuous damage until your stability recovers.
Watch the gauge, not the clock

The stability gauge is your key readout underground and during storms. A perfectly stable spot refills it (~1% every 4s, i.e. ~+0.25%/s — that's the natural cap). A fully unstable one drains it instead. If it's on screen and falling, reality is thin where you stand. Move back toward the surface, away from rifts, or toward your lit base before it bottoms out.

Area stability vs your stability

Two numbers are in play. Area stability is the 0–150% reading of the place you're standing. Your stability is the personal bar that fills toward, or drains toward, the area value:

  • Area at or above 100% → your bar regenerates (capped at ~+0.25%/s in a fully stable spot).
  • Area below 100% → your bar drains toward that floor. The lower the area, the faster and deeper the drain.
  • Near a rift → all low-stability effects are forced on you regardless of your actual bar (see §02).

The whole mechanic can be switched off with /worldconfig temporalStability false (default: on). defer exact values to your world settings and the in-game Handbook (H)

Stability by depth

There is no fixed "X% at Y blocks" curve — area stability is driven by world generation, not a clean depth formula. The reliable shape:

Where stability sits (general tendency, not a fixed table)

01 — The three drains

What Drains Stability

Three things pull your stability down. Learn to recognize each one. They have very different rates and fixes.

slow · positional

Going deep

Ambient stability falls the deeper you mine. Near the surface it's fine. Far down it sits permanently low, so a long mining trip slowly bleeds your bar. The fix: go up.

fast · positional

Standing near a rift

A temporal rift drains stability rapidly the closer you get. Linger beside one and your bar empties in moments. Don't camp next to a rift. Back away and it recovers.

timed · world event

Being out in a storm

During a temporal storm, ambient stability drops world-wide for the duration. The storm is telegraphed in advance, so you can plan around this drain.

They stack

The worst case is all three at once: mining deep when a storm hits, near an underground rift. That's how stability bottoms out fast and how unprepared players die. Stay out of that overlap.

02 — Tears in the world

Rifts

Temporal rifts are shimmering tears, "rust world" phenomena that puncture the boundary between your world and the one rusting through it. They appear out in the world (more readily at night and where stability is already low) and do two things at once.

  • They spawn hostiles on the surface. A rift acts as a doorway. Drifters climb out near it, along with tougher rift creatures (bowtorn and shivers) that are far meaner than an ordinary night-time drifter. The surface around a rift is dangerous, day or night.
  • They drain stability fast. Stand too close and the gauge plummets, roughly 3% per second. The drain is steep and positional. Step away and it stops.

How rifts form & cycle

Rifts are generated randomly on the surface near players. How many exist at once tracks the world's Rift Activity level, which itself shifts at random every few hours between Low, Medium, High, Very High and Apocalyptic. New worlds are eased in:

Rift Activity ramp on a fresh world
World ageHighest activity possible
First 3 daysLow only
Until day 20up to High
From day 20Very High unlocks
From day 30Apocalyptic unlocks

Rifts wax and wane on a cycle. They flare up, fade, and reappear elsewhere rather than sitting in one spot forever. Higher activity means more rifts open at once.

The Rift Activity world setting

One config command, /worldconfig temporalRifts, controls how rifts behave, with three modes:

temporalRifts modes
ModeVisuals / soundStability drainSpawns
Visible (default)Seen & heardYes, drains nearbyDrifters spawn near rifts
InvisibleNoneStill drains — you just can't see the riftDrifters still spawn

Invisible is the trap: rifts still bleed your stability with no warning. Off trades away the entire rift threat and most surface drifters with it.

Never build next to a rift

A rift beside your base means a steady drip of surface drifters and a permanent stability sink right where you sleep. If one opens near home, keep your distance and wait for the cycle to move it on. Don't try to live alongside it.

You can't seal a rift (in vanilla)

There is no vanilla tool to close a rift. They dissipate on their own as Rift Activity cycles down — the only "fix" is patience and distance. Common misconceptions worth clearing up:

  • The tuning spear does not seal rifts. It's a Clockmaker-only tool (bronze spear + temporal gear + metal parts) whose job is to hack a bronze or corrupt locust with one hit and turn it into a pet that fights for you. Useful, but unrelated to rifts.
  • Rift-warding blocks are mod-only. Items that suppress rifts in an area (e.g. a "Rift Ward") come from mods, not the base game.
  • The base-game answer is the world setting. If rifts ruin a save, change /worldconfig temporalRifts or lower rift activity — there's no in-world device.

For the creatures that come out of rifts (drifter tiers, bowtorn, shivers, locusts and the rest) see the full bestiary in The Bestiary.

03 — The world event

Temporal Storms

A temporal storm is a periodic, world-wide event that arrives roughly every ~10–20 in-game days. For its duration, reality frays everywhere and drifters spawn all around you regardless of light level, even in broad daylight. It is the most dangerous recurring event in the game.

Storms are telegraphed well in advance with two messages: a "temporal storm approaching" warning about 8½ hours (in-game) ahead, then a final "imminent" warning roughly 29 minutes before it lands. As it nears, the sky darkens and an unmistakable audio cue sets in. You won't be caught out.

Each storm comes at an intensity of Light, Medium or Heavy. Intensity changes how far stability falls and how strong the distortion is — it does not change duration:

Storm intensity (stable areas drop to roughly these levels)
IntensityStable area drops to ≈Roughly how often

A Heavy storm dropping even the surface to ~5% means the unavoidable-damage and forced-spawn thresholds (§00) hit you out in the open — that's why a Heavy storm is a genuine siege. Two more things scale with your save:

  • There is a grace period. A fresh world won't throw a storm at you immediately. The first one is days away, and the first storm is never Heavy, giving new players room to get established.
  • Storms escalate. Each storm makes the next ~10% more frequent (capping at +100%, so the cadence tightens toward ~5–15 days), and average duration and intensity climb over time. Early storms are survivable in a hut. Late-game storms are a serious siege.

The storm routine

  1. Heed the warning. When the "temporal storm approaching" message appears, stop whatever long project you're on. You have a known window before it hits.
  2. Get out of the deep. The worst place to be when a storm lands is deep underground. Surface and head home before the sky darkens.
  3. Stockpile & seal up. Top off food, arrows and torches. Make sure your base is fully lit and walled, with the door shut and every gap closed.
  4. Ride it out. Stay inside, lit and walled. Fight drifters from a chokepoint you control. Never wander out into the open during the storm.
  5. Wait for the all-clear. The storm ends with its own message and the sky brightening. Only then go back out and harvest any drifter drops (gears) once it's safe.
Storms are also harvest time

A storm dumps drifters on top of you, and drifters drop temporal gears. A well-defended base turns the scariest event in the game into your best source of gears. Fight from a doorway and you come out richer.

04 — The core enemy

Drifters & the Deep

Drifters are the game's signature hostile: shambling, temporally-corrupted humanoids that spawn in darkness, near rifts, and everywhere during storms. They're manageable one at a time on the surface. The deep changes the maths entirely.

Drifters come in escalating variants, and the stronger ones live deeper. The further down you mine, the tougher the drifters you meet and the harder they hit. Surface drifters are a nuisance. Their deep cousins can wreck an under-geared miner in seconds.

Killing them heals reality

Killing supernatural enemies (drifters and their kin) restores some of your stability, and stronger enemies restore more. Clearing the things attacking you shores the world back up, so a good fight is also a refill.

05 — The emergency button

Temporal Gears

A temporal gear is a dense little clockwork artefact, and a key item in the temporal system. Get them two ways: as a drop from drifters (especially during storms) and from traders. Hoard them. They have two jobs.

Use 1 — instant stability

This is the emergency refill. Hold a temporal gear in your offhand, then use a knife in your main hand on it. The gear is consumed and your stability jumps back up by about 30%, instantly, anywhere, even at the bottom of a mine in a storm. It isn't free.

Consuming a gear deals about 2 HP of self-damage on top of spending the gear. Use it when the bar is about to bottom out and you can't reach the surface in time, not every time it dips.

Use 2 — powering translocators & teleporters

Temporal gears are also a crafting and repair ingredient. They power and repair translocators (the ancient teleport gates you find in ruins) and teleporters, and they appear in various high-tier recipes. This is the long-game reason to bank gears rather than burning them all on stability.

For where translocators and traders live, and how to fix a broken translocator with gears, see The Map & the Ruins.

07 — Hold the line

Storm-Proofing Your Base

Because storms are telegraphed, a properly built base turns them from a crisis into a chore. Aim for a sealed, fully-lit, walled refuge with chokepoints you control, somewhere you can stand and pick off drifters while the bar holds steady.

  1. Seal it. No gaps, no open roof, no dark corners. Drifters spawn in darkness and squeeze through holes. A storm-proof base is light-tight and air-tight.
  2. Light it fully. Wall-to-wall light so nothing spawns inside with you. Torches, lanterns or a fire pit, whatever keeps every tile bright.
  3. Build a chokepoint. One controlled doorway you can fight from, so you face drifters one at a time instead of being surrounded.
  4. Stockpile before the storm. Lay in food, arrows and spare torches before the sky darkens. You don't want to craft mid-siege.
  5. Never be deep when it hits. Surface and get home on the warning. Getting caught deep underground during a storm is a common way to lose a character.
A doorway is a weapon

Fight drifters from a chokepoint you built: a one-wide doorway, a fence-gap, a raised ledge. Funnelling them lets a modest weapon clear a whole storm's worth of drifters (and collect their gears) with little risk.

08 — The mystery (spoiler-light)

The Lore

Temporal stability is also the thread of the whole story. The warping, the gears, the drifters and the deep all point at the same thing: a "rust world" bleeding into yours, and a long-vanished civilisation that learned to move through time and space and then fell silent.

You uncover that story slowly and on your own terms. Your Diary (J) records story notes as you find them. Ruins scatter the clues across the map. The translocators you repair with temporal gears are the literal remnants of whoever came before. The answer is out there to dig up.

Follow the breadcrumbs, not a wiki

Part of the appeal is piecing the rust world together yourself, so this page won't spoil it. When you're ready to go looking, The Map & the Ruins covers ruins, traders and translocators, the places the story actually lives.

The practical takeaway is the one that keeps you alive: watch the bar, stay out of the overlap, bank your gears, and seal your base before the sky goes dark.
