The Rust & The Gear

Every other survival game makes the dark dangerous. Vintage Story makes reality itself dangerous. Temporal stability is the integrity of the world at your exact location — high on the open surface, low in the deep, and torn open by rifts and temporal storms. Let it fall and the screen warps, monsters pour in, and the rust world reaches through. This is how stability works, what eats it, and how you fight back.

00 — The mechanic

What It Is

Temporal stability is a hidden percentage measuring how firmly reality holds together where you are standing. When it's full you'll never see it; the moment it starts to drop, a stability bar appears on your HUD and the world begins to misbehave.

Most of the world's surface is stable — out in daylight, far from a rift, you can live for days without the bar ever appearing. Stability falls in three situations: when you go deep underground (the deeper, the worse), when you stand near a temporal rift, and during a temporal storm. As the number drops, the symptoms escalate.

What low stability does to you (symptoms worsen as the number falls)
StabilityWhat you see & feel
High (surface)No bar, no effects. Reality is solid — business as usual.
DroppingThe stability bar appears. Subtle screen warping and colour shifts creep in at the edges.

Watch the bar, not the clock

The stability bar is your single most important readout underground and during storms. If it's on screen and falling, you're somewhere reality is thin — move back toward the surface, away from rifts, or toward your lit base before it bottoms out.

01 — The three drains

What Drains Stability

There are exactly three things that pull your stability down. Learn to recognise each one — they have very different rates and very different fixes.

slow · positional

Going deep

Ambient stability falls the deeper you mine. Near the surface it's fine; far down it sits permanently low, so a long mining trip slowly bleeds your bar. The fix is simple — go up.

fast · positional

Standing near a rift

A temporal rift drains stability rapidly the closer you get. Linger beside one and your bar empties in moments. Don't camp next to a rift; back away and it recovers.

timed · world event

Being out in a storm

During a temporal storm ambient stability drops world-wide for the duration. The storm is telegraphed in advance, so this drain is the one you can plan around.

They stack

The worst case is all three at once: mining deep when a storm hits, near an underground rift. That's how stability bottoms out fast and how unprepared players die. The whole game of survival is staying out of that overlap.

02 — Tears in the world

Rifts

Temporal rifts are shimmering tears — "rust world" phenomena that puncture the boundary between your world and the one that's rusting through it. They appear out in the world (more readily at night and where stability is already low) and they do two nasty things at once.

  • They spawn hostiles on the surface. A rift acts as a doorway: drifters climb out near it, along with tougher rift creatures — bowtorn and shivers — that are far meaner than an ordinary night-time drifter. The surface around a rift is genuinely dangerous, day or night.
  • They drain stability fast. Stand too close and your stability bar plummets. The drain is steep and positional, so the cure is distance — step away and it stops.

Rifts wax and wane on a cycle: they flare up, fade, and reappear elsewhere rather than sitting in one spot forever. Rift activity rises and falls over time and can be checked — so you can read when the world is "loud" with rifts versus quiet — but the practical rule never changes.

Never build next to a rift

A rift beside your base means a steady drip of surface drifters and a permanent stability sink right where you sleep. If one opens near home, keep your distance and wait for the cycle to move it on — don't try to live alongside it.

For the creatures that come out of rifts — drifter tiers, bowtorn, shivers, locusts and the rest — see the full bestiary in The Bestiary.

03 — The world event

Temporal Storms

A temporal storm is a periodic, world-wide event that arrives roughly every ~10–20 in-game days. For its duration, reality frays everywhere and drifters spawn all around you regardless of light level — even in broad daylight. It is the single most dangerous recurring event in the game.

The crucial mercy is that storms are telegraphed well in advance. You get a warning message some time before it begins, and as it approaches the sky darkens and an unmistakable audio cue sets in. There's no excuse for being caught out — the game is explicitly giving you time to prepare.

Two more things scale with your save:

  • There is a grace period. A fresh world won't throw a storm at you immediately — the first temporal storm is days away, giving new players time to get established before the sky ever darkens.
  • Storms escalate. The further into the game you are, the more intense and longer storms become. The early ones are survivable in a hut; late-game storms are a serious siege.

The storm routine

  1. Heed the warning. When the "temporal storm approaching" message appears, stop whatever long project you're on. You have a known window before it hits.
  2. Get out of the deep. The worst place to be when a storm lands is deep underground. Surface and head home before the sky darkens.
  3. Stockpile & seal up. Top off food, arrows and torches; make sure your base is fully lit and walled, with the door shut and every gap closed.
  4. Ride it out. Stay inside, lit and walled. Fight drifters from a chokepoint you control — never wander out into the open during the storm.
  5. Wait for the all-clear. The storm ends with its own message and the sky brightening. Only then go back out; harvest any drifter drops (gears!) once it's safe.
Storms are also harvest time

A storm dumps drifters on top of you — and drifters drop temporal gears. A well-defended base turns the scariest event in the game into your best source of gears. Fight smart from a doorway and you come out richer.

04 — The core enemy

Drifters & the Deep

Drifters are the game's signature hostile — shambling, temporally-corrupted humanoids that spawn in darkness, near rifts, and everywhere during storms. They're manageable one at a time on the surface, but the deep changes the maths entirely.

Drifters come in escalating variants, and the stronger ones live deeper. The further down you mine, the tougher the drifters you meet and the harder they hit — surface drifters are a nuisance, but their deep cousins can wreck an under-geared miner in seconds.

05 — The emergency button

Temporal Gears

A temporal gear is the most important single item in the temporal system — a dense little clockwork artefact that you obtain two ways: as a drop from drifters (especially during storms) and from traders. Hoard them. They have two jobs.

Use 1 — instant stability

This is the in-a-pinch refill. Hold a temporal gear in your offhand, then use a knife in your main hand on it: the gear is consumed and your stability jumps back up by about 30%, instantly, anywhere — even at the bottom of a mine in a storm. It isn't free, though.

Consuming a gear deals about 2 HP of self-damage on top of spending the gear. It's an emergency lever, not a routine top-up: use it when the bar is about to bottom out and you can't reach the surface in time, not every time it dips.

Use 2 — powering translocators & teleporters

Temporal gears are also a crafting and repair ingredient. They power and repair translocators (the ancient teleport gates you find in ruins) and teleporters, and they appear in various high-tier recipes. This is the long-game reason to bank gears rather than burning them all on stability.

For where translocators and traders live — and how to fix a broken translocator with gears — see The Map & the Ruins.

07 — Hold the line

Storm-Proofing Your Base

Because storms are telegraphed, a properly built base turns them from a crisis into a chore. The goal is a sealed, fully-lit, walled refuge with chokepoints you control — somewhere you can stand and pick off drifters while the bar holds steady.

  1. Seal it. No gaps, no open roof, no dark corners — drifters spawn in darkness and squeeze through holes. A storm-proof base is light-tight and air-tight.
  2. Light it fully. Wall-to-wall light so nothing spawns inside with you. Torches, lanterns, a fire pit — whatever keeps every tile bright.
  3. Build a chokepoint. One controlled doorway you can fight from, so you face drifters one at a time instead of being surrounded.
  4. Stockpile before the storm. Food, arrows and spare torches laid in before the sky darkens — you don't want to be crafting mid-siege.
  5. Never be deep when it hits. Surface and get home on the warning. Getting caught deep underground during a storm is the classic way to lose a character.
A doorway is a weapon

Fight drifters from a chokepoint you built — a one-wide doorway, a fence-gap, a raised ledge. Funnelling them lets a modest weapon clear a whole storm's worth of drifters (and collect their gears) with little risk.

For walls, doors, lighting and defensible layouts in detail, see Walls & Workshops.

08 — The mystery (spoiler-light)

The Lore

Temporal stability isn't just a survival meter — it's the thread of the whole story. The warping, the gears, the drifters and the deep all point at the same thing: a "rust world" bleeding into yours, and a long-vanished civilisation that learned to move through time and space and then fell silent.

You uncover that story slowly and on your own terms. Your Diary (J) records story notes as you find them; ruins scatter the clues across the map; and the translocators you repair with temporal gears are the literal remnants of whoever came before. Stability is the game asking you a question — the answer is out there to dig up.

Follow the breadcrumbs, not a wiki

Part of the appeal is piecing the rust world together yourself, so we'll leave it teasing rather than spoiling. When you're ready to go looking, The Map & the Ruins covers ruins, traders and translocators — the places the story actually lives.

For now, the practical takeaway is the one that keeps you alive: watch the bar, stay out of the overlap, bank your gears, and seal your base before the sky goes dark.
